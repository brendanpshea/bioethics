Lecture 5 — Autonomy, Paternalism, and Reproduction

Part A: Paternalism and Its Limits

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

A Demand You’d Override

A Demand in the Delivery Room

A woman in the second hour of an unmedicated labor is in extreme pain. She has had a dose of IV opioid that leaves her drowsy and slurring. Between contractions she grabs the rail, says she is “done with all of this,” pulls at the fetal monitor, and demands to sign herself out and go home. Her partner is frightened. The nurse believes she would not say this an hour from now.

Write or discuss:

  1. Should the team take her demand at face value and start discharge paperwork? If not, why not?
  2. What exactly is it about this refusal that makes overriding it feel safe — when overriding a calm patient’s refusal would not?
  3. How long may the team “wait her out” before stalling becomes a way of simply ignoring her?

Don’t formalize yet. The point is that almost everyone overrides here without hesitation — and the lecture is about why that instinct is sometimes right and sometimes a trap.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

  • Almost no one thinks we must take that demand at face value. We override people “for their own good” all the time — small children, a friend’s car keys, an unconscious patient in the ED.
  • So the interesting question is never whether such overriding is ever justified. It is where it stops — and what separates the override we’d all accept from the kind that becomes tyranny.
  • Today builds one tool, Mill’s framework for justified paternalism, and then stress-tests it on the two hardest places medicine actually uses it: drug policy and pregnancy.

Today

By the end of Part A, you will be able to:

  • Define paternalism and state Mill’s harm principle and its exceptions.
  • Distinguish soft from hard paternalism by whether the overridden choice is compromised or competent.
  • Build the justification for a hard paternalism we accept — prescription-only opioids.
  • Explain why drug policy is hard, drawing on both unrestricted opiates and prohibition.
  • Place policies aimed at pregnant women on a soft-to-hard spectrum, and state what Ferguson v. City of Charleston held.
  • Explain why partial autonomy — adolescents especially — is the hardest case.

A Puzzle to Hold

The US is among the most punitive peer countries on drugs, and has the worst outcomes.

  • It imprisons people at ~541 per 100,000 — many times the rate of other wealthy democracies — and has the highest overdose death rate of any rich nation (~30 per 100,000).
  • Its infant and maternal mortality are also worse than its peers’.
  • Yet on tobacco — taxed and age-limited rather than banned — its results are among the best.

Note

Stringency alone does not explain this. Singapore and Japan are harsher than the US on drugs and have far fewer overdose deaths. The framework below is built to address the puzzle. (Tikkanen et al. 2022)

The Millian Framework

Paternalism, Defined

Paternalism is interfering with a person’s liberty, without their consent, and doing so only because you judge it to be for their own good. The metaphor is exact: a parent overriding a child for the child’s sake. (Dworkin 2020)

That third clause — only because it is for their good — is what makes paternalism distinctive. A seatbelt law that protected only the wearer is paternalism. A law against drunk driving is not: it protects other people on the road. Keep the two apart, because Mill treats them completely differently.

Why the “only because” matters. Many real laws have mixed motives. A cigarette tax protects the smoker (paternalism) and raises revenue and reduces secondhand smoke (not paternalism). When we ask whether a policy is “justified paternalism,” we are asking about the part of its justification that appeals to the chooser’s own good.

Mill’s Harm Principle

“The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant.… Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign.”

— John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859), ch. 1

(Mill 1863)

Read strictly, this is a near-total ban on paternalism: the state may stop you from harming others, never from harming only yourself. The cases that follow test where this line holds.

Mill’s Own Exceptions

The harm principle is not the all-purpose shield it is sometimes treated as. In the same book, Mill carved out who it does not cover.

Mill himself excluded:

  • Children — “those who are still in a state to require being taken care of by others.”
  • People who cannot be reached by “free and equal discussion.”
  • His famous bridge: if a man is about to cross a bridge you know is broken and you cannot warn him in time, you may stop him — to make sure he knows.

What this tells us:

The case for leaving people alone rests on three assumptions — that the person is competent, choosing freely, and well-informed. Where those hold, Mill says hands off. Where they fail, even Mill lets us step in. The whole soft/hard distinction grows out of this.

Soft vs. Hard — The Real Dividing Line

The line is not how forceful the intervention is. It is what kind of choice you are overriding.

Soft paternalism Hard paternalism
Overrides a choice that is… compromised — coerced, ignorant, incapacitated, or impaired competent, informed, and voluntary
What you’re really doing restoring or protecting the person’s own agency substituting your judgment for theirs
Mill’s verdict permitted — it isn’t a real override of a sovereign choice forbidden — this is the line he draws
The bridge stopping the man until he knows the bridge is out stopping him after he knows, because you think living is better

Whether a given choice is compromised enough to count as soft is the judgment the hard cases turn on. (Feinberg 1986)

Is It Soft? Three Worked Calls

Clearly soft:

  • The sedated, pain-flooded laboring patient from the opening. Capacity is transiently compromised; you wait it out to give the real chooser back. You are restoring agency, not replacing it.
  • Age limits on alcohol, tobacco, and tattoos. The claim is that a 16-year-old’s capacity to weigh long-run risk is not yet whole — Mill’s “children” exception, drawn at a rough line.

Arguably soft — and contested:

  • Cigarette taxes. A standard hard-paternalism reading says: a competent adult chose to smoke, leave him be. But addiction degrades the voluntariness of each cigarette. The smoker, asked when sober, usually wishes he smoked less. A tax pushes against the addicted choice and toward the reflective one — which looks more like soft paternalism in disguise.

The Three-Question Test

Before overriding anyone “for their own good,” ask:

  1. Is the person competent — able to understand and weigh the facts right now?
  2. Is the choice voluntary — free from coercion, manipulation, and (in the hard sense) from addiction or false information?
  3. Does the harm fall on the chooser alone, or also on others?

If the honest answers are yes, yes, and chooser alone, Mill says: leave the person be. A “no” on question 1 or 2 opens the door to soft paternalism. A “no” on question 3 takes the case out of paternalism entirely. The genuinely hard cases are the ones where every answer is yes and we still want to step in.

Hard Paternalism, Defended

A Hard Case We Already Accept

Here is a piece of hard paternalism almost everyone lives with comfortably: you cannot buy morphine, oxycodone, or fentanyl without a prescription.

A competent, fully-informed adult who has read every warning still may not walk into a pharmacy and buy opioids at will. That is not a “harm to others” rule and not a soft-paternalism case — the buyer is, by stipulation, competent and informed. It is the state substituting its judgment for a sovereign adult’s. By Mill’s strict line, it should be forbidden. Yet we keep it, in nearly every country on earth. Why?

Why Prescription Gating Survives Mill

The case for the gate:

  • Addiction changes the chooser. The first dose is a competent choice; opioid dependence then degrades the voluntariness of every dose after. The policy protects the future self from the present one.
  • Information asymmetry is severe. Few patients can actually judge dose, interaction, and overdose risk. The prescriber supplies the competence the buyer lacks.

More of the case:

  • The harm is fast and irreversible. A wrong choice about overdose isn’t one you get to revise. Mill’s defense of liberty leans on learning from mistakes; some mistakes here are unrepeatable.
  • A choosing-self / later-self gap. The person who wants the drug now and the person who will live with dependence are, in the ways that matter to Mill, not choosing on equal terms.

Each argument defends the gate by treating the choice as less voluntary and less informed than it first appears — reframing apparently hard paternalism as closer to soft. Much defensible hard paternalism takes this form.

Make It Contemporary

Live versions of the same gate, 2024–25:

  • Australia’s prescription-only nicotine rule for vaping (in force 2024): you can vape, but you must go through a doctor.
  • The FDA’s proposal to cut nicotine in cigarettes to non-addictive levels — gating the product, not the person.
  • Tightened opioid-prescribing limits after the 2010s overdose wave — which then pushed some patients to street supply.

What each is really doing:

Each dresses hard paternalism in a clinical pathway. “You may have it — through a doctor” feels gentler than “you may not have it.” But it is still the state deciding a competent adult may not simply choose for himself.

And the opioid example warns of the cost: tighten the legal gate too hard, and you can push people toward something worse.

Why Drug Policy Is Hard

Two Different Questions

So far we have sorted overrides by whether they are legitimate — the soft/hard, Mill question. Whether a drug policy actually works is a separate question, and the answer is not “be softer.”

  • Legitimacy (Axis 1): is overriding this choice justified? Turns on whether the choice is compromised or competent.
  • What works (Axis 2): does the regime cut harm, and on whom does the burden fall? Turns on coherence, consistent enforcement, funded treatment, and supply — not on how harsh it is.

The puzzle from the start of class lives on Axis 2. Keep the two apart: a policy can be ethically defensible and still fail, or effective and still unjust.

The Case for Control: Unrestricted Opiates

Before modern drug laws, opioids were sold without restriction. The consequences were significant.

In 19th-century America, opium, morphine, and (after 1898) heroin were sold legally and without prescription. They were in patent medicines, “women’s tonics,” and over-the-counter remedies. Heroin was marketed by Bayer as a cough suppressant.

Infants were dosed with opium-laced “soothing syrups” — Mrs. Winslow’s among them — to quiet teething. Some died. By 1900, a large share of the addicted population were ordinary patients, mostly women, hooked through medicine they had been told was safe.

This is the case for paternalism in drug policy: a wholly unrestricted market produced mass addiction in people who never made an informed choice. (Young 1961)

The Case against Bans: Prohibition

The Eighteenth Amendment (1919) banned making, selling, and shipping alcohol; the Volstead Act gave it teeth. It was repealed in 1933 — the only constitutional amendment ever fully overturned.

In between, drinking fell briefly and then climbed back. Illegal liquor became a major industry, federal prison populations roughly tripled, and the murder rate hit a peak it would not match for sixty years.

Three lessons that recur in modern drug policy:

  • Banning a widely-used substance does not erase demand for it.
  • The black market that replaces a legal one is more violent.
  • Enforcement falls hardest on the communities least able to push back.

Both poles — unrestricted access and total prohibition — produced serious harms. The policy question concerns what sits between them.

Stringency Doesn’t Predict Outcomes

Country Stance Overdose (per 100k) Incarceration (per 100k)
United States Punitive ~30 ~541
Portugal Lenient ~1 ~110
Netherlands Lenient ~1.5 ~65
Singapore Very harsh low ~156
Japan Harsh <0.5 ~36

The lenient countries and the harsh ones both have far lower overdose mortality than the US. Stringency alone does not predict the outcome: the most punitive country in the table and two of the most lenient sit at opposite ends of the policy spectrum, and the US is worse than all of them. (Tikkanen et al. 2022; Institute for Crime & Justice Policy Research 2023)

Two Coherent Models

The countries that do well share not a stance but a coherent design — enforcement, treatment, and supply policy that pull the same direction. Two opposite designs both work.

Consistent, predictable strictness (Singapore, Japan).

Both states made deliberate, sustained efforts to keep drug use rare and supply thin: uniform enforcement, strong norms, and — in Singapore — compulsory treatment alongside heavy penalties. Use rates and overdose deaths are low, and have stayed low for decades.

Thoughtful decriminalization (Portugal).

Portugal removed criminal penalties for personal possession in 2001 and redirected resources into treatment, outreach, and dissuasion commissions. Overdose deaths, HIV transmission, and drug-related incarceration all fell substantially.

The US matches neither: punitive without consistency, and without the funded treatment that both models rely on. (Oregon’s 2020 decriminalization, repealed by 2024, removed penalties without building the treatment side — the Portuguese half it left out.)

What the Soft-Paternalism Middle Achieved

Between unrestricted sale and outright bans sit tools that moved the numbers without charging the user.

  • Tobacco taxes, age limits, and ad and indoor-smoking limits. US adult smoking fell from ~42% in 1965 to ~11% in 2022; price accounts for about half the decline.
  • The drinking age. Raised to 21 in 1984; alcohol-related traffic deaths among 18–20-year-olds fell by about a third over the next decade.
  • Naloxone (Narcan). A widely distributed antidote, not a ban — the single step with the clearest effect on the modern opioid death rate.

The common mechanism is changing the price, availability, age of access, or downstream harm of a behavior rather than prosecuting those who engage in it.

Thought Question — Should the State Ban Tobacco?

Tobacco kills about 480,000 Americans a year — more than all illegal drugs combined. It is sold legally to anyone over twenty-one. The FDA has the power to cut nicotine to non-addictive levels; it has proposed this but not done it.

Write or discuss.

  1. Apply the three-question test to a thirty-year-old smoker. Is his daily choice really “competent and voluntary,” given addiction?
  2. Would you support a steep federal tobacco tax but not a ban? If so, what is doing the work in your view — and would the same reasoning apply to alcohol? To methamphetamine?
  3. If cutting nicotine to non-addictive levels is soft paternalism (it restores voluntary choice), is that a stronger justification than a ban — or a sneakier one?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction worth carrying into the pregnancy block: deterrence-of-care.

An enforcement policy can drive the very people it claims to protect away from the system meant to help them. When tobacco rules target the user, smokers don’t quit — they hide it from their doctor. When drug enforcement reaches into pregnancy, women don’t stop using — they skip prenatal care.

The Axis-2 lesson lands on the clinician. The distinctive feature of US prenatal drug policy is less its severity than its structure: it routes enforcement through the clinical encounter — test, report, refer to police — placing the clinician within the enforcement chain. A state can target supply without doing so. The clinician’s question is therefore not “strict or lenient” but whether the clinical encounter should serve an enforcement function at all.

Pregnancy as a Paternalism Problem

When the Second Party Enters

Mill’s third question — does the harm fall on the chooser alone? — assumes a single body. Pregnancy is the standard case where it does not: a choice the law would otherwise treat as self-regarding can affect a fetus the woman may intend to carry to term.

This creates a specific pressure — to reclassify her self-regarding choices as other-regarding, moving them out of the zone Mill protects. Background circumstances often narrow her real options as well (poverty, addiction, an abusive partner, no childcare); this does not license overriding her, but it belongs in the judgment. Whether the reclassification is warranted — and which intervention it would license — is what the spectrum below lets us test.

A Spectrum of Interventions

The aim — protecting the future child — is pursued with tools from mild to coercive. Read the rows as a ladder.

Intervention Example Type
Risk messaging “No safe amount” of alcohol; broad deli-meat, sushi, soft-cheese cautions, sometimes overstating thin evidence Soft (information-shaping)
Transient medical override Waiting out the sedated, pain-flooded laboring patient’s demand to leave Soft (capacity restored)
Mandatory reporting Newborn toxicology → child-protective referral and a Plan of Safe Care Mixed — services or surveillance, by state
Criminal prosecution Prenatal drug use charged as child abuse or homicide Hard — a charge pregnancy alone creates

Climbing from soft to hard, two things move together: the overridden choice becomes more competent and voluntary, so Mill’s presumption against interference strengthens, and the intervention’s empirical record worsens. The cases that follow examine the top rungs.

The Question, Stated Sharply

May the state charge a pregnant woman, on the basis of her pregnancy, with a graver crime than the same act would carry in anyone else?

The drug cases below involve illegal substances — so the conduct is already an offense. The novelty is what pregnancy adds. Outside pregnancy, methamphetamine use is, at most, simple possession; in these cases it is charged as child abuse, chemical endangerment, or homicide. The drug schedule is not doing the work — a fetal-harm theory is, and that theory is not limited to illegal drugs (the same machinery already reaches alcohol, prescribed medication, and — the second-most-common basis in the data — refusing recommended care). The question takes no position on the moral status of the fetus; it asks about the enforcement practice and whom it reaches.

Ferguson v. City of Charleston (2001)

The top rung’s mechanism: how the state reaches the hard end — covert testing routed to police.

In the late 1980s, the Medical University of South Carolina in Charleston began a secret urine drug-testing program on some pregnant patients. Positive results went to police. Hospital staff wrote the rules; the local prosecutor signed off. Triggers included things like “no prenatal care.”

Ten of the eleven women arrested were Black. The eleventh was a white woman whose chart noted she “lives with her boyfriend, who is a Negro.”

Important

Held, 6–3 (Stevens, J.): When the program’s main goal was to gather evidence for police, it was a Fourth Amendment search needing consent. The secret testing was unconstitutional.

Not decided: whether testing tied to treatment (no police) would be allowed, or whether civil steps short of arrest raise the same problem.

(Supreme Court of the United States 2001)

Tennessee’s Fetal-Assault Law — and the Data

The top rung’s result: what criminal prosecution actually produced.

2014. Tennessee made it a misdemeanor to take a narcotic during pregnancy “if her child is born addicted to or harmed by” it.

2014–16. About one hundred women were arrested, mostly in poor rural counties. Drug-use rates in pregnancy did not fall. Women reported avoiding prenatal care; some gave birth at home, alone, to dodge the hospital drug test.

2016. The legislature let it sunset.

What the national data show. Faherty et al. (2019), studying 4.5 million births, found states with punitive policies (criminal charges, civil commitment, or reporting as abuse) had a 27% higher rate of newborn drug withdrawal — the very outcome the laws were sold as preventing.

This is the deterrence-of-care pattern from earlier — the clearest causal line from punitive policy to a worse outcome. (The wider US maternal-mortality gap is driven mainly by access, race, and postpartum coverage, not by criminalization.)

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) — newborn opioid withdrawal — is the set of symptoms (tremors, feeding trouble, sometimes seizures) a baby shows after in-utero opioid exposure. It is treatable, and most babies do well with good care. It is also the diagnosis punitive reporting laws claim to reduce — and don’t.

(Faherty et al. 2019)

The Scale, and a Name

The top rung in human terms: who the hard end actually reaches.

Paltrow and Flavin counted 1,396 arrests and forced interventions on pregnant women in the US between 1973 and 2005 — the landmark count. Of those: 71% were low-income, 59% were women of color. The most common charge was drug-related; the second was refusing care. The pace has risen since Dobbs.

In 2021, a Comanche County jury found Brittney Poolaw, 21, guilty of first-degree manslaughter for a pregnancy loss at about seventeen weeks. The medical examiner found no cause; trace methamphetamine was in fetal tissue; the state’s own toxicologist said the levels weren’t clearly the cause. About one in five pregnancies is lost on its own at that stage. She was sentenced to four years; the conviction was affirmed in 2023.

Read the number carefully.

1,396 is not the count of pregnant women who used drugs. It is the count who were charged, jailed, or forced into a procedure by the state.

Dorothy Roberts’s Killing the Black Body (1997) is the classic account of why these patterns track race and class.

(Paltrow and Flavin 2013; Roberts 1997)

Where the Profession Draws the Line

The professional verdict on the top rung — and its convergence with the data.

ACOG Committee Opinions 473 / 664:

  • Substance use in pregnancy is “a medical condition that should be managed with the patient.”
  • Criminal charges and forced detention are “not effective in reducing” drug or alcohol abuse and are “contrary to the welfare of the mother and the fetus.”
  • Testing without consent is rejected; CPS reporting “depends on state law.”

Why this is more than an opinion.

ACOG’s ethical claim (manage it as medicine, don’t punish) and the population data (Faherty: punishment → worse withdrawal rates) point the same way.

That kind of agreement between an ethical argument and the empirical record is rare enough to carry real weight.

(American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2011, 2016; Faherty et al. 2019)

Thought Question — A Patient on Methamphetamine

A 32-week-pregnant patient arrives in early labor. Her urine is positive for methamphetamine. She has had three prenatal visits, no other problems, and says she used “yesterday, maybe the day before.” State law requires reporting drug exposure to CPS; the hospital may choose whether to involve police.

Write or discuss.

  1. Which rows of the pressure-spectrum are in play here, and which would you actually use?
  2. The attending asks whether to call CPS now or after delivery. What is the right answer, and why?
  3. Does your answer change if the drug is alcohol? Fentanyl? Cannabis that is legal in this state? Be honest about why.

Make It Contemporary

Three live trends, 2022–2025:

  • Pregnancy Justice reports a sharp jump in arrests for pregnancy outcomes since Dobbs — from roughly fifty a year nationally to more than two hundred in 2023.
  • Since 2022, at least nine states have introduced fetal-personhood bills that would widen the basis for charges like Poolaw’s.
  • Federal CAPTA still requires a Plan of Safe Care for every substance-exposed newborn — a service model that some states run as support and others as surveillance.

What unifies the punitive trend:

Each move treats the fetus as a legal subject whose interests limit what the pregnant person may do — pushing the “harm to others” exception to swallow Mill’s rule.

The medical position has not changed. The legal world it operates in has shifted sharply.

Partial Autonomy — The Hardest Case

When Autonomy Is Real but Incomplete

The framework has a clean answer at the two ends: full autonomy (hands off) and none (decide for them). The genuinely hard cases all live in the middle — where autonomy is partial.

We have already met three of them without naming the pattern:

  • The addicted chooser — competent overall, but each drug choice is partly compromised.
  • The pregnant patient — one chooser, but her choices reach a second party.
  • And the clearest of all, the one Mill named first: the adolescent.

The Adolescent

A 16-year-old is not a small child and not quite an adult. The mature-minor doctrine lets some adolescents consent to some care on their own — and many states let minors consent without a parent to a defined list: contraception, STI treatment, mental-health and substance-use care. (Coleman and Rosoff 2013)

The logic is soft-paternalist on both sides: we restrict them (age limits, parental consent for surgery) because capacity is still forming, and we free them (confidential STI care) because forcing parental involvement would drive them away from care entirely.

Mature-minor doctrine — a legal rule, varying by state, that lets a minor judged mature enough to understand a treatment consent to it without a parent. It is decided case-by-case, usually for lower-risk care, and is the law’s attempt to draw Mill’s “children” line at something finer than a birthday.

Thought Question — A Fifteen-Year-Old’s Refusal

A 15-year-old with a serious but treatable cancer has a strong chance of cure with chemotherapy and almost none without it. He understands all of this clearly and calmly, and refuses — he has watched the treatment’s effects and does not want them. His parents are split. He is not, by any clinical measure, incompetent.

Write or discuss.

  1. Is his refusal more like the competent adult’s (honor it) or the small child’s (override it)? What in his situation pushes each way?
  2. If you would override him but not a 25-year-old in the identical clinical spot, exactly what is the difference doing the work — and is it the same thing that justified age limits earlier?
  3. Where would respecting his refusal be honoring his autonomy, and where would it be abandoning him?

The One Idea

Mill’s line is simple; life is not.

Leave competent, informed, freely-choosing adults alone. Step in only to protect others, or to protect a choice that is compromised — and then only as much as it takes to give the real choice back.

Every hard case is a fight over one word: voluntary.

Most defensible paternalism wins by showing the choice was less free than it looked — addiction, youth, transient incapacity, pressure. Most indefensible paternalism loses by ignoring what its enforcement does to the very people it claims to help.
