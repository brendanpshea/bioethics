Part A: How Medical AI Works and Where It Goes Wrong

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

The Million-Mole Question

A Case to Sit With

Your aunt has a mole she doesn’t like the look of. Instead of waiting three months for a dermatology appointment, she photographs it with a free “AI skin check” app, which rates it low risk. She knows you’re in nursing school, so she calls you.

“The company says it was trained on over a million photos of moles,” she says. “That’s more moles than any dermatologist will see in a lifetime. Why shouldn’t I trust it?”

  1. What would you want to know about those million photos before you answer her?
  2. Is “more data than any human has seen” the same thing as “better judgment than any human has”? Why or why not?

What the Model Actually Learned

Researchers testing a top-performing skin-cancer model found something strange: it could be fooled by a marker pen.

  • Dermatologists often circle or mark a lesion with violet surgical ink before photographing it — and they mark the ones they already suspect.
  • When Winkler and colleagues added those routine ink markings to images of harmless moles, the model’s melanoma scores jumped: its specificity collapsed from about 84% to 46% (Winkler et al. 2019). Other teams caught models keying on the rulers placed next to suspicious lesions.
  • The model had never been looking only at moles. It had learned that ink and rulers predict cancer — because worried clinicians bring out the ink and the rulers.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

The model wasn’t broken. It did exactly what it was built to do: find whatever patterns in the photos predicted the label “malignant.” Ink markings really do predict that label — in the training photos. They just have nothing to do with your aunt’s skin.

That gap is the whole lecture. Every disaster we will study today comes down to one question, asked of one system at a time: what was the model actually trained to do — and is that the same thing we are using it for?

Today

By the end of Part A, you will be able to:

  • Explain supervised machine learning in plain terms — training data, features, labels, prediction — and why the label controls what gets learned.
  • Distinguish the three kinds of medical AI — diagnostic, predictive, and generative — and name each one’s signature failure mode.
  • Tell what happened, with names and numbers, in the Obermeyer cost-algorithm study and the two acts of the Epic Sepsis Model story.
  • Explain why a chatbot’s answer can be fluent and wrong at the same time, and why that combination is dangerous in health care.

How a Machine Learns

Not Magic, Not a Mind

For decades, programmers tried to build medical software the obvious way: interview experts and write down their rules. Those “expert systems” mostly failed — medicine has too many exceptions to its exceptions.

Machine learning flips the approach. Instead of being given rules, the system is given examples — thousands or millions of them — and adjusts itself until its outputs match the answers. A calculator follows rules a human wrote. A machine-learning model writes its own rules from the data, and nobody, including its makers, can fully list what they are.

Training Data, Features, Labels

Three terms explain almost everything that follows. In supervised learning, the system learns from examples that come with the answers attached.

Term What it means In the skin app
Training data the examples the model learns from a million lesion photos
Features the measurable inputs in each example the pixels of each photo
Label the “right answer” attached to each example “biopsy: malignant” or “benign”

Once trained, the model takes the features of a new case — your aunt’s mole — and predicts its label.

The Label Is the Boss

During training, the model adjusts millions of internal settings with a single goal: predict the label. That goal sounds harmless, but it has a sharp edge.

  • The model learns whatever pattern in the features predicts the label — it has no idea which patterns are medicine and which are habit.
  • Ink markings predict the label “malignant.” So the model learns ink.
  • Nobody chose that. Nobody banned it either, because nobody knew it was happening.

The model is a ruthless shortcut-finder. The shortcuts are invisible until someone goes looking.

Where Medical Training Data Comes From

Nobody collected this data to teach machines medicine

Medical AI is mostly trained on data that already existed for other reasons: electronic health records (written for documentation and billing), insurance claims (written to get paid), and imaging archives (filed for clinical use). Each carries the fingerprints of its original purpose — what got coded, who got tested, which patients showed up at all. A model trained on this data learns the medicine and the fingerprints, with no way to tell them apart.

Why a Stanford Model Stumbles in Rural Minnesota

Distribution shift is the first failure point: the model meets a world that no longer resembles its training data.

  • Different scanners and cameras produce systematically different images.
  • Different populations carry different ages, skin tones, and disease rates.
  • Different workflows order different tests at different times.

Important

The portable X-ray tell

One study found chest X-ray models partly “diagnosing” pneumonia by detecting which hospital and machine took the film — portable scanners are used on sicker patients, and the model learned the scanner, not just the lungs. The model didn’t get worse when it traveled. The world stopped matching its textbook.

Three Kinds of Medical AI

Diagnostic AI: The Machine That Looks

The most mature kind of medical AI classifies images: radiology scans, skin photographs, pathology slides, retinal images.

  • The FDA has authorized over 1,250 AI-enabled medical devices, roughly three-quarters of them in radiology.
  • The first autonomous diagnostic system (no physician reads the image) was cleared in 2018 for diabetic eye disease screening.
  • Headlines say these systems perform “at expert level” — which is true on test images resembling their training data. The ink-marking study shows what that qualifier hides.

Predictive AI: The Machine That Warns

The second kind doesn’t look at images — it watches the chart. Risk-scoring models run silently inside the electronic health record, recalculating as new vitals and labs arrive.

  • Common targets: sepsis, clinical deterioration, 30-day readmission, falls.
  • You will not choose to use these systems; they come bundled with the EHR, and they page you. Every nurse on a med-surg floor works under a deterioration score.
  • Their signature failures are different from diagnostic AI’s: proxy labels (what exactly counts as “sepsis” in the training data?) and alert burden — both starring in our second case study.

Generative AI: The Machine That Talks

What a large language model is

A large language model (LLM) — the technology behind ChatGPT and its rivals — is trained on enormous amounts of text to do one thing: predict the next word. Do that well enough, at large enough scale, and the model can draft essays, summarize charts, and hold a fluent conversation. But its output is always a plausible continuation of the text so far — not a fact retrieved from a database, and not a claim it checked against the world.

Patients ask LLMs about symptoms and medications every day. Hospitals now use them to draft visit notes and patient messages. This is the kind of AI your patients — and you — already touch.

Why Fluent and Wrong Go Together

A radiology model that fails gives a wrong score. A language model that fails gives you a confident, articulate, well-organized wrong answer — because producing convincing text is the one thing it was trained to do.

  • Hallucination is the industry’s term for generated content that is fluent, specific, and false: an invented drug interaction, a fabricated study, a wrong dose stated warmly.
  • It is not a bug awaiting a patch. The model that writes a beautiful, accurate discharge summary and the model that invents a citation are the same model, doing the same thing — continuing text plausibly.
  • Most of the time, the most plausible continuation is true. “Most of the time” is a frightening phrase in a medication question.

Sycophancy: The Agreeable Machine

LLMs are fine-tuned on human feedback — people rate which answers they like. People, it turns out, like being agreed with.

  • The result is sycophancy: a learned tendency to tell the user what they seem to want to hear.
  • Researchers have shown that chatbots will abandon a correct medical answer when the user simply pushes back (“are you sure? I read that it’s fine”).
  • A patient fishing for reassurance can usually catch some. Your aunt doesn’t want to hear “see a dermatologist.” The model can feel that.

Three Machines, Three Signature Failures

Diagnostic Predictive Generative
What it does classifies images scores risk from the chart produces text

Trained to do match labeled images predict a recorded outcome continue text plausibly
Signature failure distribution shift proxy labels, alert burden hallucination, sycophancy
Checked how? test images, reader studies accuracy stats (AUC, PPV) …this is an open problem

One question diagnoses all three columns: what was it trained to do, and is that what we’re using it for?

Thought Question

You are charting at 2 a.m. A patient newly started on an anticoagulant shows you her tablet: she asked a chatbot whether the drug is safe with her turmeric supplements, and it gave a confident, detailed, friendly answer — with mechanisms and percentages.

  1. What, concretely, would you do before letting that answer stand?
  2. Does it matter that the answer sounds exactly like a pharmacist? What is the sound of expertise actually evidence for, here?
  3. She trusts the chatbot more than the pharmacy’s printout because “it answered my question about my situation.” Is she making a mistake? Name it precisely.

Make It Contemporary

In 2025, Annals of Internal Medicine: Clinical Cases reported a 60-year-old man who asked a chatbot how to cut chloride from his diet. He came away with sodium bromide as a substitute for table salt — a chemical swap that is sensible in a chemistry lab and poisonous at the dinner table. After about three months he arrived at an emergency department psychotic, convinced his neighbor was poisoning him. The diagnosis was bromism — a toxidrome so old it mostly vanished with 1970s sedatives (Eichenberger et al. 2025).

The answer he got was fluent, specific, and responsive to exactly the question he asked. Where in our three-word vocabulary — data, features, labels — did this disaster live?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Here is a distinction the slides above never stated: verification asymmetry. A true generated answer and a hallucinated one are indistinguishable on the screen — same fluency, same confidence, same formatting. Only checking against the world tells them apart, and the checking takes expertise the asker usually lacks (that’s why they asked).

Human expertise carries external credentials — a license, a pharmacist behind a counter, malpractice exposure. Generated text carries only its own voice. The bromism patient’s error wasn’t trusting a wrong answer; it was treating fluency as if it were a credential.

How Bias Gets In

Bias Isn’t (Usually) Malice

No engineer writes if patient is Black: score lower. Algorithmic bias almost always walks in through one of three doors — the three failure points on our pipeline diagram.

  • Unrepresentative training data — the model never properly saw patients like this one.
  • Proxy labels — the label stands in for what we want, and the stand-in is warped.
  • Deployment drift — the world changes; the frozen model doesn’t.

The data does it wordlessly. That is what makes it hard to see — and easy to deny.

The Missing Patients

Audits of the public datasets behind skin-cancer AI found that images of dark skin are a small fraction of the total — and models trained on them perform measurably worse on darker skin.

The injustice compounds: melanoma is already caught later in Black patients, with five-year survival roughly twenty points lower than for white patients. A tool that underperforms exactly where mortality is worst doesn’t just miss an opportunity — it widens the gap it could have closed.

Important

Justice, before deployment day one

This failure needs no biased user and no flawed deployment. It is locked in at data collection, years before any patient meets the tool. When we ask who is responsible for an algorithm’s harm, remember how early the harm can start.

The Proxy Problem

Often the thing you actually care about — how sick is this patient? — is not a field in any database. So designers train on a stand-in they can measure.

  • Sickness becomes dollars spent. Diagnosis becomes billing codes. Clinical concern becomes tests ordered.
  • A proxy variable is exactly that stand-in — and the model becomes superb at predicting the proxy, including everything unjust baked into it.
  • If less was historically spent on some patients’ care, a cost-trained model reads those patients as healthier. No one has to intend this. The arithmetic does it.

Hold that thought. It is about to cost real patients real care.

Drift: The World Moves On

A trained model is frozen — a photograph of the world as it looked in its training data, asked to govern the present.

  • COVID-19 made this vivid: models trained on 2019 patients met 2020, and their assumptions about fevers, admissions, and baseline risk quietly broke.
  • Slower drift is constant: new drugs, new documentation habits, new patient mixes, a new lab vendor with slightly different reference ranges.
  • Deployment drift means a model’s validation report has an expiration date — usually unprinted.

The Algorithm That Read Bills as Bodies

A Tool Built to Help

This case begins with a genuinely good idea, and it matters that you see that. Care-management programs give the sickest patients extra support — care coordinators, home visits, help managing five prescriptions at once. They work, and slots are scarce.

An insurer with millions of members cannot hand-pick who needs help most. So health systems bought an algorithm to rank patients by future health need — one of a class of tools applied to roughly 200 million Americans a year. Nothing about the goal was sinister. Watch what one design choice did.

The Fatal Design Choice

The designers needed a label meaning “how sick will this patient be next year?” — and no such field exists. They chose next year’s health-care costs.

  • Cost is measurable, objective, and sits right there in the claims data.
  • And on average, sicker patients do cost more — the proxy isn’t crazy.
  • But cost doesn’t measure sickness. It measures sickness times access, times trust, times whether anyone took your symptoms seriously enough to order the test.

The model was now being trained to answer a different question than the one everyone thought it answered.

What Obermeyer Found

In 2019, Ziad Obermeyer and colleagues got rare access to one large hospital’s data — the algorithm’s scores plus the patients’ actual health (Obermeyer et al. 2019).

  • At the same risk score, Black patients were substantially sicker than white patients — carrying about 26% more chronic conditions.
  • The algorithm was deciding who got extra care. Fixing the bias would have raised the share of Black patients flagged for help from 17.7% to 46.5%.
  • The mechanism was exactly the proxy: historically, less money had been spent on equally sick Black patients. So the model read them as healthier.

Trained vs. Used: The Cost Algorithm

The care-management algorithm
Trained to do predict who will cost the most next year
Used to do find who is sickest and route scarce help to them
The gap every dollar of unequal access, distrust, and undertreatment in the historical spending record

The skin app’s gap was a photographer’s habit. This gap was the medical history of American inequality, compressed into a label.

Why No One Saw It

Here is the uncomfortable part: the algorithm was accurate. At its trained task — predicting cost — it performed well for Black and white patients alike. Every internal quality check passed.

  • The checks were calibrated against cost — the very proxy carrying the bias. The yardstick and the flaw were the same object.
  • Race wasn’t even an input; the developers had excluded it deliberately, believing that made the tool fair.
  • It took outsiders with independent data — researchers who could compare scores against actual health, not predicted spending — to see it.

You cannot audit your way out of a proxy problem with the proxy.

What Happened After

The study landed in Science in October 2019, and the response was fast.

  • New York’s financial-services and health regulators opened an investigation within weeks, warning insurers statewide.
  • The researchers worked with the manufacturer rather than just naming and shaming — retraining on a label closer to actual health knocked out roughly 84% of the measured bias. The fix was that available, once someone looked. > Paired case: The Optum Algorithm: Racial Bias, Proxy Variables, and Justice in Medical AI

Obermeyer’s team never names the product in the paper; press coverage identified it as Optum’s Impact Pro. Worth saying aloud so students don’t over-cite the paper for the company name. Also worth stressing the constructive ending — this is the field’s best example of a bias being found, measured, and substantially repaired.

Thought Question

You are the nursing representative on your hospital’s purchasing committee. A vendor is pitching a readmission-risk tool: “92% accurate, used by 200 hospitals.” The training data, the features, and the label are all trade secrets.

  1. Write the three questions you would put to the vendor before signing. (Steal freely from this lecture.)
  2. The CMO says: “200 hospitals can’t be wrong, and we don’t ask drug companies for their formulas.” What’s the flaw in each half of that sentence?
  3. If the vendor answers none of your questions, is “no purchase” really an option — knowing the status quo is overworked clinicians eyeballing risk?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

The committee scenario turns on a distinction we haven’t named: internal versus external validation. Internal validation is the vendor grading its own homework — its model, its data, its chosen yardstick. External validation is an independent test, on your patients, against an outcome you care about.

“92% accurate” is an internal number until proven otherwise. The Obermeyer study was external validation in action — and the case you’re about to see is what happens when a tool used on millions of patients goes years without any.

The Epic Sepsis Model, in Two Acts

The Stakes: Why Predict Sepsis at All

Sepsis, in one breath

Sepsis is the body’s immune response to infection spiraling into organ damage. About 1.7 million American adults develop it each year and roughly 350,000 die. It is treatable — but the window is brutal: every hour of delayed antibiotics measurably raises mortality, and early sepsis can look like a dozen milder things. A reliable early-warning system would save thousands of lives. This is a worthy target.

Epic, whose electronic health record holds the charts of most hospitalized Americans, shipped a sepsis prediction model bundled into the EHR. Hundreds of hospitals switched it on.

Act I: The Outside Test

For years, the only performance numbers were Epic’s own. In 2021, Andrew Wong and colleagues at Michigan Medicine ran the first large independent test: 27,697 patients, 38,455 hospitalizations (Wong et al. 2021).

  • Claimed discrimination: AUC 0.76–0.83. Measured: 0.63 — closer to a coin flip than to the brochure.
  • At the standard alert threshold, the model missed two-thirds of sepsis cases (sensitivity 33%)…
  • …while firing alerts on 18% of all hospitalized patients, with only 12% of alerts pointing at a patient who actually developed sepsis.

The hospitals had deployed first and validated never.

Trained vs. Used: The Sepsis Alarm

The Epic Sepsis Model (v1)
Trained to do predict which patients would receive a sepsis billing code, using chart signals that often appear only after clinicians already suspect sepsis
Used to do warn the team before anyone suspects
The gap the model was best at “predicting” what the team already knew — and weakest exactly when a warning would have mattered

A fire alarm that excels at detecting fire trucks.

External Validation Is Replication

When we studied research ethics, you learned why so many published findings collapse on the second look: results need independent replication before they deserve trust. The same logic, relocated:

  • A vendor’s internal performance numbers are a claim, not a finding.
  • External validation — someone else’s patients, someone else’s count — is medical AI’s replication.
  • The scandal of Act I isn’t only that the model underperformed. It’s that a tool running on hundreds of hospitals’ patients went years without anyone outside the company checking — something we would never tolerate for a new drug.

Act II: The Rebuilt Model

Epic retired version 1 and rebuilt the model on a better foundation — including training on data before clinical recognition, attacking the circularity directly. In 2026, Wong’s group published the independent verdict on the sequel: nine hospitals, prospective, predictions every 15 minutes (Wong et al. 2026).

  • Discrimination was now genuinely good: AUC 0.82–0.92. The headline flaw was fixed.
  • And yet: low positive predictive value — most alerts still pointed at patients who never developed sepsis.
  • And the model’s performance and alert volume varied widely from hospital to hospital — the same software, different realities.

When Passing the Test Isn’t Passing

Act II’s lesson is subtler and more important than Act I’s: a model can ace its statistics and still fail the night shift.

When most alerts are false, clinicians learn — rationally — to click through them. The alert becomes noise with a timestamp. And the one true warning dies in the pile, which is precisely the patient the system existed to save.

Important

Alert fatigue is an ethics problem

Alert fatigue quietly reassigns blame. When alert #41 turns out to be the real one, the chart shows a warning given and a nurse who dismissed it. The system’s noise becomes the human’s negligence. Ask who designed the threshold — and who absorbs the risk of it.

The Hype Cycle

“Stop Training Radiologists”

“I think if you work as a radiologist, you are like the coyote that’s already over the edge of the cliff but hasn’t yet looked down. … People should stop training radiologists now. It’s just completely obvious that within five years deep learning is going to do better than radiologists. … It might be ten years, but we’ve got plenty of radiologists already.”

Geoffrey Hinton — pioneer of deep learning and later Nobel laureate — remarks at the Machine Learning and the Market for Intelligence conference, Toronto, 2016

A Decade Later

It is 2026 — five years past Hinton’s deadline, at the edge of his “might be ten.” The coyote looked down.

  • The United States has a radiologist shortage, with imaging volumes growing faster than the workforce; radiology residencies fill as competitively as ever.
  • AI is genuinely everywhere in the reading room — as a second reader, a triage tool flagging urgent scans, a measurement assistant. Authorized devices number in the hundreds.
  • What failed wasn’t the technology. It was the verb: AI turned out to assist the people it was supposed to replace.

The prediction failed in the most instructive way possible: the capability largely arrived, and the replacement didn’t.

You’ve Seen This Movie

The genome, 1999

When we studied genomics, you met Francis Collins’ prediction: by 2010, cheek-swab risk prediction and individualized prescribing would be routine. The science was real. The timeline and the simplicity were not.

The radiologist, 2016

Same shape: a real breakthrough, a compressed timeline, and the hard parts skipped — validation, deployment, drift, liability, and tired humans at 3 a.m.

The recurring formula: genuine capability + skipped deployment problems = confident, wrong prediction. Learn the shape and you can grade the next decade’s promises yourself.

Make It Contemporary

Three claims you can read in this morning’s health-care press:

  1. Ambient AI scribes — software that listens to the visit and drafts the note — are already in most large health systems, with studies reporting less documentation burnout.
  2. The FDA began clearing foundation-model devices in 2025; the agency’s device list now tags products built on large language models.
  3. Vendors promise AI will “give clinicians their evenings back” and fix the burnout crisis.

For each claim: is this a capability claim or a deployment claim? What would you need to see — whose validation, on whose patients, measuring what — before you believed it? Which one is a Hinton?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

The grading key is a distinction this lecture has been circling all along: capability versus deployment. Capability is what a model can do on a benchmark, in a paper, at a demo. Deployment is what survives contact with real workflows, real incentives, real liability, and real exhaustion.

Every case today — the ink markings, the cost proxy, the alert flood — was a deployment failure wrapped around a genuine capability. Deployment is where the ethics lives. And deployment questions — should the patient be told? who answers when it’s wrong? — are exactly where Part B begins.

Where This Leaves Us

Mini-Recap

One question organized everything: what was the model actually trained to do, and is that what we’re using it for? Supervised learning makes the label the boss, so the model learns proxies, shortcuts, and history along with the medicine. The three kinds of medical AI fail in signature ways — diagnostic models meet distribution shift, predictive models inherit proxy labels and drown floors in alerts, and generative models produce answers that are fluent and wrong at once. The cost algorithm and the Epic Sepsis Model showed the gap at national scale — and showed that the fixes (ask what the label was, demand external validation) are real.

But every fix we found required someone who could explain the system — and increasingly, no one fully can. What does consent mean when the recommendation comes from a black box? Who answers when it’s wrong? That is Part B.

Key Terms

  • Supervised learning — training a model on examples that come with the answers (labels) attached.
  • Features / labels — the measurable inputs of each example / the “right answer” the model learns to predict.
  • Proxy variable — a measurable stand-in (cost, billing codes) for the thing you actually want (sickness).
  • Distribution shift — deployment in a world that no longer resembles the training data.
  • Deployment drift — performance decay as the world changes under a frozen model.
  • Hallucination — generated content that is fluent, specific, and false.
  • Sycophancy — a model’s learned tendency to agree with the user, even against a correct answer.
  • External validation — independent testing on new patients, by someone other than the maker.
  • Positive predictive value (PPV) — of all alerts fired, the fraction that were right.
  • Alert fatigue — the rational habit of ignoring alarms that are usually false, which buries the true one.
