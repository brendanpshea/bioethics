1. A History of Legal Boundaries

Exposure & Infanticide in Antiquity

The Historical Reality

  • In ancient Roman and Greek societies, the abandonment or exposure of unwanted newborn infants was widely tolerated and legally practiced.
  • This practice served as the primary mechanism for family planning, population control, and managing congenital anomalies.

The Patria Potestas Model

  • Under Roman law, the male head of the household held absolute legal authority over all family members, which included the power of life and death.
  • A newborn did not possess inherent legal status at birth; protection was granted only when the father formally accepted the child into the household.

Point out that in antiquity, the boundary of legal and moral protection did not sit at fertilization, neural activity, or birth. It sat at paternal acceptance.

The Shift to Moral Status

  • The rise of Judeo-Christian ethics in the late Roman Empire led to the gradual outlawing of infant exposure and infanticide.
  • These moral shifts pushed the threshold of legal protection backward from paternal acceptance to the moment of birth.
  • The newborn was recognized for the first time as an independent legal subject owed protection against the household’s immediate utility.
  • This historical transition established the precedent that the state holds a fundamental duty to protect vulnerable nascent life.

English Common Law & “Quickening”

  • Under English Common Law, abortion was not treated as a crime or misdemeanor prior to the biological boundary of quickening (Mohr 1978).
  • Quickening is defined as the moment when a pregnant person first feels the fetus move in the womb, typically occurring between 16 and 20 weeks.
  • Before quickening occurred, there was no objective evidence of independent life under common law understanding, meaning early termination was legally neutral.
  • Once quickening was documented, performing an abortion was treated as a misdemeanor rather than a capital offense.

Emphasize that the quickening standard represents a subjective, patient-centered boundary of legal status rather than an abstract biological marker.

Early American Law & Advertised Midwifery

  • Early American jurisdictions inherited and fully adopted the English Common Law quickening standard.
  • Prior to the mid-19th century, early-term abortifacient medications and services were openly advertised and sold in U.S. newspapers.
  • Reproductive healthcare was largely managed within the home by patients, midwives, and herbalists without formal state interference.
  • Before 1840, no state in the union had enacted statutory bans on early-term abortion.

The 19th-Century Professionalization Shift

  • The newly formed American Medical Association (AMA) launched a aggressive national campaign in 1847 to criminalize abortion at all stages (Mohr 1978).
  • Elite physicians utilized this campaign to establish monopoly control over medical practice, successfully squeezing out midwives and homeopaths.
  • Physicians argued that biological life was continuous from fertilization, making the common law quickening threshold scientifically arbitrary.
  • By 1880, every U.S. state had enacted statutes criminalizing abortion at all stages, shifting reproductive control from the home to state-licensed doctors.

Detail the dual motives of the AMA campaign: genuine scientific conviction about embryonic continuity alongside professional self-preservation and market control.

2. Legal Literacy Primer

Statutes vs. Common Law

Statutory Law

  • Written laws are enacted directly by legislative bodies such as Congress or state legislatures.
  • These codes are precise, prospective, and codify specific rules, prohibitions, and penalties.
  • An example is a state legislature passing a statutory code banning abortion after six weeks.

Common Law

  • Case law is developed dynamically by judges through written decisions of courts.
  • Rulings are iterative, retrospective, and based on the historical accumulation of decisions.
  • An example is the court-developed duty-to-warn standard in modern psychiatry (Tarasoff).

Intro students often confuse these two. Emphasize that the U.S. healthcare system sits at the intersection of both statutory codes and judicial common law rulings.

Precedent Defined

Legal Precedent

A precedent is a principle or rule established in a previous legal case that is either binding on or persuasive for a court when deciding subsequent cases with similar facts.

  • Lower courts must follow the binding precedents set by higher courts within their specific jurisdiction.
  • Higher courts generally follow their own prior precedents to maintain horizontal consistency and legal integrity.
  • Precedents allow individuals, hospitals, and clinicians to predict the legal consequences of their professional clinical actions.

The Principle of Stare Decisis

  • The legal principle of stare decisis translates literally to “stand by things decided.”
  • Under this rule, courts must adhere to prior precedents and not lightly overrule established rulings.
  • This conservative standard prevents the law from changing rapidly with every political shift or judicial appointment.
  • Adhering to prior rulings ensures that citizens can rely on the law as a stable and predictable framework over decades.

Explain that stare decisis is not an absolute rule, but a powerful judicial presumption in favor of consistency over individual correction.

The Moral Weight of Reliance Interests

  • Citizens develop legitimate expectations and structure their lives, educations, and careers relying on established precedents.
  • Over decades, individuals made long-term decisions about families and careers relying on the availability of reproductive autonomy.
  • Rulings that overrule long-standing precedents directly disrupt these reliance interests, particularly impacting women’s participation in public life.
  • To overrule a precedent, a court must justify why correcting a perceived error is worth disrupting massive reliance interests.

Overturning Precedent: The High Bar

  • High courts can and do overturn prior precedents if a ruling is judged fundamentally unworkable or unjust.
  • The court examines whether the precedent is clear enough for lower courts to apply consistently, or if it generates endless confusion.
  • Judges consider whether medical science, technology, or societal understanding have evolved to make the old rule obsolete.
  • A famous historical example is the Supreme Court overturning the segregationist precedent of Plessy v. Ferguson in Brown v. Board of Education.

Use Brown v. Board to show that overturning precedent is sometimes morally mandatory, but requires meeting a heavy burden of justification.

Precedent in Action: Casey’s Defense of Roe

  • In Planned Parenthood v. Casey, a newly appointed conservative-leaning court was widely expected to overrule Roe v. Wade (US Supreme Court 1992).
  • Justices O’Connor, Kennedy, and Souter refused to overrule Roe, basing their entire decision on stare decisis and reliance interests.
  • The court ruled that overruling under pressure would subvert the judiciary’s legitimacy and disrupt societal reliance on reproductive liberty.
  • The compromise preserved the essential holding of Roe (pre-viability access) while permitting more pre-viability regulations.

Precedent Overturned: Dobbs’ Reasoning

  • In Dobbs v. Jackson, the Supreme Court overruled both Roe and Casey entirely, ending fifty years of federal constitutional protection (US Supreme Court 2022).
  • Justice Alito argued that Roe was egregiously wrong from the day it was decided, violating originalist constitutional limits.
  • The majority claimed that Roe’s trimester framework was unworkable and had failed to produce a stable societal settlement.
  • Alito argued that because Roe withdrew protection from what many citizens consider a human person, reliance interests could not protect a wrongful precedent.

Highlight Alito’s point: stare decisis cannot be used to protect a precedent that the current court judges to involve the unjust termination of potential human life.

Legal Literacy Review

Predictability vs. Correction

  1. In your own life, how important is it that rules remain stable and predictable over time? Why might a court choose to stand by an old decision even if it thinks the original reasoning was flawed?
  2. How do people rely on long-standing legal rights when making major plans like choosing a career, getting married, or deciding when to start a family?
  3. If key laws can be reversed whenever new judges are appointed, does that make the court look like a neutral legal referee or a political body? How can a legal system protect its credibility?

3. The U.S. Constitutional Arc

Three Visions of Liberty

  • Roe v. Wade (1973) defined liberty as privacy and autonomy under the Due Process Clause, protected by a biological trimester grid (US Supreme Court 1973).
  • Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992) defined liberty as relational reliance and self-definition, protected by the “undue burden” standard (US Supreme Court 1992).
  • Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) defined liberty as strictly bounded by history and democratic federalism, returning reproductive control to local state legislatures (US Supreme Court 2022).

Use this slide to group these three cases conceptually. Frame them as three completely distinct philosophical definitions of what “liberty” means under the constitution.

Model 1: The Trimester Model & Biological Grid (Roe)

The Core Holding

  • The 14th Amendment’s liberty guarantee includes a fundamental right to privacy that covers reproductive decisions (US Supreme Court 1973).
  • Any state law restricting this right must meet the highest bar of constitutional review, known as strict scrutiny.

The Biological Grid

  • During Trimester 1, the state cannot restrict abortion; decisions rest solely with the patient and doctor.
  • During Trimester 2, the state may regulate abortion only to protect maternal health.
  • During Trimester 3 (Viability), the state’s interest in potential life becomes compelling, allowing full bans except to save maternal life.

Model 2: The Viability / Undue Burden Model (Casey)

  • Casey abandoned the trimester grid, establishing viability as the single moral and legal threshold (US Supreme Court 1992).
  • Before viability, the state may regulate abortion to protect potential life, provided the regulation does not create an undue burden.
  • An undue burden is defined as a regulation that has the purpose or effect of placing a substantial obstacle in the path of a patient seeking an abortion.
  • After viability, the state’s interest is compelling, and it may ban abortion entirely, provided it has exceptions for maternal life and health.

Point out that Casey lowered the level of constitutional protection from “strict scrutiny” to “undue burden,” paving the way for targeted restrictions (TRAP laws).

The Reliance Focus in Casey

  • The Casey majority emphasized that a generation of women had structured their lives and careers around reproductive autonomy.
  • The court argued that reproductive control is essential for women’s equal participation in the economic and social life of the nation.
  • Rejecting Roe under political pressure would cause deep harm by undermining the reliance interests of millions of citizens.
  • This focus highlights how reproductive laws directly impact structural justice and gender equality.

Model 3: The Democratic / History Model (Dobbs)

  • Dobbs declared that the U.S. Constitution does not confer a right to abortion, overruling Roe and Casey entirely (US Supreme Court 2022).
  • Justice Alito held that any constitutional right not explicitly named in the text must be “deeply rooted in this Nation’s history and tradition.”
  • Because 19th-century common law and state statutes criminalized abortion, Alito concluded it lacks any constitutional basis.
  • The authority to regulate, ban, or protect abortion is returned entirely to the elected representatives of each individual state.

Highlight that Dobbs changes the ethical forum: abortion is no longer a question of constitutional rights managed by federal courts, but a political question settled by state-by-state elections.

Comparative Grid: U.S. Constitutional Arc

Legal Model / Case Constitutional Basis Core Legal Threshold State Regulatory Power
Roe v. Wade (1973) 14th Amendment Privacy Trimesters (1st, 2nd, 3rd) Banned in Trimester 1; regulated in 2 for health; bans allowed in 3
Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992) 14th Amendment Liberty & Reliance Viability (approx. 24 weeks) Allowed pre-viability unless it poses an undue burden
Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) Democratic Federalism / State Interest None (Returned to States) Unrestricted power to ban from conception with no federal right

Constitutional Arc Debate

Where should moral questions be settled?

  1. When a society is deeply divided on a moral issue, is it better to let the voters in each state decide through local elections, or should a national court declare a single standard that protects everyone?
  2. If the law only protects rights that are “deeply rooted in history,” what does that mean for modern issues that our ancestors never envisioned, like modern technology or changing family structures?
  3. How does a state-by-state legal patchwork affect fairness? Is it fair that a person’s legal rights and healthcare options change completely just by crossing a state line?

4. The Bedside-Law Collision

The U.S. Post-Dobbs Patchwork & 2x2 Grid

The State Landscape

  • The U.S. instantly fractured into highly restrictive and highly protected zones following the Dobbs decision.
  • Pre-existing trigger laws designed to automatically ban abortion the moment Roe was overturned took effect immediately in several states.

The 2x2 Legal Matrix

  • Total Bans: Abortion banned from conception with minimal exceptions (e.g., Texas, Idaho).
  • Gestational Limits: Restrictions at 6, 12, or 15 weeks (e.g., Florida, North Carolina).
  • Protected Access: Access explicitly codified in state constitutions (e.g., California, Michigan).
  • Shield States: Laws protecting local clinicians from out-of-state subpoenas (e.g., Massachusetts).

Explain how this patchwork creates extreme legal complexity for patients who must cross state lines to access care, and clinicians operating near borders.

The “Chilling Effect” in Clinical Practice

  • The chilling effect represents the inhibition or discouragement of legitimate professional clinical judgment due to fear of severe legal sanctions.
  • Post-Dobbs statutes in restrictive states carry severe felony penalties, including up to 99 years in prison, massive personal fines, and mandatory loss of medical license.
  • When faced with severe criminal prosecution, clinician attention inevitably shifts from patient welfare to personal legal defense.
  • Fear of prosecution forces clinicians to delay or withhold standard-of-care treatments until a patient’s condition becomes undeniably life-threatening (Harris 2022).

Clinical Autonomy Under Threat

  • Clinicians are historically treated as autonomous moral agents responsible for patient welfare under the principle of beneficence.
  • Post-Dobbs laws strip clinicians of this professional autonomy, replacing standard clinical guidelines with statutory mandates.
  • Physicians in emergency departments must frequently consult hospital lawyers before performing routine medical procedures for miscarriages.
  • This creates a direct moral conflict between clinical duty (beneficence) and personal self-preservation (avoiding a felony).

Stress the deep moral distress this creates: doctors are trained to act early to prevent sepsis or hemorrhage, but laws force them to wait until these disasters occur.

The Ambiguity of Medical Exceptions

  • Most restrictive state laws include exceptions only for a “medical emergency” or “to save the life of the mother.”
  • Clinical deterioration is a continuous, unpredictable spectrum rather than a binary switch between healthy and dead.
  • Statutes do not define how close to death a patient must be—or what percentage chance of mortality they must face—to license medical intervention.
  • Clinicians are forced to watch patients deteriorate because intervening too early could be prosecuted as a felony.

Bedside Case 1: Ectopic Pregnancy

Ectopic Pregnancy & Delayed Care

An ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilized egg implants outside the uterus (usually in a fallopian tube). It is biologically incapable of surviving, and as it grows, it will inevitably rupture the tube, causing life-threatening internal hemorrhage.

  • The clinical standard of care demands the immediate termination of the pregnancy via surgery or medication to protect the patient’s life and fertility.
  • Because some state laws ban all abortions from fertilization, physicians have reportedly delayed treating ectopic pregnancies until they can document a rupture or a failing heart rate.
  • Forcing a patient to wait until a fallopian tube ruptures violates the principle of non-maleficence, subjecting them to avoidable trauma and risking permanent infertility.

Highlight that ectopic pregnancy has a 100% fetal mortality rate anyway, making delayed care a pure harm to the patient with zero benefit to potential life.

Bedside Case 2: Previable Preeclampsia

  • A patient at 20 weeks gestation develops severe preeclampsia, risking maternal stroke, kidney failure, or seizures.
  • At 20 weeks, the fetus is completely previable (cannot survive outside the womb), and the only medical cure is delivery.
  • The fetus still has a heartbeat, meaning delivery represents an abortion under restrictive state statutes.
  • Hospital legal teams in restrictive states have advised doctors to wait until the patient develops active organ failure or seizures before delivering, to prove they met the emergency exception.
  • The medical team is forced to violate beneficence, actively watching a patient under their care slide into a preventable medical catastrophe.

Clinician Moral Distress

  • Moral distress represents the psychological suffering experienced when a clinician knows the ethically correct action to take but is blocked from acting by legal or systemic constraints.
  • Pediatricians, OB-GYNs, and emergency physicians in restrictive states report unprecedented levels of anxiety, moral distress, and burnout.
  • Restrictive states have seen significant drops in residency applications and a flight of maternal-fetal medicine specialists, creating OB-GYN deserts.
  • When specialists leave, the justice burden falls heaviest on low-income patients who cannot afford to travel out of state for basic prenatal care.

Connect this slide back to the principle of Justice: the downstream effect of clinical moral distress is a structural reduction in healthcare access for the most vulnerable.

Bedside Policy Debrief

How should hospital boards respond?

  1. Imagine you are running a hospital in a state with a strict abortion ban. How do you help doctors decide when a patient is “sick enough” to qualify for a life-saving exception without risking prison time?
  2. If a doctor believes that waiting for legal clearance will cause permanent harm to a patient, what should they do? Does their duty of beneficence to the patient override their duty to follow the state law?
  3. How does the threat of severe criminal penalties change the relationship between a patient and their doctor? What happens to trust when a doctor has to worry about legal liability?

5. Global Policy and State Coercion

Placing the U.S. in Global Context

  • Global abortion laws range from total prohibition to unrestricted elective access, reflecting highly diverse cultural and political balances.
  • The U.S. is one of the very few developed nations to actively restrict reproductive rights in the 21st century, contrasting with a global trend toward liberalization.
  • Most European nations balance fetal and maternal interests through early gestational limits combined with complete universal healthcare integration.
  • Canada treats abortion entirely as a standard medical procedure with zero criminal statutes, relying solely on professional medical guidelines.

Use this section to broaden the debate, showing students that the U.S. culture-war framing is not the only way modern democracies organize reproductive policy.

Model A: The European Compromise

  • Countries like France, Germany, and Italy generally restrict elective abortion after 12 to 14 weeks.
  • These limits are paired with comprehensive state support: mandated impartial counseling, short waiting periods, and universal public funding.
  • Because abortion is fully covered by national health insurance and provided in standard clinics, it remains highly accessible, safe, and free of social stigma.
  • While Europe has earlier gestational limits than pre-Dobbs America, its lack of financial barriers and systemic stigma means actual access is often smoother and more equitable.

Model B: The Canadian Medical Model

  • In 1988, the Supreme Court of Canada (R. v. Morgentaler) struck down the country’s abortion law as a violation of a woman’s security of the person.
  • Since 1988, Canada has had no federal or provincial criminal law governing abortion whatsoever.
  • Abortion is regulated like any other surgical or medical procedure under provincial medical licensing boards and professional standard guidelines.
  • Fully funded by provincial healthcare plans, ensuring that access is distributed based on medical need rather than individual ability to pay.

Explain that Canada’s model has not led to skyrocketing late-term abortions; professional standards and clinical guidelines naturally govern the practice responsibly without criminal codes.

China’s One-Child Policy & Coercive Anti-Natalism (1979–2015)

  • Fearing that rapid population growth would paralyze economic development, the Chinese government turned to extreme state coercion in the 1970s.
  • China’s One-Child Policy restricted urban couples to a single child, representing the most radical experiment in reproductive engineering in human history (Fong 2016).
  • The policy was enforced through massive financial penalties, mandatory IUD insertions, forced abortions, and state-sanctioned sterilizations.
  • The state treated female fertility as a collective national resource, overriding individual bodily self-determination to achieve state utility goals.

The Mechanisms of Coercion

State Surveillance

  • A vast network of state officials monitored women’s menstrual cycles, contraception use, and pregnancies.
  • Factory managers, neighborhood committees, and local informants monitored compliance, making reproductive privacy impossible (Fong 2016).

The Consequences

  • Women pregnant with unauthorized second children were routinely forced into clinics for terminations up to the third trimester.
  • The policy created a massive gender imbalance due to sex-selective abortion, alongside a rapidly shrinking labor force.

Highlight Fong’s research: the policy left China with a rapidly shrinking workforce and millions of “surplus men” who will never find partners.

China’s Sudden Demography Pivot

  • Facing a rapidly shrinking labor force and an aging demographic crisis, the Chinese state abruptly ended the One-Child Policy in 2015.
  • The government rapidly pivoted to a Two-Child Policy in 2016, and then a Three-Child Policy in 2021.
  • While the rules changed, the core bioethical issue remained: individual reproduction remains treated as a tool of state economic planning.
  • Utilitarian calculations that treat individual lives solely as means to national economic utility inevitably violate the Kantian duty to respect persons as ends in themselves.

6. Coercion, Paternalism, and Population

The Birth Dearth & Demographic Panic

  • Across the developed world, fertility rates have collapsed well below the replacement rate of 2.1.
  • Falling birth rates lead to shrinking workforces, collapsing tax bases, and underfunded social safety nets for the elderly.
  • Governments are increasingly shifting their focus from population control to active state interventions designed to increase birth rates.
  • These pro-natalist interventions range from supportive, voluntary incentives to extreme, coercive paternalism.

Use this slide to introduce the pro-natalist side of state intervention, showing that coercion can occur in both directions: forcing people not to have children, or forcing them to have them.

Non-Coercive Pro-Natalism (Support Models)

  • Countries like Singapore and France use non-coercive, voluntary incentives to make childbearing more attractive (Singapore National Population and Talent Division 2023).
  • State support includes cash baby bonuses paid directly to parents, subsidized IVF treatments, and priority access to public housing.
  • Governments also provide highly subsidized childcare and paid parental leave for both mothers and fathers.
  • These policies respect autonomy, aiming to reduce the economic and structural barriers that prevent people from having children they already desire.

Coercive Pro-Natalism: Romania’s Decree 770

  • Fearing economic stagnation, dictator Nicolae Ceaușescu issued Decree 770 in 1966, totally banning all abortion and contraception for women under 45 with fewer than four children (Kligman 1998).
  • The state treated the womb as national property, declaring that the fetus was the property of the entire society.
  • Secret police agents monitored hospitals; women underwent mandatory monthly gynecological exams at work to detect early pregnancies and prevent secret abortions.
  • The policy spurred a massive black market of unsafe illegal abortions, leading to over 10,000 maternal deaths and a massive wave of abandoned children in state orphanages.

Detail the tragic legacy of Romania’s Decree 770: it remains the most extreme modern warning of what happens when a state uses coercive pro-natalism to treat citizens as breeding stock.

Case Debrief: The Scope of State Interest

Does the state own the future?

  1. Is there a difference between a government offering positive incentives (like tax breaks or housing priorities) to encourage childbearing and a government actively banning reproductive healthcare? Where is the ethical boundary?
  2. If a state provides massive financial help to new parents, is that a supportive public health policy, or does it create subtle pressure that overrides personal choice?
  3. How should a society balance its need for future generations with an individual’s right to choose a life path that does not include children?

7. Conceptual Integration & Conclusion

The Four Principles Conflict Grid

Principle Pro-Choice / Liberal Interpretation Pro-Life / Conservative Interpretation
Autonomy The pregnant person holds absolute self-ownership and the right to make irreversible physical decisions about their body. The fetus has a nascent, developing capacity for autonomy that must be protected against external termination.
Beneficence Preserving the health, life-plans, physical safety, and socioeconomic flourishing of the pregnant person. Preserving the primary, fundamental benefit of life itself for the developing child.
Non-Maleficence Avoiding the severe physical, psychological, and systemic harms of forced pregnancy and delayed clinical care. Avoiding the ultimate, irreversible harm of ending a human life (killing).
Justice Ensuring that reproductive burdens do not fall disproportionately on low-income, marginalized, and minority patients. Protecting the most vulnerable, defenseless, and voiceless human subject (the fetus) under the law.

Keep this slide visible for a long time. It shows that the abortion debate is not a simple battle between “morality” and “immorality,” but a clash of two internally coherent, competing interpretations of the four core principles.

The Coherence of Moderation

  • Public debates often present the choice as an absolute binary between total protection from conception vs. total access up to birth.
  • Many bioethicists defend a gradualist model, where moral status and the right to protection grow continuously as the fetus develops.
  • A gradualist position protecting elective access early, but restricting it after viability with robust health exceptions is not a weak compromise.
  • It represents a coherent, principled attempt to balance patient autonomy (which dominates early) with state interest in potential life (which becomes compelling later as biological capacity develops).

Bridge to Case Study 4: Kate Cox

  • In 2023, a Texas mother named Kate Cox learned at 20 weeks gestation that her fetus had Trisomy 18—a severe chromosomal anomaly that is almost always fatal at or shortly after birth.
  • Continuing the pregnancy threatened Cox’s health and future fertility, requiring a risky third C-section.
  • Because Texas’s total abortion ban had extremely narrow exceptions, Cox’s physician petitioned the courts for permission to perform the termination.
  • The Texas Supreme Court denied the request, ruling that her condition was not yet life-threatening enough to meet the statutory exception.
  • In Case Study 4, we will analyze this landmark case, exploring the direct collision of bedside clinical reality and restrictive state policy.
