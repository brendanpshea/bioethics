A Patient at Twenty-Two Weeks

A Patient at Twenty-Two Weeks

A 22-week-pregnant woman is admitted to the ICU with severe bacterial pneumonia. She is awake and oriented. The team recommends placing her on a ventilator and starting IV antibiotics; without the ventilator, she will likely die within seventy-two hours. She refuses, citing fetal effects of the sedation, and says she will take her chances.

Write or discuss:

  1. Is her refusal morally identical to the refusal of a non-pregnant patient in the same clinical state?
  2. Does the answer change if she becomes briefly delirious from oxygen deprivation overnight?
  3. If the team disagrees with her decision, what — short of going to court — are the team’s legitimate options?

Don’t formalize. Let students react. We’ll come back to the legal contours of cases like this when we discuss In re A.C. and Burton v. State.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

  • The standard idea of autonomy was built for one patient. Pregnancy is the first case where some say there is more than one — and even that claim is disputed.
  • The instinct to override a refusing pregnant patient has a long, bad history. It often hurts both the patient and the future baby the override was meant to protect.
  • Today’s job is to learn a framework sharp enough to tell three kinds of refusal apart: a refusal we should accept, a refusal we should keep trying to talk through, and a refusal the law may override.

Today

By the end of Part A, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish liberal and relational autonomy as two complementary models of what it means to “make one’s own decision,” and say which model fits which kind of case.
  • State Mill’s harm principle, identify its limits, and apply a standard three-question test for justified paternalism to a contemporary drug-policy case.
  • Explain why some drug bans have backfired while some forms of soft paternalism (taxes, age limits, harm-reduction services) have worked.
  • Describe what Ferguson v. City of Charleston (2001) and In re A.C. (1990) held, and what each refused to decide.
  • Explain how mandatory reporting, child-protective services, and the federal Plan of Safe Care apply when a newborn has been exposed to substances in utero.
  • Explain why pregnant women were historically excluded from clinical trials, and why the 2018 PRGLAC report reframed that exclusion as itself a harm.

Two Models of Autonomy

“Making Your Own Decision” Is Vague

Almost everyone in bioethics says they respect autonomy. The word covers more ground than people notice.

  • A cancer patient declining chemo to spend her remaining months at home.
  • A teenager being pressured by family into an unwanted abortion.
  • A homeless patient asked to consent to a research trial while waiting for a bed.
  • A pregnant woman refusing a recommended cesarean because she fears surgery.

All four involve “the person deciding for herself.” None of them are the same kind of case. Two models of autonomy — liberal and relational — sharpen what we are protecting in each.

Liberal Autonomy

The standard model rests on a few claims that are easy to state and hard to shake.

  • The patient is the author of her own care.
  • Valid informed consent has four parts: disclosure, understanding, voluntariness, and capacity.
  • A competent adult may refuse the treatment her doctors recommend, for any reason or no reason.
  • When a patient refuses, the clinician’s job is to persuade, not to coerce.

This model has done huge work since Nuremberg. It is the right lens when the worry is interference — somebody pushing against the patient’s own choice.

Relational Autonomy

Relational autonomy is the view, most linked to Susan Sherwin, that real autonomy is shaped through our relationships, social position, and material life — not in spite of them. (Sherwin 1992)

The view is not anti-autonomy. It is a fuller picture of what autonomy is.

  • A choice made under crushing pressure is still a “choice” in the liberal sense. But calling it free, without further comment, misses something.
  • The clinician’s job runs past the consent form. It also means tending to the conditions under which the patient is choosing.

Relational autonomy is the right lens when the worry is constraint — when background conditions narrow what choices are even on the table.

Two Models, Two Jobs

Liberal autonomy Relational autonomy
The self Bounded, prior to relationships Constituted through relationships
The threat it watches for Interference by an identifiable agent Constraint from background conditions
What it tells the clinician Document consent and step back Attend to the conditions of choice
Where it shines Refusal of unwanted treatment; the cancer patient choosing hospice Choices shaped by poverty, racism, coercive partners, addiction
Failure mode if used alone Treats coerced consent as real consent Slides toward paternalism by second-guessing choices

The competent clinician carries both and switches between them.

Why Pregnancy Strains Either Model Alone

Pregnancy is the paradigm case where each lens shows something the other misses.

What the liberal model is good at:

  • Refusal of a cesarean by a competent adult is a refusal — not a special case.
  • The body in front of you is one patient’s body, and her body is not a public resource.
  • The professional consensus (A.C., ACOG 664) is built on this lens.

What the relational model is good at:

  • A 19-year-old in active addiction, with no childcare and an abusive partner, “consenting” to an opioid-free birth plan is not, by itself, evidence the plan is freely chosen.
  • Most US pregnancies happen to women whose economic and social circumstances do real work shaping what a “free” choice looks like.
  • Harm reduction (next section) is the relational lens applied to policy.

Paternalism — A Primer

Paternalism, Defined

Paternalism is acting on someone’s behalf, against their stated wishes, for what you take to be their own good. The metaphor is exact: a parent overriding a child for the child’s sake.

Almost no one thinks all paternalism is wrong. We accept it toward small children, toward people in psychotic crisis, toward an unconscious patient in the ED. The hard question is where it stops.

The framework below is not just about pregnancy. It is what we need before the pregnancy cases can be judged fairly.

Mill’s Harm Principle

“The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant.… Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign.”

— John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859), ch. 1

(Mill 1863)

Mill’s Limits — In His Own Words

The harm principle is not the all-purpose shield it is sometimes treated as. In the same chapter, Mill carved out exceptions.

Mill himself excluded:

  • Children — “those who are still in a state to require being taken care of by others.”
  • People who cannot benefit from “free and equal discussion.”
  • “Backward states of society” — a 19th-century blind spot the modern reader should mark and reject.

What this means for us:

Liberal autonomy is a position with edges, not a default. The case for non-interference rests on working assumptions: that the person is competent, choosing freely, and well-informed. Those are exactly the assumptions that fail in the cases relational autonomy is built to catch.

Soft vs. Hard Paternalism

Soft paternalism Hard paternalism
What it overrides Choices that are not fully voluntary — made under coercion, ignorance, or incapacity Choices that are fully voluntary, competent, and informed
Example Detaining a delirious post-op patient who tries to leave the hospital pulling out her IV Banning the sale of cigarettes to a competent adult who has read every warning label
Mill’s view Permitted — not a real violation of autonomy, because the person isn’t really choosing Not permitted — overriding sovereignty
Where it gets hard Determining when a choice is “not voluntary enough” Almost every legitimate public-health intervention sits in this zone

The Standard Test for Justified Paternalism

Three questions, taken together, set a strong default against stepping in.

  1. Is the person competent — able to understand and weigh the facts?
  2. Is the choice voluntary — free from pressure, manipulation, or false information?
  3. Does the harm fall on the chooser alone, or also on others?

If the answers are yes, yes, and chooser alone, the liberal answer is: leave the person be. Most real cases turn on honest disagreement about each question.

When Bans Fail and Soft Paternalism Works

Prohibition, 1920–1933

The Eighteenth Amendment (1919) banned making, selling, and shipping alcohol in the US. The Volstead Act gave it teeth. It was repealed in 1933 — the only constitutional amendment ever fully overturned.

In between, drinking fell briefly and then climbed back. Illegal liquor became a major industry. Federal prison populations roughly tripled. The murder rate hit a peak it would not match for sixty years.

Three lessons that recur in modern drug policy:

  • Banning a widely-used substance does not erase the demand for it.
  • The black market that replaces a legal market is more violent.
  • Selective enforcement falls hardest on the communities with the least power to push back.

What Has Worked: The Soft-Paternalism Ledger

Bans are not the only tool. Some of the biggest public-health wins of the past sixty years used softer tools — and worked.

  • Tobacco taxes. US adult smoking fell from ~42% in 1965 to ~11% in 2022. About half of that drop is due to price (taxes). The rest comes from ad limits, age limits, and indoor-smoking bans. None of it required a ban on tobacco itself.
  • The drinking age. When the national age was raised to 21 in 1984, alcohol-related traffic deaths among 18–20 year olds fell by about a third over the next decade.
  • Seat belts and motorcycle helmets. These are rules for how you drive, not bans on driving. They save tens of thousands of lives a year.
  • Naloxone (Narcan) distribution. No ban — just a widely shared antidote. It is the single step with the clearest effect on the modern opioid death rate.

The pattern: changing the price, availability, age of access, or downstream harm of a behavior can shift outcomes without ever charging the user.

The numbers do not settle the policy question on their own. They do force one question: after fifty years and forty billion dollars a year, what does the policy claim to have done?

Oregon Measure 110 — The Decriminalization Experiment

2020. Oregon voters passed Measure 110. It made possession of all drugs — including heroin, meth, and fentanyl — a $100 ticket rather than a crime. Cannabis-tax money was sent to treatment.

2021–23. Few people went into treatment. The ticket system was almost never enforced. Fentanyl arrived in volume in the same years. Overdose deaths rose sharply. Street drug use became more visible. Public support for the law fell apart.

2024. The Oregon legislature passed HB 4002. It repealed Measure 110 and made possession a misdemeanor again — but with a treatment-track built in.

The honest reading. Decriminalizing drugs without paying for real treatment is not “the relational autonomy answer.” It is a half-built bridge. Portugal — the original model — paired decriminalization with big investments in treatment and outreach.

Marijuana Legalization — A Live Ledger

What looks fine:

  • No clear spike in teen use in most legal states.
  • Race gaps in marijuana arrests have fallen sharply.
  • Tax revenue is large, and some of it pays for treatment and schools.

What is real and uncomfortable:

  • Daily and near-daily adult use has risen. In some surveys it now beats daily drinking.
  • ER visits for cannabis-induced vomiting and for cannabis-induced psychosis have climbed in legal states.
  • Today’s cannabis flower averages ~20% THC, vs. ~4% in the 1990s. Concentrates run far higher.

Wide legalization may still be the right call. It is not the cost-free reform some of its early backers suggested.

Harm Reduction as a Third Frame

Harm reduction accepts that some people will keep using drugs. It tries to cut the medical and social damage — without making sobriety a condition for help.

Concrete examples:

  • Naloxone (Narcan) distribution to opioid users and those around them.
  • Syringe-services programs reducing HIV and hepatitis C transmission.
  • Methadone and buprenorphine for opioid use disorder during pregnancy.
  • Supervised consumption sites (legal in Canada; piloted in NYC since 2021).

The philosophical signature:

Harm reduction does not need to settle whether drug use is morally wrong. It asks a different question: what step, in this person, this week, cuts harm the most?

That is soft paternalism in public-health clothes — and relational autonomy turned into policy.

Thought Question — Should the State Ban Tobacco?

Tobacco kills about 480,000 Americans a year — more than all illegal drugs combined. It is sold legally to anyone over twenty-one. The FDA has the power to cut nicotine to non-addictive levels. It has proposed doing so, but has not done it.

Write or discuss.

  1. Apply the three-question test (competent? voluntary? harm to chooser alone?) to a thirty-year-old smoker.
  2. What does secondhand smoke do to the third question, and what does that commit you to about other secondhand harms?
  3. If you would support a steep federal tobacco tax but not a ban, what is doing the work in your view — and would that same reasoning apply to alcohol? To methamphetamine?

Make It Contemporary

Three live policies that turn on these questions:

  • The FDA’s proposed menthol cigarette ban, delayed in 2024 over fears of unequal enforcement.
  • State and federal limits on flavored e-cigarettes, aimed at teen uptake.
  • Australia’s prescription-only nicotine rule for vaping, in force since 2024.

What each is doing under the hood:

  • Menthol is about secondhand harms — uptake patterns in communities with fewer choices.
  • Vape limits are about competence — the choice is made by teens whose ability to choose freely is in dispute.
  • Australia is soft paternalism dressed up as a clinical pathway. You can have it. You just have to go through a doctor.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction worth carrying into the pregnancy block.

Deterrence-of-care names a failure mode. An enforcement policy can drive the people it claims to protect away from the very system that was meant to help them.

When tobacco rules target the user, smokers do not stop smoking. They hide it from their doctor. When drug enforcement reaches into pregnancy, women do not stop using. They skip prenatal visits. The next forty minutes is about how that pattern plays out when the patient is pregnant.

Substance Use During Pregnancy

The Question, Stated Sharply

May the state criminalize, in a pregnant woman, conduct that — were she not pregnant — would be unprosecutable?

That is the question. Every case in this section turns on it. Notice that it takes no position on the moral status of the fetus. It asks instead about the enforcement practice and what it does to the people it touches.

Ferguson v. City of Charleston (2001) — Facts

In the late 1980s, the Medical University of South Carolina in Charleston started a secret urine drug-testing program on some pregnant patients in its prenatal clinic. Positive results were sent to police. Hospital staff wrote the rules for who got tested; the local prosecutor signed off. Triggers included things like “no prenatal care.”

Ten of the eleven women arrested under the program were Black. The eleventh was a white woman whose chart noted that she “lives with her boyfriend, who is a Negro.”

The legal question:

Were these searches Fourth Amendment searches that needed patient consent? Or were they “special-needs” searches a hospital may do without consent, because the main goal is public health rather than law enforcement?

The Supreme Court decided in 2001.

Ferguson — Holding

Important

Held, 6–3 (Stevens, J.): When the main goal of the searches was to gather evidence for police, the special-needs exception did not apply. The secret drug-testing program violated the Fourth Amendment.

What the Court did not decide: Whether drug testing tied to treatment referral — with no police — would be allowed. Whether the prosecutions themselves were lawful (a separate state-law question). Whether civil steps short of arrest (custody removal, civil commitment) raised the same concern.

(Supreme Court of the United States 2001)

Tennessee’s Fetal Assault Law — Rise and Fall

2014. Tennessee enacts Public Chapter 820, making it a misdemeanor for a woman to take a narcotic during pregnancy “if her child is born addicted to or harmed by the narcotic.”

2014–16. About one hundred women are arrested. Most cases are in poor, rural counties. Drug-use rates in pregnancy do not fall. Women report avoiding prenatal care. Some give birth at home, alone, to avoid the hospital’s required drug test.

2016. The law is allowed to sunset. The legislature does not renew it.

What the data showed. Faherty et al. (2019) looked at state-level data across the country. States with punitive policies on substance use in pregnancy had higher rates of newborn drug withdrawal — the very outcome the laws were sold as preventing.

The deterrence-of-care idea from earlier in the lecture has a body count.

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) — also called newborn opioid withdrawal — is the set of symptoms a baby exposed to opioids in the womb shows after birth: tremors, feeding problems, fussiness, sometimes seizures. It is treatable, and most babies do well with the right care. It is also the diagnosis punitive reporting laws are sold as cutting down.

(Faherty et al. 2019)

The Scale

Paltrow and Flavin counted 1,396 arrests and forced interventions on pregnant women in the US between 1973 and 2005. Their 2013 study in JHPPL is the landmark count. Pregnancy Justice has kept tracking. The pace has sped up since Dobbs.

Of the cases they documented:

  • 71% of the women were low-income.
  • 59% were women of color.
  • The most common charge was drug-related. The second was refusing care.

Dorothy Roberts’s Killing the Black Body (1997) is the classic account of why these patterns track race and class.

Read this number carefully.

1,396 is not the number of pregnant women who used drugs. It is the number who were charged, locked up, or forced to undergo a medical procedure by the state.

(Paltrow and Flavin 2013; Roberts 1997)

Brittney Poolaw, Oklahoma, 2021

In October 2021, a Comanche County jury found Brittney Poolaw, twenty-one, guilty of first-degree manslaughter for a pregnancy loss at about seventeen weeks. The medical examiner could not find a cause. Trace amounts of methamphetamine were found in fetal tissue. Even the state’s own toxicologist testified the levels were not clearly the cause. About one in five pregnancies at this stage is lost on its own. Poolaw was sentenced to four years.

— Pregnancy Justice case summary; State v. Poolaw, Comanche Co. CF-2020-114

The conviction was affirmed on appeal in 2023.

ACOG’s Position — And Its Empirical Backing

ACOG Committee Opinion 473 / 664:

  • Substance use in pregnancy is “a medical condition that should be managed with the patient.”
  • Criminal charges and forced detention are “not effective in reducing the incidence of alcohol or drug abuse” and are “contrary to the welfare of the mother and the fetus.”
  • Drug testing without consent is rejected. Reporting to child-protective services is “controversial” and depends on state law.

Faherty et al. (2019), JAMA Network Open:

A state-level study of 4.5 million births. States with punitive policies (criminal charges, civil commitment, or reporting as abuse) had a 27% higher rate of newborn withdrawal than states without.

ACOG’s ethical claim and the population data point the same way. That kind of agreement is rare enough to matter.

(American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2011, 2016; Faherty et al. 2019)

Mandatory Reporting, CPS, and Plan of Safe Care

The fight about criminal charges can hide a more common question that comes up in actual delivery rooms. When a baby has been exposed to drugs in the womb — legal or illegal — what is supposed to happen next?

CAPTA and the Plan of Safe Care. The federal Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA) was updated in 2016 and 2018. It tells states to set up a system in which every baby found to be substance-exposed at birth is referred to child-protective services. The family must also get a written Plan of Safe Care that addresses the health and recovery needs of the baby, the mother, and the household.

CAPTA says clearly that a CAPTA referral is not a finding of child abuse. The goal is to connect families to services, not to take the child away. In practice, states do this very unevenly.

What That Looks Like, Across States

State approach What gets reported What gets called abuse/neglect Typical outcome
Service-led (e.g., Vermont, Washington) All substance-exposed infants → home-visiting and treatment services Only if other risk factors present Most infants stay with parent
Investigative (most states) Positive newborn toxicology → CPS investigation State-by-state; some treat exposure alone as neglect Wide variation; removal rates track race
Punitive (Alabama, parts of TN/WI) Positive newborn toxicology → CPS and law enforcement Exposure can itself ground prosecution Removal is common; criminal charges possible

The clinician’s job is the same across all three columns: identify, document, and connect the family with services. The legal system that takes over from there is not in the clinician’s hands. But the clinician should be honest with patients about what to expect.

Thought Question — A Patient on Methamphetamine

A 32-week-pregnant patient comes to a community hospital in early labor. Her urine tests positive for methamphetamine. She has had three prenatal visits, no other problems, and tells the nurse she used “yesterday, maybe the day before.” State law requires the hospital to report drug exposure to CPS. The hospital can choose whether to involve police.

Write or discuss.

  1. The patient asks what happens next. What does the care team owe her in honest information? Is anything fair to hold back until after delivery?
  2. The attending asks whether to call CPS now or after delivery. What is the right answer, and why?
  3. Does your answer change if the drug is alcohol? Fentanyl? Cannabis that is legal in this state? Be honest about why.

Make It Contemporary

Three live trends, 2022–2025:

  • Pregnancy Justice reports a sharp jump in arrests for pregnancy outcomes since Dobbs — from about fifty a year nationally to more than two hundred in 2023.
  • Since 2022, at least nine states have introduced fetal-personhood bills that would widen the legal basis for charges like Poolaw’s.
  • The Alabama Supreme Court’s LePage ruling (2024) called frozen embryos “extrauterine children.” We will look at it in detail in Part B. It is the most aggressive recent move in this direction.

What unifies these:

Each treats the fetus or embryo as a legal subject with claims that limit what the pregnant person (or the patient seeking IVF) may do.

The medical position has not changed. The legal world it works in has changed sharply.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction the body of the lecture has been circling.

When a pregnant patient and her team disagree, there are three kinds of disagreement. Each calls for a different response.

  • About the facts. (“That medicine will harm my fetus.” It won’t, or it will less than the disease itself.) The remedy is better information.
  • About the values. (“Even if the medicine is safer, I want to take my chances.”) The remedy is respect for refusal.
  • About whose interests count. (“Your refusal will harm your fetus, who cannot speak.”) This is where the lawsuits live. And here ACOG’s modern position lands: fetal interests exist, but forcing the patient is almost never the right response to them.

Refusal of Recommended Obstetric Care

When the Doctor Says Yes and the Patient Says No

Three kinds of refusal cases recur. They look similar but are different in ethics and law.

  • Refusal of cesarean. The clinician thinks a surgical delivery is needed. The patient prefers a vaginal delivery, or no delivery at all.
  • Refusal of blood transfusion. Most often a Jehovah’s Witness with heavy bleeding around delivery. The standard model honors the refusal. Pregnancy has long been used as a reason to override it.
  • Refusal of bed-rest or hospital stay for a high-risk pregnancy. The burdens fall almost all on the patient. The benefits are statistical and indirect.

The starting case for all three is In re A.C. (1990).

In re A.C. (1990) — Angela Carder

Angela Carder was twenty-seven, twenty-six weeks pregnant, and dying of widespread cancer when she was admitted to George Washington University Hospital in 1987. Her cancer doctor had treated her since she was a teenager. The agreed plan was comfort care.

Hospital leaders asked a court for an order to perform a cesarean against her will, on the grounds that the fetus might survive. The hearing was held in the hospital hallway. The order was granted. The cesarean was performed. The infant lived two hours. Carder died two days later. Her death certificate listed the cesarean as a contributing cause.

The appeals court’s job was not to say whether the trial judge had been merciful or cruel.

It was to decide whether the test the judge had used would stand. That test balanced the patient’s right to refuse against the state’s interest in potential life.

The DC Court of Appeals, sitting en banc, gave its answer about two and a half years later.

(District of Columbia Court of Appeals 1990)

The 1990 Appellate Repudiation

Important

Held (DC Court of Appeals, en banc, April 1990): “In virtually all cases the question of what is to be done is to be decided by the patient — the pregnant woman — on behalf of herself and the fetus. The trial court erred in subordinating A.C.’s right of bodily integrity in favor of the state’s interest in potential life.”

The court did not decide what should happen in a different case — say, a patient who was not competent and a fetus that was clearly viable. But it set the bar for that case very high. In the thirty-five years since, no American appeals court has upheld a similar order.

ACOG Committee Opinion 664 — The Modern Consensus

“Pregnancy is not an exception to the principle that a decisionally capable patient has the right to refuse treatment, even treatment needed to maintain life. … Coercive and punitive legal approaches to pregnant women who refuse medical advice fail to recognize that all competent adults are entitled to informed consent and bodily integrity.”

— ACOG, Committee Opinion 664 (2016, reaffirmed 2022) (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2016)

Why it works as a primary source.

It is short. Most students can read it in fifteen minutes. It is the cleanest single statement of where the medical profession lands today. The A.C. court’s holding maps onto it almost line for line.

Burton v. State (Florida, 2010) reached the same result for forced hospital stays. It threw out an order that had forced a woman with ruptured membranes to stay in the hospital against her will. (District Court of Appeal of Florida, First District 2010)

Thought Question — A Refused Cesarean

A patient near her due date has had one prior cesarean. Her obstetrician now recommends a repeat cesarean. He gives two reasons: the baby is bigger than average, and the fetal heart monitor has looked worrying for the last forty minutes. The patient — competent, alert, and afraid of surgery — refuses. She asks to keep laboring.

The obstetrician turns to the nurse and says: “Help her understand.”

Write or discuss.

  1. What does the care team owe the patient in the next ten minutes? What would not be okay to do, even with the obstetrician’s push?
  2. When would it be right to call a hospital ethics committee? When would it be right to go to court?
  3. Apply both models of autonomy to the case. Does either lens push you toward a different answer?

Brain Death and Pregnancy

Marlise Muñoz, Fort Worth, 2013–14

On November 26, 2013, Marlise Muñoz collapsed in her kitchen from a blood clot in her lungs. She was 33 and fourteen weeks pregnant. Paramedics restarted her heart. By the next morning, she met the criteria for whole-brain death. Her entire brain, including the brainstem, had stopped working.

Her husband Erick — a paramedic — and her parents asked the hospital to stop life support. They said this matched her stated wishes. The hospital refused, pointing to the Texas Advance Directives Act. The family sued. In January 2014, a trial court ordered the hospital to disconnect support. By then the fetus had been growing for ten more weeks. Imaging showed severe abnormalities.

The case has been re-litigated, in one form or another, in twelve states since.

Texas Health and Safety Code § 166.049:

“A person may not withdraw or withhold life-sustaining treatment under this subchapter from a pregnant patient.”

No exception for brain-dead patients. No exception for non-viable pregnancies. No way for an advance directive to override it. About a dozen US states have similar wording as of 2025.

The Adriana Smith case (Atlanta, 2025) is the latest version. A brain-dead pregnant woman was kept on body support for four months under a disputed reading of Georgia’s LIFE Act.

Research Exclusion — The Other Side of Vulnerability

The Other Side of “Vulnerability”

The same word that for fifty years protected pregnant women from risky trials has, for fifty years, kept them out of the evidence base for the drugs they take.

Earlier in the course, “vulnerability” was a reason to be careful. It has a second face. Keeping a group out can itself be a harm, when it leaves them without good data on their own care. The next three slides walk through that second face.

Why Pregnant Women Were Excluded

Period What happened What it triggered
1957–62 Thalidomide. Prescribed for morning sickness in Europe and (off-label) in the US; produced ~10,000 cases of severe limb malformation in babies International revision of drug-approval standards; FDA Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)
1940s–70s Diethylstilbestrol (DES). Prescribed to prevent miscarriage; later linked to a rare vaginal cancer in the daughters of women who took it Reinforced perception that drugs in pregnancy were uniquely dangerous
1977 FDA guidance excluding women of reproductive age from most early-phase trials, regardless of whether they were pregnant A near-total absence of pregnancy-specific safety data for the next forty years
1993 FDA reversal — women of reproductive age permitted in trials, but pregnant women remained largely excluded The current gap

Teratogenic means a drug that causes birth defects when taken during pregnancy. Thalidomide is the textbook example. Fear of another thalidomide drove the exclusion policy — and made sure almost no drug developed later had pregnancy-specific data either.

PRGLAC (2018) and the FDA’s Reframing

Important

PRGLAC Task Force, 2018 Report to Congress.

About 95% of prescription drugs in common use during pregnancy lack good pregnancy-specific safety and dosing data. The task force concluded that “protection by exclusion” had failed both pregnant women (who take the drugs anyway) and the doctors who prescribe them anyway.

The FDA’s 2018 draft guidance, issued at the same time, made including pregnant participants the default. Excluding them now needs a reason.

(Task Force on Research Specific to Pregnant Women and Lactating Women 2018)

SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine) — antidepressants ~6% of US pregnancies Observational only; no RCTs; debate ongoing about neonatal effects
Antihypertensives — blood pressure medications ~9% of US pregnancies Some have decades of use without trial data; ACE inhibitors known to cause birth defects
Anti-seizure medications ~0.5% of US pregnancies Most pregnancy data come from voluntary registries, not trials
Anticoagulants — blood thinners Common in high-risk pregnancies Dosing largely extrapolated from non-pregnant adults
COVID-19 vaccines and antivirals Recommended in pregnancy Pregnant women excluded from pivotal trials; post-marketing data only

The same justice principle that warns against wrongful exploitation — putting the burdens on pregnant women while others get the benefits — also warns against wrongful exclusion: keeping them out of the evidence base their own care depends on.

Three Faces of Pregnancy Autonomy

One question, three terrains:

  • What may the state do to a pregnant person on the grounds that her conduct affects a future person? (Substance use)
  • What may a medical team do to a pregnant patient against her stated preferences? (Refusal of care)
  • What may either do to a pregnant body when the person herself is no longer there? (Brain death)

And the inversion:

  • What may the state and the medical profession do to pregnant people by leaving them out of the evidence base?

The same word — autonomy — does different work in each terrain. The two-models framework you have today is what tells you which work it is doing in each case.

A team member asks whether to file a child-welfare report “to be safe.” Using harm reduction and the Plan of Safe Care, give one reason to file and one reason not to.

Pick one substance — alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, or fentanyl — and argue that the US is treating it wrongly. What approach should we use instead, and why?
