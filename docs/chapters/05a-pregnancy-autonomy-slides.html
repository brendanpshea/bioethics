A Patient at Twenty-Two Weeks

A Patient at Twenty-Two Weeks

A 22-week-pregnant woman is admitted to the ICU with severe bacterial pneumonia. She is awake and oriented. The team recommends intubation and IV antibiotics; without intubation, she will likely die within seventy-two hours. She refuses, citing fetal effects of the sedation, and says she will take her chances.

Write or discuss:

  1. Is her refusal morally identical to the refusal of a non-pregnant patient in the same clinical state?
  2. Does the answer change if she becomes briefly delirious from oxygen deprivation overnight?
  3. If the team disagrees with her decision, what — short of going to court — are the team’s legitimate options?

Don’t formalize. Let students react. We’ll come back to the legal contours of cases like this when we discuss In re A.C. and Burton v. State.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

  • The standard model of autonomy was built for one bounded patient. Pregnancy is the first case where some argue there is more than one — and even that framing is contested.
  • The clinical instinct to “override” a refusing pregnant patient has a long history of going badly for both the patient and the future baby the override was meant to protect.
  • Today’s job is to learn a framework precise enough to distinguish a refusal we should accept, a refusal we should keep trying to persuade against, and a refusal the law may override.

The Standard Model and Where It Strains

Liberal Autonomy: One-Slide Recap

The standard model of autonomy rests on a handful of claims that are easy to state and hard to dislodge.

  • The patient is the author of her own care.
  • Valid informed consent requires disclosure, understanding, voluntariness, and capacity.
  • A competent adult may refuse medically recommended treatment, for any reason or no reason.
  • The clinician’s job in the face of a refusal is persuasion, not coercion.

That set has done enormous work since Nuremberg. It also, in a growing list of cases, runs out.

Where the Model Works: A Single Adult Patient

A fifty-year-old man with stage IV pancreatic cancer is told he has a 40% one-year survival chance with aggressive chemotherapy. He declines; he prefers to spend his remaining time gardening. His oncologist tries twice to talk him into the protocol, then helps him enroll in hospice.

Nothing about that scene troubles anyone in the room. The standard model handles it perfectly.

What the standard model assumes:

  • One patient with one set of interests.
  • A choice that primarily affects the chooser.
  • A clinician who disagrees but defers.
  • A legal system that backs the deferral.

Where It Strains: The Pregnant Patient

What changes:

  • The body in front of you contains, on some accounts, a second locus of moral concern.
  • The choice the patient makes can — depending on the case — be argued to affect a future person who cannot yet refuse on their own behalf.
  • The clinician’s professional norms about non-maleficence may pull in two directions at once.

What does not change (according to the modern professional consensus):

  • The pregnant person remains the patient with the right to refuse.
  • Her competence is presumed unless specifically rebutted.
  • Her body is not a public resource for the protection of a future person.

The fight is over how to honor both lists at once.

Paternalism — A Primer

Paternalism, Defined

Paternalism is acting on someone’s behalf, against their stated preferences, for what one takes to be their own good. The metaphor is exact: a parent overriding a child for the child’s sake.

Almost no one thinks all paternalism is wrong. We accept it toward small children, toward people in psychotic crisis, toward an unconscious patient in the ED. The hard question is where it stops.

The framework below is not specific to pregnancy. It is what we need before the pregnancy-specific cases can be evaluated honestly.

Mill’s Harm Principle

“The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant.… Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign.”

— John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859), ch. 1

(Mill 1863)

Soft vs. Hard Paternalism

Soft Paternalism Hard Paternalism
What it overrides Choices that are not fully voluntary — made under coercion, ignorance, incapacity Choices that are fully voluntary, competent, and informed
Example Detaining a delirious post-op patient who tries to leave the hospital pulling out her IV Banning the sale of cigarettes to a competent adult who has read every warning label
Mill’s view Permitted: not a real violation of autonomy, because the person isn’t really choosing Not permitted: this is overriding sovereignty
Where it gets hard Determining when a choice is “not voluntary enough” Almost every legitimate public-health intervention sits in this zone

The Standard Test for Justified Paternalism

Three questions, taken together, license a presumption against intervention.

  1. Is the person competent — able to understand and weigh the relevant information?
  2. Is the choice voluntary — free from coercion, manipulation, or material misinformation?
  3. Does the harm fall on the chooser alone, or also on identifiable others?

If the answers are yes, yes, and chooser alone, the liberal answer is: leave the person be. Most of the real work is reasonable disagreement about each question.

Prohibition, 1920–1933

The Eighteenth Amendment (1919) prohibited the manufacture, sale, and transportation of “intoxicating liquors” in the US; the Volstead Act gave it teeth. It was repealed in 1933 — the only constitutional amendment ever fully overturned.

In between, alcohol consumption fell briefly and climbed back, illegal manufacture became a major industry, federal prison populations roughly tripled, and the homicide rate hit a peak it would not match for sixty years.

Three lessons that recur in modern drug policy:

  • Banning a widely-used substance does not erase the demand.
  • The black market that replaces a legal market is more violent than the legal one it replaced.
  • Selective enforcement falls hardest on the communities with the least political power to push back.

The War on Drugs at Fifty

The numbers do not, by themselves, settle the policy question. They do force a specific question: at fifty years and forty billion dollars a year, what does the policy claim to have achieved?

Harm Reduction as a Third Frame

Harm reduction accepts that some people will continue using substances and tries to minimize the medical and social damage — without making abstinence a precondition for help.

Concrete examples:

  • Naloxone (Narcan) distribution to opioid users and those around them.
  • Syringe-services programs reducing HIV and hepatitis C transmission.
  • Methadone and buprenorphine for opioid use disorder during pregnancy.
  • Supervised consumption sites (legal in Canada; piloted in NYC since 2021).

The philosophical signature:

Harm reduction does not require resolving whether use is morally wrong. It asks a different question — what intervention, in this person, this week, reduces harm the most? That is soft paternalism in public-health clothes.

Thought Question — Should the State Ban Tobacco?

Tobacco kills roughly 480,000 Americans a year — more than all illegal drugs combined. It is sold legally to anyone over twenty-one. The FDA has authority to cut nicotine to non-addictive levels and has proposed, but not implemented, doing so.

Write or discuss.

  1. Apply the three-question test (competent? voluntary? harm to chooser alone?) to a thirty-year-old smoker.
  2. What does secondhand smoke do to the third question, and what does that commit you to about other secondhand harms?
  3. If you would not ban tobacco, can you coherently support criminal penalties for methamphetamine? Identify the premise you are relying on.

Make It Contemporary

Three live policies that turn on these questions:

  • The FDA’s proposed menthol cigarette ban, delayed in 2024 over concerns about disparate enforcement.
  • State and federal restrictions on flavored e-cigarettes, aimed at adolescent uptake.
  • Australia’s prescription-only nicotine model for vaping, in force since 2024.

What each is doing under the hood:

  • Menthol is about secondhand harms — uptake patterns in communities with less choice.
  • Vape restrictions are about competence — the choice is made by adolescents whose voluntariness is disputed.
  • Australia is soft paternalism dressed as a clinical pathway: you can have it, you just have to go through a doctor.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction worth carrying into the pregnancy block.

Deterrence-of-care is the failure mode where an enforcement policy causes the people it claims to protect to avoid the system protection was supposed to flow through.

When tobacco enforcement targets the user, smokers do not stop smoking; they hide it from their doctor. When drug enforcement reaches into pregnancy, women do not stop using; they avoid prenatal care. The next forty minutes is about how that pattern plays out when the patient is pregnant.

Relational Autonomy

Relational Autonomy — The Critique

Relational autonomy is the view, most associated with Susan Sherwin, that the liberal model describes an abstraction rather than a real person.

Real autonomy is exercised through relationships, social positions, economic conditions, and bodies that are never fully bounded. Treating autonomy as a property of an isolated chooser misses most of what shapes a real decision.

The view is not anti-autonomy. It is a richer account of what autonomy is.

Where Relational Autonomy Changes the Clinical Move

The liberal model would ask:

  • Does the patient understand the information?
  • Has she given valid consent?
  • Has she signed?

If yes, the encounter is essentially done. The clinician has discharged the duty.

Relational autonomy adds:

  • Who is in the room with her, and who is not?
  • Is the choice she is making available to her because of the conditions of her life, or despite them?
  • What changes about her choice if the conditions change?

The encounter ends not when the form is signed but when those questions are answered honestly.

(Sherwin 1992)

Liberal vs. Relational Autonomy

Liberal Autonomy Relational Autonomy
The self Bounded, prior to relationships Constituted through relationships
The interests Fixed; the chooser brings them to the choice Responsive; partly shaped by social conditions
The threat to autonomy Coercion by an identifiable agent Background conditions that narrow what choices are even available
The clinical implication Document the consent and step back Attend to the conditions under which the consent is being given
Where it shines Refusal of unwanted treatment Cases where the choice is shaped by poverty, racism, or domestic violence

Why It Matters for Pregnancy

Pregnancy is the paradigm case where the liberal picture strains.

  • The chooser is bodily continuous, for nine months, with the entity her choice is partly about.
  • Most US pregnancies happen to women whose economic, social, and medical circumstances do real work in shaping what counts as a “free” choice.
  • Medicine has historically treated her as the gestational vehicle of a future patient — and her refusals as obstacles to be managed.

Relational autonomy does not tell us to override her. It tells us to take the conditions of her choice more seriously than the liberal model required.

Substance Use During Pregnancy

The Question, Stated Sharply

May the state criminalize, in a pregnant woman, conduct that — were she not pregnant — would be unprosecutable?

That is the question. Every case in this section turns on it. Notice that it presupposes nothing about the moral status of the fetus; it asks about the enforcement practice and what it does to the people it touches.

Ferguson v. City of Charleston (2001) — Facts

In the late 1980s, MUSC in Charleston began a program of covert urine drug screening on selected pregnant patients in its prenatal clinic. Positive results were forwarded to police. The selection criteria — written by MUSC staff and approved by the local solicitor — included markers like “no prenatal care.”

Ten of the eleven women arrested under the program were Black. The eleventh was a white woman whose chart noted that she “lives with her boyfriend, who is a Negro.”

The legal question:

Were these searches Fourth Amendment searches requiring patient consent, or “special-needs” searches that an institution may conduct without consent because their primary purpose is public-health-related rather than law-enforcement-related?

The Supreme Court decided in 2001.

Ferguson — Holding

Important

Held, 6–3 (Stevens, J.): When the immediate objective of the searches was to generate evidence for law enforcement, the special-needs exception did not apply. The covert drug-testing program violated the Fourth Amendment.

What the Court did not decide: Whether drug testing tied to treatment referral — with no police involvement — would be permissible. Whether the prosecutions themselves were lawful (a separate state-law question). Whether civil consequences short of arrest (custody removal, civil commitment) raised the same constitutional concern.

(Supreme Court of the United States 2001)

Wisconsin Act 292 — The “Cocaine Mom” Law

Wisconsin Act 292 (1997). Permits civil detention of a pregnant woman if a court finds probable cause she has used a controlled substance “to a severe degree” and is unwilling to accept treatment. She has no right to appointed counsel until forty-eight hours in. The fetus, by contrast, is appointed a guardian ad litem at the outset.

The statute remains in force. The most recent Seventh Circuit ruling (2023) sent the case back without resolving the constitutional question.

Alabama’s Chemical Endangerment Statute

Alabama’s chemical-endangerment law was enacted in 2006 to punish adults who exposed children to the toxic chemicals of clandestine meth production. Within five years, prosecutors were using it to charge pregnant women whose newborns tested positive for any controlled substance — reading the unborn child as the protected “child.”

The Alabama Supreme Court affirmed this reading in Ex parte Ankrom (2013). Prosecutions accelerated. Between 2006 and 2022, several hundred women — disproportionately poor and rural — were convicted, with sentences from probation to ten years.

The statute is identical in form to the one used against meth-lab operators. Its application to pregnant women was a prosecutorial choice, not a legislative one.

Tennessee’s Fetal Assault Law — Rise and Fall

2014. Tennessee enacts Public Chapter 820, making it a Class A misdemeanor for a woman to take a narcotic during pregnancy “if her child is born addicted to or harmed by the narcotic.”

2014–16. Roughly one hundred women are arrested. Concentration is in poor, rural counties. Drug-use rates in pregnancy do not decline. Women report avoiding prenatal care; some give birth at home, unattended, to escape mandatory hospital testing.

2016. The statute is allowed to sunset; the legislature declines to renew it.

The empirical aftermath. Faherty et al. (2019), examining state-level data nationwide, found that states with punitive policies toward substance use in pregnancy had higher rates of neonatal abstinence syndrome — exactly the outcome the policies were sold as preventing.

The deterrence-of-care concept from earlier in the lecture has a body count.

(Faherty et al. 2019)

The Scale

Paltrow and Flavin documented 1,396 arrests and forced interventions on pregnant women in the US between 1973 and 2005 — a landmark 2013 study in JHPPL. Pregnancy Justice has continued the tracking; the pace has accelerated post-Dobbs.

Of the documented cases:

  • 71% of the women were low-income.
  • 59% were women of color.
  • The most common charge was drug-related; the second was refusal of care.

Dorothy Roberts’s Killing the Black Body (1997) is the canonical analysis of why these patterns track race and class.

Read this number carefully.

1,396 is not the number of pregnant women who used drugs. It is the number who were prosecuted, detained, or forced into a medical intervention by the state.

(Paltrow and Flavin 2013; Roberts 1997)

Brittney Poolaw, Oklahoma, 2021

In October 2021, a Comanche County jury convicted Brittney Poolaw, twenty-one, of first-degree manslaughter for a pregnancy loss at approximately seventeen weeks. The medical examiner could not identify a cause; meth and amphetamine metabolites were detected in fetal tissue at levels the toxicologist himself testified were not clearly causal. Pregnancy loss at this stage occurs on its own in roughly twenty percent of cases. Poolaw was sentenced to four years.

— Pregnancy Justice case summary; State v. Poolaw, Comanche Co. CF-2020-114

The conviction was affirmed on appeal in 2023.

ACOG’s Position — And Its Empirical Backing

ACOG Committee Opinion 473 / 664:

  • Substance use in pregnancy is “a medical condition that should be managed in collaboration with the patient.”
  • Criminal sanctions and forced detention are “not effective in reducing the incidence of alcohol or drug abuse” and are “contrary to the welfare of the mother and the fetus.”
  • Mandatory testing without consent is rejected. Mandatory reporting to child-protective services is “controversial” and depends on state law.

Faherty et al. (2019), JAMA Network Open:

State-level analysis of 4.5 million births. States with punitive policies (criminalization, civil commitment, or mandatory reporting as abuse) had a 27% higher rate of neonatal abstinence syndrome than states without.

ACOG’s normative claim and the population-level data point the same way. That convergence is uncommon enough to matter.

(American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2011, 2016; Faherty et al. 2019)

Thought Question — A Patient on Methamphetamine

You are the L&D charge nurse on a Tuesday night. A 32-week-pregnant patient arrives in early labor. Her urine tox is positive for methamphetamine. She has had three prenatal visits, no other complications, and tells you she used “yesterday, maybe the day before.” Your state has mandatory reporting of in-utero drug exposure to CPS; the hospital has discretion about police involvement.

Write or discuss.

  1. What do you tell her about what will happen next? What do you not yet tell her?
  2. Your charge physician asks whether you think CPS should be called now or after delivery. What’s your answer, and what is it grounded in?
  3. Does your answer change if the substance is alcohol? If it is fentanyl? If it is cannabis legal in your state? Be honest about why.

Make It Contemporary

Three live trends, 2022–2025:

  • Pregnancy Justice has documented a sharp post-Dobbs acceleration in arrests for pregnancy outcomes — from roughly fifty per year nationally to more than two hundred in 2023.
  • At least nine states have introduced fetal-personhood bills since 2022 that would expand the statutory basis for charges like Poolaw’s.
  • The Alabama Supreme Court’s LePage ruling (2024) declaring frozen embryos to be “extrauterine children” — discussed at length in Part B — is the most aggressive recent extension of the same legal logic.

What unifies these:

Each treats the fetus or embryo as a legal subject with claims that constrain what the pregnant person (or the patient seeking IVF) may do.

The professional medical position has not changed. The legal environment in which it operates has changed sharply.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction the body of the lecture has been circling.

When a pregnant patient and her team disagree, there are three different kinds of disagreement that call for different responses:

  • About the facts. (“That medication will harm my fetus.” It won’t, or it will less than the disease.) The remedy is persuasion and better information.
  • About the values. (“Even though the medication is safer, I want to take my chances.”) The remedy is respect for refusal.
  • About whose interests count. (“Your refusal will harm your fetus, who cannot speak.”) This is where the litigation lives — and where ACOG’s modern position lands: fetal interests exist, but coercion of the patient is almost never the right response to them.

Refusal of Recommended Obstetric Care

When the Doctor Says Yes and the Patient Says No

Three families of cases recur. They look superficially similar and are ethically and legally different.

  • Refusal of cesarean. The clinician believes a section is indicated; the patient prefers vaginal delivery, or no delivery at all.
  • Refusal of transfusion. Most commonly a Jehovah’s Witness with peripartum hemorrhage; the standard model honors the refusal; pregnancy has historically been used to override it.
  • Refusal of management for ruptured membranes or previa. Bedrest, hospitalization, cerclage — burdens almost entirely on the patient, benefits statistical and indirect.

The foundational case for all three is In re A.C. (1990).

In re A.C. (1990) — Angela Carder

Angela Carder was twenty-seven, twenty-six weeks pregnant, and dying of metastatic cancer when she was admitted to George Washington University Hospital in 1987. Her oncologist had treated her since adolescence. The agreed plan was palliation.

Hospital administrators sought a court order for a non-consensual cesarean, on the grounds that the fetus might be viable. The hearing was convened in the hospital corridor. The order was granted. The cesarean was performed. The infant lived two hours. Carder died two days later. The cesarean was listed on her death certificate as a contributing cause.

The case the appellate court eventually decided was not whether the trial judge had been merciful or cruel.

It was whether the framework the judge had used — balancing the patient’s right to refuse against the state’s interest in potential life — was a framework the law would tolerate going forward.

The DC Court of Appeals, sitting en banc, answered that question almost two and a half years later.

(District of Columbia Court of Appeals 1990)

The 1990 Appellate Repudiation

Important

Held (DC Court of Appeals, en banc, April 1990): “In virtually all cases the question of what is to be done is to be decided by the patient — the pregnant woman — on behalf of herself and the fetus. The trial court erred in subordinating A.C.’s right of bodily integrity in favor of the state’s interest in potential life.”

The court did not decide what should happen in a hypothetical case where a pregnant patient was incompetent and the fetus highly viable — but it set the bar for that case so high that, in the thirty-five years since, no American appellate court has affirmed a similar order.

ACOG Committee Opinion 664

“Pregnancy is not an exception to the principle that a decisionally capable patient has the right to refuse treatment, even treatment needed to maintain life. … Coercive and punitive legal approaches to pregnant women who refuse medical advice fail to recognize that all competent adults are entitled to informed consent and bodily integrity.”

— ACOG, Committee Opinion 664 (2016, reaffirmed 2022) (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2016)

The opinion has the additional pedagogical virtue of being short. Most students can read it in fifteen minutes; it is the cleanest single statement of the modern professional consensus.

Burton v. State (Florida, 2009–2010)

If A.C. feels like a 1980s artifact, Samantha Burton shows it is not.

In 2009, Burton was twenty-five weeks pregnant when her membranes ruptured. Her physician at Tallahassee Memorial recommended hospitalization and bedrest. Burton — two other children at home, no childcare, a job she could not afford to lose — asked to be discharged for a second opinion. The hospital obtained a court order by telephone compelling her to remain. She stayed three days against her will; her fetus was delivered stillborn by emergency cesarean.

The Florida District Court of Appeal vacated the order in 2010, holding that even the state’s interest in potential life “cannot override Burton’s right to make her own medical treatment decisions.” (District Court of Appeal of Florida, First District 2010)

Thought Question — A Refused Cesarean at Thirty-Eight Weeks

You are the labor nurse. The obstetrician has just asked you to “help her understand.”

Brain Death and Pregnancy

Marlise Muñoz, Fort Worth, 2013–14

On November 26, 2013, Marlise Muñoz collapsed in her kitchen from what was later determined to be a pulmonary embolism. She was 33 and fourteen weeks pregnant. Paramedics restored cardiac activity en route to John Peter Smith Hospital. By morning, she met criteria for whole-brain death.

Her husband Erick — a paramedic — and her parents requested withdrawal of support, consistent with her previously expressed wishes.

The hospital refused, citing the Texas Advance Directives Act.

The family filed suit in January 2014. The trial court ordered the hospital to remove Muñoz from mechanical support. The hospital complied on January 26, 2014.

By that point, the fetus had been gestating for approximately ten additional weeks. Imaging had shown lower-extremity malformations, hydrocephalus, and a heart abnormality.

The case has been litigated, in one form or another, in twelve states since.

Lindemann Nelson on Postmortem Pregnancy

“The woman, in this kind of case, no longer exists as a person of the sort who can have agent-relative duties toward her fetus. To speak of her body as a ‘maternal environment’ for the fetus is to grant the fetus a claim to her tissues that we would grant to no other party — including her existing children, her parents, or a stranger in need of an organ. We do not, in any other context, conscript the dead.”

— Hilde Lindemann Nelson, Bioethics (1994) (Lindemann Nelson 1994)

The point is sharp and worth thinking with. Texas’s statute does not, on its face, treat the brain-dead pregnant woman as conscripted. But what else, in substance, would the conscription look like?

Texas Advance Directives Act § 166.049

Important

Texas Health and Safety Code § 166.049:

“A person may not withdraw or withhold life-sustaining treatment under this subchapter from a pregnant patient.”

No carve-out for brain-dead patients, no carve-out for non-viable pregnancies, no mechanism for an advance directive to override it. Roughly a dozen US states have comparable statutory language as of 2025.

A 2014 effort to amend the statute failed in committee; a 2023 effort failed for similar reasons.

Research Exclusion — The Other Side of Vulnerability

The Other Side of “Vulnerability”

The same word that for fifty years protected pregnant women from risky trials has, for fifty years, kept them out of the evidence base for medicine they take.

Earlier in the course, “vulnerability” appeared as a reason to be careful. It has a second face. Exclusion is a form of harm when it leaves a class of patients without an evidence base for their care. The next three slides walk that second face.

Why Pregnant Women Were Excluded

Period What happened What it triggered
1957–62 Thalidomide. Prescribed for morning sickness in Europe and (off-label) in the US; produced ~10,000 cases of severe limb malformation in babies International revision of drug-approval standards; FDA Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)
1940s–70s Diethylstilbestrol (DES). Prescribed to prevent miscarriage; later linked to a rare vaginal cancer in the daughters of women who took it Reinforced perception that drugs in pregnancy were uniquely dangerous
1977 FDA guidance excluding women of reproductive age from most early-phase trials, regardless of whether they were pregnant A near-total absence of pregnancy-specific safety data for the next forty years
1993 FDA reversal — women of reproductive age permitted in trials, but pregnant women remained largely excluded The current gap

PRGLAC (2018) and the FDA’s Reframing

Important

PRGLAC Task Force, 2018 Report to Congress.

Of prescription medications in common use during pregnancy, roughly 95% lack adequate pregnancy-specific safety and dosing data. The task force concluded that “protection by exclusion” had failed both pregnant women (who receive the medications anyway) and the profession (which prescribes them anyway).

The FDA’s 2018 draft guidance, issued contemporaneously, made inclusion of pregnant participants the default, with exclusion requiring justification.

(Task Force on Research Specific to Pregnant Women and Lactating Women 2018)

What the Evidence Gap Looks Like in Practice

Drug class Pregnancy use Pregnancy-specific safety/dosing data
SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine) ~6% of US pregnancies Observational only; no RCTs; debate ongoing about neonatal effects
Antihypertensives ~9% of US pregnancies (chronic + gestational) Labetalol and nifedipine have decades of use without trial data; ACE inhibitors known to be teratogenic
Anti-seizure medications ~0.5% of US pregnancies Most pregnancy data come from voluntary registries, not trials
Heparin / anticoagulants Common in high-risk pregnancies Dosing largely extrapolated from non-pregnant adults
COVID-19 vaccines and antivirals Recommended in pregnancy Pregnant women excluded from pivotal trials; post-marketing data only

The same justice principle that warns against wrongful exploitation — burdens on pregnant women, benefits on others — warns equally against wrongful exclusion: keeping them out of the evidence base their own care depends on.

Three Faces of Pregnancy Autonomy

One question, three terrains:

  • What may the state do to a pregnant person on the grounds that her conduct affects a future person? (Substance use)
  • What may a medical team do to a pregnant patient against her stated preferences? (Refusal of care)
  • What may either do to a pregnant body when the person herself is no longer there? (Brain death)

And the inversion:

  • What may the state and the medical profession do to pregnant people by leaving them out of the evidence base?

The same word — autonomy — does different work in each terrain. The framework you have today is what tells you which work it is doing.

Review Questions

Write a brief answer to each, or use them as discussion prompts.

Recall

Distinguish soft from hard paternalism, and explain why a deterrence-of-care objection treats most prenatal criminal-law interventions as failures even on their own protective terms.

Apply

A 30-week pregnant patient with opioid use disorder declines residential treatment, citing care of her two other children, but agrees to outpatient buprenorphine and weekly visits. A nurse asks whether she should file a child-welfare report “to be safe.” Using harm reduction and relational autonomy, lay out the case for and against filing, and identify what concrete information about state law you would need before answering.

Debate

Pregnant people have been systematically excluded from clinical trials in the name of protecting the fetus, leaving most drugs used in pregnancy without adequate evidence in pregnant bodies. Is the “vulnerability as exclusion” status quo more protective or more harmful, and what should the default be for new trials of non-teratogenic drugs likely to be used in pregnancy?
