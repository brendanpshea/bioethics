Lecture 3 — Research Ethics

Part A: How Clinical Research Works (and Why It’s Different from Care)

Brendan Shea, PhD

2026-05-18

A Case to Open With

A Trial Offer at the Worst Time

You have metastatic cancer. Your oncologist says:

  • “The standard drug has stopped working.”
  • “There is a study you could join, but I need to be clear: it is a randomized trial.”
  • “One group gets the current standard treatment. The other gets the new drug.”
  • “I do not get to choose your group, and neither do you.”
  • “I hope it helps you, but the study is designed to answer a question, not to tailor a plan just for you.”
  1. Does this sound like care, research, or both?
  2. What would you still want to know before saying yes?
  3. If your doctor is the one recruiting you, what confusion is most likely?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three starting points matter here:

  • Research can happen inside ordinary care settings without becoming ordinary care. Same clinic, different goal.
  • Randomization is the clue. Once assignment is by chance, the clinician is no longer choosing only on the basis of what seems best for this patient.
  • Hope is not the same as a promise. Ethical recruitment does not kill hope, but it cannot quietly market the study as personalized treatment.

The opening move should make students feel how easily research can sound like an ordinary treatment recommendation. The point is not to make them anti-research; it is to make them notice the difference in purpose.

Today

By the end of Part A, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish clinical care from clinical research, and say why the difference changes consent and oversight
  • Explain randomization, control, placebo, and blinding by walking through the 1954 polio trial
  • Tell a clinical endpoint from a surrogate, and judge whether a result could be chance
  • Summarize what trial phases I–IV are for
  • Explain the therapeutic misconception and why it matters at the bedside
  • Use the opioid-evidence story to say what a given study can and cannot support

Research and Care Are Not the Same

One Crucial Difference

  • Clinical care aims to help the patient in front of you
  • Clinical research aims to produce generalizable knowledge (Emanuel et al. 2000)
  • Both may benefit a patient
  • But direct patient benefit is not what makes an activity research
  • That difference changes consent, oversight, and acceptable risk

The Same Room Can Hide the Difference

Why it feels like care

  • Same hospital
  • Same white coats
  • Same lab draws and imaging
  • Same reassuring language of “options”
  • Often the same physician

Why it is not the same

  • The protocol fixes what can happen
  • Randomization may assign the arm
  • The main goal is learning, not tailoring
  • The results are meant to guide future care
  • Independent review is required before it begins

Why Research Exists

  • Without research, medicine would freeze in place
  • New drugs, devices, and procedures need to be tested before they become standard
  • Even old treatments need comparison, follow-up, and correction
  • Ethical research is not the enemy of patient care
  • Ethical research is one reason patient care can improve at all

Research vs. Clinical Care at a Glance

Question Clinical care Clinical research
Main goal Help this patient now Produce reliable knowledge
Who should benefit first? The individual patient Future patients, and sometimes current participants
Who sets the plan? Clinician and patient Protocol, eligibility rules, and study design
What governs it? Professional standards, licensure, ordinary consent IRB review, protocol rules, research consent (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office for Human Research Protections 2026)
What counts as success? Good care for this person Valid evidence that answers the research question

The Physician-Investigator Tension

  • A clinician may also be a physician-investigator
  • That can be efficient and even helpful
  • It can also blur whether advice is being given as a doctor or as a researcher
  • Modern research ethics assumes this tension is real, not rare (World Medical Association 2024)
  • That is one reason independent review matters

Inside a Randomized Trial

Why Randomize?

  • If doctors chose the groups, healthier and sicker patients could cluster without anyone meaning to (National Cancer Institute 2024)
  • Randomization assigns people by chance
  • That helps keep the comparison scientifically fair
  • But it also means the clinician is no longer hand-picking the treatment for this patient
  • So randomization is ethically important, not just statistically useful

When Is It OK to Randomize a Sick Person?

  • The honest answer needs a concept: clinical equipoise
  • It means the expert community is genuinely unsure which arm is better
  • If we already knew, randomizing would be wrong — we’d be assigning some patients to the worse option on purpose
  • Equipoise is the moral license for a trial
  • This is why “I’d just give everyone the new drug” misses the point: we do not yet know it is better

One Map of a Parallel-Group Trial

A Real Trial: Polio, 1954

  • In 1952 the United States recorded roughly 58,000 polio cases, most of them children — it was the most feared childhood disease in the country.
  • Jonas Salk’s killed-virus injected vaccine looked promising, but “promising” vaccines had failed and even harmed children before.
  • So the country ran the largest medical experiment in history: about 1.8 million schoolchildren, the “Polio Pioneers,” organized by the March of Dimes and directed by Thomas Francis Jr.
  • The trial’s job was not to deliver a cure but to prove whether there was one.
  • Every mechanic from the last few slides — randomize, control, blind, compare — was used on these children, and the design choices decided whether anyone could believe the result (Francis et al. 1955).

Two Designs, One Trial

The 1954 trial actually ran two designs at once — and that is the lesson:

Placebo-controlled areas Observed-control areas
Who got vaccine Grades 1–3 randomized to vaccine or saline Only 2nd-grade volunteers vaccinated
The controls Randomized peers given a saline shot Grades 1 and 3, simply observed
Blinding Double-blind — no one knew who got what Not blinded
How trustworthy High — groups comparable, judgment unbiased Weaker — volunteers differ; diagnosis can be swayed

Same disease, same year, same vaccine: the design, not the vaccine, is what made one half of the trial believable.

What the Control Group Gets

  • Sometimes the control group gets current standard care
  • Sometimes it gets standard care plus a placebo
  • Sometimes, if no proven treatment exists, it may get placebo or no active treatment (National Cancer Institute 2024; World Medical Association 2024)
  • Control does not mean “ignored”
  • Everyone in the study is still monitored under a protocol

Placebo Does Not Always Mean “Nothing”

A placebo can do different jobs.

  • If no proven treatment exists, placebo may stand in for no active treatment.
  • If a proven treatment already exists, placebo is often added on top of standard care so no one is denied basic treatment.
  • The ethical question is not just “placebo or no placebo?” It is what participants are missing by being in that arm (National Cancer Institute 2024; World Medical Association 2024).

What Blinding Actually Does

  • Blinding means some people in the trial do not know which arm a participant is in
  • Single-blind usually means the participant does not know
  • Double-blind usually means the participant and the treating team or evaluators do not know
  • The point is to reduce expectation effects and biased judgment
  • “Double-blind” is about cleaner evidence, not secrecy for its own sake

Why Blinding Mattered in 1954

Diagnosing mild polio is partly a judgment call — a borderline weak limb can be read either way. In the 1954 trial’s observed-control areas, the examining doctors knew which children had been vaccinated. A doctor who hoped the vaccine worked could, without any dishonesty, lean toward “not polio” in a vaccinated child.

That is exactly why the placebo-controlled areas were double-blind: no one at the bedside knew, so hope could not tip the count.

Who Watches the Trial While It Runs?

  • A trial is not left alone until it ends
  • An independent data safety monitoring board (DSMB) reviews the data as it accumulates
  • It can stop a trial early for harm — one arm is clearly hurting people
  • It can also stop early for benefit — one arm is so clearly better that continuing the other is no longer fair
  • Equipoise has a deadline: once the evidence is in, the trial must end

The Same Machinery, Today

  • The 2020 COVID-19 vaccine trials used exactly the 1954 logic at modern scale: tens of thousands of volunteers, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled.
  • The primary endpoint was a clinical one — laboratory-confirmed symptomatic COVID-19 — not just an antibody level in the blood.
  • An independent data safety monitoring board reviewed the accumulating results at planned interim points.
  • When the effect was large and the safety data adequate, the FDA granted Emergency Use Authorization, with full approval following after more follow-up.
  • Seventy years apart, the polio and COVID trials are recognizably the same instrument, used carefully because the stakes were enormous.

Reading a Trial’s Result

What Are We Even Measuring? Endpoints

  • A clinical endpoint is something a patient can feel or would obviously care about: living longer, avoiding a stroke, walking without pain.
  • A surrogate endpoint is a stand-in measured because it is faster or cheaper — a lab number, a scan finding, a blood pressure reading.
  • Surrogates speed research up, but a treatment can move the surrogate and still not help patients — or even harm them.
  • The classic warning is the CAST trial: drugs that “fixed” an abnormal heart rhythm on the monitor actually increased deaths.
  • So the first question to ask of any trial is: did patients actually do better, or did a number just move?

Could It Just Be Chance?

  • Any two groups of people differ a little by luck, even if the treatment does nothing at all.
  • Statistics ask one question: how likely is a difference this big if the treatment really had no effect?
  • “Statistically significant” roughly means “probably not just luck” — it does not mean large, important, or certain.
  • Small trials are easily fooled by chance, which is why large samples and independent replication matter so much.
  • A single significant result is a lead worth following, not a verdict to act on alone.

Honest Trials Can Still Mislead

  • A result can mislead even when no one lied or faked anything.
  • Who got into the trial limits who the answer applies to: narrow eligibility rules can exclude the very patients you treat.
  • People who drop out are usually different from those who finish, so analyzing only the “completers” quietly flatters the treatment.
  • Intention-to-treat analysis — count everyone in the group they were randomized to, even if they quit — is the standard guard against that.
  • The disciplined question is always: who is missing from this result, and which way would that bias it?

When a Letter Becomes “Evidence”

  • In 1980 a five-sentence letter to the editor reported that, among hospitalized patients given opioids, addiction was rare (Porter and Jick 1980).
  • It was not a trial: no randomization, no control, no long-term or outpatient follow-up.
  • Over the next two decades it was cited hundreds of times as if it had proven that long-term outpatient opioids rarely cause addiction.
  • The reading-evidence lesson is exact: the type of study and who was in it cap what any result can support.
  • How that misreading was promoted is a research-integrity story we return to later — here the point is simply that a letter is not an RCT.

Thought Question

A new drug’s Phase III trial is reported as “positive.”

  • It significantly improved a surrogate marker on a scan.
  • The trial was small, and about 25% of participants dropped out.
  • The analysis included only those who completed.
  1. Name the three weaknesses you can already see.
  2. What would you want to know before telling a patient this drug “works”?
  3. Is “the FDA might approve it” the same as “it helps patients”?

Make It Contemporary

Regulators sometimes grant accelerated approval based on a surrogate endpoint, with a confirmatory trial promised later.

  • Patients with no other option get the drug years sooner.
  • Some confirmatory trials later show no real benefit — and a few are never finished.

Is faster access worth weaker evidence? Who should decide, and what should happen if the confirmatory trial fails?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three distinctions to carry forward:

  • Statistically significant ≠ clinically meaningful. A real but tiny effect on a surrogate can be true and still not worth having.
  • Regulatory approval ≠ proven patient benefit. Approval is a decision under uncertainty, not a guarantee.
  • The standing questions are always the same: what was measured, in whom, and who is missing from the result?

Trial Phases

Four Phases, Four Questions

Phase The main question Typical scale
I Is it safe? What dose can people tolerate? Small
II Does it seem to work? Larger
III Is it better than the current standard? Large, often randomized
IV What happens after approval in the real world? Very large, longer-term

One Simple Phase Map

What Each Phase Is Really Asking

  • Phase I is small (often 15–30 people in cancer) and asks what dose is tolerable and what harms appear; direct benefit is not the point, which is what makes it ethically hard (National Cancer Institute 2024).
  • Phase II enrolls more people and asks whether the treatment seems to work, while safety is still watched closely; many promising drugs fail right here.
  • Phase III is the large, usually randomized comparison against the best existing option, and it is where placebo and control debates bite hardest because its result becomes the new standard.
  • Phase IV runs after approval and catches rare harms and real-world performance that tightly controlled trials miss.
  • “Clinical trial” is therefore not one thing — the same words cover wildly different risks, hopes, and likely benefits.

Why Phases Matter Ethically

  • Students often hear “clinical trial” and imagine one thing
  • But Phase I and Phase IV do not ask the same question or offer the same likely benefits
  • Consent should match the phase, not rely on a generic script
  • Risks, hopes, and expectations shift from phase to phase
  • So the ethical explanation has to be specific

The Participant’s Problem

The Therapeutic Misconception

  • Therapeutic misconception happens when participants think the study intervention is being chosen mainly for their personal medical benefit (Appelbaum et al. 1982)
  • The misunderstanding is not just “I hope this helps”
  • It is more like “my doctor is selecting this because it is best for me”
  • That is often false once protocol rules and randomization control the options
  • A person can sign a form and still misunderstand the basic situation

Why It Happens So Easily

  • Recruitment often comes from a trusted clinician
  • Serious illness makes hope urgent
  • Research visits look like treatment visits
  • Words like “option” and “opportunity” blur categories
  • Many people hear “new” and assume “better”

A Research Consent Is Not a Treatment Consent

  • Valid consent has four elements; research adds requirements on top of them
  • It must say, in plain words, “this is research” — not treatment chosen for you
  • It must disclose randomization: a chance process, not your clinician, may pick your arm
  • It must state the goal is knowledge, and that direct benefit is uncertain or unlikely
  • It must make withdrawal at any time, without penalty, explicit
  • The nurse is often the one who notices a form was signed but not understood

The Dangerous Sentence

“This study may help you” can be true. “This study is being chosen because it is the best treatment for you” is often false.

The distinction is what the therapeutic misconception tries to protect (Appelbaum et al. 1982; Emanuel et al. 2000).

Early-Phase Trials and Thin Personal Benefit

  • Healthy volunteers and desperate patients join studies for very different reasons
  • In Phase I, researchers mainly ask what dose is tolerable and what harms appear
  • Some participants may benefit, but many will not
  • Payment, hope, and lack of alternatives can all cloud judgment
  • Ethical recruitment has to say this plainly

Thought Question

You are a research nurse enrolling patients in a first-in-human oncology trial.

  • A patient says, “If you are offering this, you must think it gives me the best chance.”
  • The oncologist who is also the principal investigator is standing beside you.
  1. What is the most important misunderstanding to correct?
  2. How blunt should you be about the low chance of direct benefit?
  3. If the patient says, “I know the odds are low, but I want to try anyway,” has the misconception been fixed?

Make It Contemporary

The same structure appears outside oncology too:

  • A family enrolls a child in a gene-therapy study because no approved treatment exists.
  • A healthy adult joins a Phase I trial mainly because the payment covers two months of rent.

Both may be voluntary. But are both equally informed and equally free?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three payoffs to insist on:

  • Hope is allowed. The goal is not to turn participants cynical; it is to keep hope from masquerading as evidence.
  • Misunderstanding is not the same as irrationality. A person may fully understand that a study is not personalized care and still choose it.
  • The hardest part is relational. When a trusted clinician recruits for a study, the burden to separate care from research falls more heavily on the professional, not less (World Medical Association 2024).

Wrapping Up Part A

The Main Distinction

  • Trials are ethically hard because they borrow the setting of care but not its single-minded goal
  • Randomization, blinding, control groups, and phases are scientific tools
  • The same tools also let you read a result: what was measured, could it be chance, who is missing
  • Those tools are exactly why research needs its own ethics
  • Part B turns from the structure of trials to the rulebook meant to protect the people inside them

Wrapping Up Part A

Clinical research is not ordinary care with extra paperwork. Its purpose is to produce reliable knowledge, which is why it uses randomization, control groups, blinding, and staged phases — visible in one real instrument, the 1954 polio trial, and again in the COVID-19 vaccine trials. Those same tools also teach you to read a study: clinical versus surrogate endpoints, whether a result could be chance, and who dropped out or was left out. The key task in Part A is learning to say, clearly and without panic: this is research, not just treatment by another name — and here is how good its evidence actually is.
