Lecture 5 — Reproductive Ethics

Part B: Making Family — Markets, Surrogacy, and Parental Rights

Author
Affiliation

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

1 A Contract in 1985

1.1 A Contract in 1985

In 1985, William Stern and Mary Beth Whitehead signed a contract through a New Jersey surrogacy broker. The contract obligated Whitehead, on the basis of a $10,000 fee, to undergo artificial insemination with Stern’s sperm, to abstain from cigarettes, alcohol, and non-prescription medication, to submit to amniocentesis on Stern’s request, to terminate the pregnancy if Stern requested it in the event of “physiological abnormality,” to deliver the child to the Sterns at birth, and to make no effort to form a parent-child relationship with the child.

Write or discuss:

  1. Whitehead signed knowingly and was paid the agreed amount. Why should the law care what she agreed to?
  2. Are the clauses about smoking, drinking, and amniocentesis morally equivalent to the clauses about parental rights, or different in kind?
  3. If the contract is wrong, is the wrong done to Whitehead, to the child, or to the rest of us?

These are the actual terms summarized in the New Jersey Supreme Court’s opinion. The case is In re Baby M, 109 N.J. 396 (1988). We will return to what the court did with the contract later in the lecture.

1.2 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three things to hold onto from that scene:

  • The same contract has been treated four different ways in four different jurisdictions. New Jersey voided it. California enforces gestational versions of it routinely. India banned it in 2015. France treats arrangements like it as criminal.
  • The disagreement is not about whether people will continue to want what the Sterns wanted. It is about what kind of arrangement the law should make available to them.
  • The lecture’s question is what to make of markets that move pieces of family life — gametes, gestation, parental rights — across the boundaries we used to assume were not market boundaries at all.

1.3 Today

By the end of Part B, you will be able to:

  • State Elizabeth Anderson’s commodification argument in standard form and identify its load-bearing premise.
  • Give the strongest version of John Robertson’s procreative-liberty reply.
  • Describe what In re Baby M (1988) and Johnson v. Calvert (1993) held, and explain the intent test for parenthood.
  • Account for the practical end of donor anonymity in the consumer-DNA era.
  • Explain what the Alabama Supreme Court held in LePage v. Mobile Infirmary (2024) and why the legislative “fix” left the underlying holding in place.

2 The Technology, in Five Slides

2.1 What IVF Actually Does

In vitro fertilization (IVF) is the process of combining egg and sperm outside the body and transferring a resulting embryo back into the uterus. A typical cycle proceeds through five steps:

1Ovarian stimulation
2Egg retrieval
3Fertilization (in dish)
4Embryo culture (3–5 days)
5Transfer or freeze

A cycle takes two to three weeks and costs (in the US, 2025) around $15,000–$25,000 before medications. About one in three transfers results in a live birth under age thirty-five; substantially fewer in older patients.

2.2 ICSI and Why It Exists

The procedure.

Intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI) injects a single sperm directly into the cytoplasm of a single egg. Introduced in 1992 to address severe male-factor infertility — sperm counts so low that conventional IVF would not work.

The drift.

By 2020, ICSI was used in roughly 70% of US IVF cycles — far beyond the male-factor indication that justified its development. Insurance does not always cover it; the clinic does not always need it; patients are rarely told the difference.

2.3 PGT — What It Tests For

Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) is performed on a few cells biopsied from an embryo before transfer. Three named variants test for different things.

What it looks for What it can tell you
PGT-A (aneuploidy) Wrong chromosome counts Embryo is likely / unlikely to implant or miscarry
PGT-M (monogenic) Single-gene disorders (Huntington’s, cystic fibrosis, Tay-Sachs) Embryo carries / does not carry the mutation tested
PGT-SR (structural) Translocations and inversions Embryo has / has not inherited the structural variant

None of these can tell you whether the resulting child will be healthy. They tell you, with caveats, about one variable at a time.

2.4 Gestational vs. Traditional Surrogacy

Traditional surrogacy. The surrogate is also the genetic mother — her egg is used. The arrangement Baby M litigated.

This form has nearly disappeared from the US market since the 1990s. The genetic connection makes the surrogate’s parental claim too strong, and contracts cannot reliably override it.

Gestational surrogacy. The surrogate gestates an embryo created from someone else’s egg and sperm. She has no genetic relationship to the child.

Roughly 98% of US surrogacy is now gestational. The legal and ethical questions diverge sharply from the traditional case; Johnson v. Calvert (1993) is the foundational ruling.

2.5 Who Is in the Room

A modern gestational surrogacy can involve up to seven distinct parties.

  • Intended parent(s) — who will raise the child.
  • Surrogate — who gestates and gives birth.
  • Egg donor — separate from the surrogate in gestational arrangements.
  • Sperm donor — sometimes separate from the intended father.
  • Surrogate’s spouse — often required as co-signatory.
  • Broker / agency — matchmaking, contract.
  • Fertility clinic — the medical infrastructure.

The number of parties is part of the ethics. Each connection used to be a single relationship; now each is a separate negotiation.

3 The Commodification Question

3.1 The Question, Stated Sharply

When reproduction enters the market, what — if anything — is being bought and sold, and what does that do to the people and the children involved?

That is the philosophical question Anderson and Robertson disagree about. The disagreement is not about whether commercial surrogacy exists — it does. The disagreement is about what kind of moral weight to give the fact that it involves an exchange of money.

3.2 Anderson’s Commodification Argument

Anderson: Commercial Surrogacy Improperly Commodifies

Elizabeth Anderson, “Is Women’s Labor a Commodity?” (1990)

  1. Some goods have their value diminished or distorted when they are produced, exchanged, or enjoyed under market norms — children and reproductive labor are among them.
  2. The proper mode of valuing a child is love (parental rights as trusts); the proper mode of valuing women’s reproductive labor is one that respects its non-fungible character.
  3. Commercial surrogacy substitutes market norms (use, disposal, fungibility) for these proper modes of valuation.
  4. Therefore, commercial surrogacy improperly commodifies both children and women’s reproductive labor.

The disputed premise is the first — that certain goods are intrinsically damaged by market exchange, rather than merely associated with markets that often go badly.

3.3 Anderson, in Her Own Words

“When women’s labor is treated as a commodity, the women who perform it are degraded. Furthermore, commercial surrogacy degrades children by reducing their status to that of commodities.”

“Commercial surrogacy substitutes market norms for some of the norms of parental love. Most importantly, it requires us to understand parental rights no longer as trusts but as things more like property rights — that is, rights of use and disposal over the things owned.”

— Elizabeth Anderson, Philosophy & Public Affairs (1990), pp. 75–76

(Anderson 1990)

3.4 Robertson’s Procreative-Liberty Reply

Robertson: Procreative Liberty Protects Surrogacy

John Robertson, Children of Choice (1994)

  1. Procreative liberty — the right to decide whether, when, and how to reproduce — is a core liberty interest deserving strong constitutional and moral protection.
  2. Surrogacy arrangements between informed, consenting adults are exercises of that liberty.
  3. The state may restrict procreative liberty only on the basis of demonstrable harm to identifiable parties — not on the basis of contested moral views about what reproduction should look like.
  4. Therefore, commercial surrogacy should be permitted, with regulation directed at preventing specific harms (fraud, coercion, harm to children) rather than at suppressing the arrangement itself.

The soft spot here is the third premise’s narrow definition of harm. Anderson would say the commodification of women and children is the harm, and Robertson has redefined the term until it cannot capture what is most contested about the practice.

3.5 Sandel’s Broader Frame

Note

Michael Sandel, What Money Can’t Buy (2012): Not every market exchange corrupts the goods it touches. Some do. Markets in friendship, in apologies, in citizenship — and, Sandel argues, in gestation — change what the good is, not just how much of it is available.

Sandel’s frame is broader than Anderson’s: it covers organ markets, kidney exchanges, congressional access, naming rights to a public building. The pattern it identifies is the same. Some social practices have meanings that cannot survive their conversion into market transactions.

(Sandel 2012)

3.6 Roberts and Reproductive Justice

The Anderson–Robertson debate, as staged, is largely about whether markets in reproduction are permissible.

A different tradition — what Dorothy Roberts and others call reproductive justice — asks a different question: whose markets, and on whose bodies? US gestational surrogates are disproportionately women in their twenties and thirties earning under $60,000 a year. International gestational surrogates were, before the 2015 bans, predominantly poor women in India, Thailand, and Nepal, paid roughly one-fifth of what the same service cost a US client.

The reproductive-justice frame does not settle the philosophical question between Anderson and Robertson. It changes which question is the most urgent. (Roberts 1997)

4 Surrogacy Regimes

4.1 Commercial vs. Altruistic

Commercial Altruistic
What changes hands A negotiated fee beyond medical expenses Medical expenses and (sometimes) lost wages only
Who chooses the surrogate Agency matching, often across state or national lines Typically a friend or relative
The implicit ethical picture Markets can handle reproductive labor with the right safeguards Markets cannot handle this; gift-relations can
Where it is the law California, Texas, several other US states; Ukraine UK, Canada, Australia, most of Europe

Most jurisdictions choose one regime. The choice is not technical; it is a stance on Anderson and Robertson.

4.3 Cross-Border Surrogacy

The pre-2015 international market. India’s commercial-surrogacy industry was estimated at $1.5 billion annually by 2012, with clients drawn from the US, UK, Israel, Australia, and Europe. Thailand and Nepal hosted parallel markets.

A typical international gestational surrogacy in India in 2014 cost the client ~$25,000 — about one-fifth of the equivalent US arrangement. The surrogate’s share was typically $6,000–$8,000.

The 2015 cascade. Thailand banned commercial surrogacy in February 2015 following the Baby Gammy case — an Australian couple who left a Down-syndrome twin with the Thai surrogate and took only the healthy sibling home. India followed in October 2015. Nepal in September. The market migrated to Mexico, then to Ukraine, and after 2022, to Colombia and Argentina.

The industry does not disappear when one jurisdiction bans it. It moves. (Mohapatra 2012)

4.4 Thought Question — Three Surrogacy Arrangements

Three real arrangements, lightly summarized. For each: is the arrangement (a) permissible, (b) permissible with regulation, or (c) impermissible? What is your reason — Anderson, Robertson, reproductive justice, or some combination?

  1. A married couple in San Francisco pays a $45,000 fee plus medical expenses to a married woman in Stockton with two children of her own, to gestate an embryo created from the couple’s own gametes.
  2. A single man in Tel Aviv pays an Indian-run agency $40,000 to find a gestational surrogate in Cancún, Mexico. The surrogate’s share is $7,000.
  3. A woman in London gestates a child for her sister, who cannot carry a pregnancy due to a uterine condition. The arrangement is reimbursed for medical expenses only.

4.5 The European Professional Consensus

The European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology (ESHRE) issued an updated ethics position in March 2025. Headline:

“Non-commercial surrogacy is an acceptable method of assisted reproduction for specific indications.… Commercial surrogacy arrangements are more likely to result in exploitation than non-commercial ones and therefore should be avoided.”

— ESHRE Ethics Committee (Shenfield et al. 2025)

The ten recommendations, in shortest form:

  • Independent counseling and independent legal advice for each party.
  • The surrogate should be able to carry safely, judged medically and psychologically.
  • Surrogacy is not acceptable where the intended parent could carry without serious risk.
  • The surrogate should already have at least one child of her own.
  • Single embryo transfer only.
  • Reimbursement for actual expenses only — no incentive payments.
  • Mandatory registration and follow-up studies.

4.6 Make It Contemporary

Three live policy debates, 2023–2025:

  • New York’s 2021 legalization in retrospect — the state has become the second-largest US surrogacy market after California; advocates point to robust statutory protections, critics to commercialization.
  • Italy’s 2024 law criminalizing Italian citizens for using surrogates abroad — the first extraterritorial enforcement of a surrogacy ban.
  • The EU’s draft framework seeking common rules on cross-border arrangements, stalled since 2023.

The pattern these share:

Every regime is reacting to the same thing — gestational surrogacy as a real, growing, technologically reliable practice that the previous generation’s law did not anticipate.

What divides them is what each takes the primary harm to be: exploitation of surrogates, commodification of children, or restriction of intended parents’ procreative liberty.

4.7 After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction the body has been circling.

Exploitation and commodification are not the same wrong. A non-exploitative market can still commodify the good it trades in. A non-commodifying arrangement (an altruistic family surrogacy with no money involved) can still exploit the surrogate through emotional pressure or asymmetric power.

Most surrogacy debates assume the two wrongs travel together. They do not. Which wrong drives your judgment shapes what fix you reach for — better contracts and informed consent address exploitation; the ban-it-or-permit-it question addresses commodification.

5 Gamete Markets

5.1 A Brief History of Sperm Donation

1884First documented donor insemination (Philadelphia)
1953First frozen sperm bank
1980sHIV-driven quarantine protocols
2005UK bans donor anonymity
2023Mass DNA identifications begin

For most of this history, sperm donation operated on a promise of donor anonymity. That promise — as the right end of the timeline shows — is no longer one the technology lets us keep.

5.2 The Rise of Paid Egg Donation

Volume.

  • ~10,000 US cycles using donor eggs per year by 2010.
  • ~17,000 by 2020.
  • Roughly one in nine IVF cycles in the US now uses donor eggs.

Price.

  • ASRM (American Society for Reproductive Medicine) guideline, now withdrawn: donor compensation should not exceed $10,000.
  • Median compensation in 2024: ~$8,000.
  • High-end programs: $25,000–$50,000 for donors meeting specific phenotypic criteria.

5.3 An Ad That Has Done Thirty Years of Work in Bioethics Classrooms

In March 1999, a classified advertisement appeared in the Yale Daily News — and in the Stanford Daily, the Harvard Crimson, and the campus papers of several other elite universities. The ad sought an egg donor between 21 and 33 who had scored above 1400 on the SAT, was at least 5 feet 10 inches tall, and had no major family medical issues. The compensation offered was $50,000, plus all expenses.

The ad was real. The couple who placed it remained anonymous. The compensation was roughly five times the going rate for an egg donor at the time, and it was contingent on phenotypic criteria — height, intellectual achievement, family background — that the standard donor market did not explicitly price.

Almost every American undergraduate ethics class for the last twenty-five years has discussed some version of this ad. The reason is that it makes a single question very hard to avoid: what, exactly, is being purchased for fifty thousand dollars?

5.4 Anonymous vs. Open-Identity Donation

Jurisdiction Donor anonymity Year
Sweden Banned 1985
UK Banned 2005
Netherlands Banned 2004
Germany Banned 2018
Australia (Victoria) Banned, retroactive to 1988 2017
United States Permitted —
France Banned 2021

The US is now an outlier among comparable jurisdictions in continuing to permit anonymous donation. Whether that legal permission still tracks practical reality is the next question. (Wilkinson and Williams 2023)

5.5 The End of Donor Anonymity

Important

23andMe and Ancestry have roughly 50 million combined users as of 2024. Any donor with one cousin in either database is, in practice, identifiable — the donor-conceived child uploads a sample, the algorithm flags a third or fourth cousin, the family tree narrows the candidates to one.

The policy of donor anonymity in the US now functions as a promise the technology no longer lets us keep. Sperm banks that continue to advertise anonymity are advertising a property of the contract, not a property of the world.

The ethical question is no longer whether anonymity should be available. It is whether donors should be required to make peace with the fact that it will not hold.

6 Parental Rights

6.1 The Question Parental-Rights Cases Force

Three claims can each plausibly ground parenthood:

  • Genetic — the person whose gametes contributed.
  • Gestational — the person who carried and delivered the child.
  • Intentional — the person who set the project in motion and means to raise the child.

In a non-contested case, all three line up in the same person. In a contested case, they pull apart. Baby M and Johnson are the two foundational decisions about which claim wins when they pull apart.

6.2 In re Baby M (1988) — Facts

The arrangement. William and Elizabeth Stern, a New Jersey couple, contracted with Mary Beth Whitehead through the Infertility Center of New York. Whitehead would be artificially inseminated with William Stern’s sperm, gestate the resulting child, and surrender parental rights at birth in exchange for $10,000.

The child — Baby M, later Melissa Stern — was born March 27, 1986. Whitehead surrendered her, then changed her mind within days and fled with the child to Florida.

The litigation. The Sterns located Whitehead in July 1986 and recovered the child. Whitehead countersued for custody. The New Jersey trial court enforced the contract, terminated Whitehead’s parental rights, and granted custody to the Sterns.

The New Jersey Supreme Court agreed to review the case in 1987.

6.3 Baby M — Holding

Important

Held (NJ Supreme Court, en banc, Feb. 1988): The surrogacy contract is void as against public policy. The state may not enforce a contract that purports to terminate parental rights before birth or that pays for the transfer of a child.

Custody was nonetheless awarded to William Stern under the best-interests-of-the-child standard. Mary Beth Whitehead retained parental rights as the genetic and gestational mother, with visitation. The Sterns adopted Melissa later.

The opinion did not ban surrogacy. It made commercial surrogacy contracts unenforceable in New Jersey. The contract did not collapse; only its legal teeth did.

(Supreme Court of New Jersey 1988)

6.4 Johnson v. Calvert (1993) — Facts

The arrangement. Mark and Crispina Calvert, a California couple, contracted with Anna Johnson in 1990. The Calverts provided both gametes; Johnson would gestate the resulting embryo and surrender the child at birth, for $10,000.

The case was different from Baby M in a single critical respect: Johnson had no genetic relationship to the child. Both the egg and the sperm were the Calverts’.

The dispute. Late in the pregnancy, Johnson demanded the remaining payments and threatened not to give up the child. The Calverts sued for declaratory relief. The trial court ruled that the Calverts were the sole legal parents. Johnson appealed.

The California Supreme Court agreed to hear the case in 1992.

6.5 Johnson — The Intent Test

Important

Held (Cal. Supreme Court, May 1993): When both gestational and genetic claims to maternity exist and conflict, the legal mother is the woman who, at the time of conception, intended to bring about the birth of the child she intended to raise.

The court rejected the “two mothers” possibility and the gestational-trumps-genetic rule. It also rejected the genetic-trumps-gestational rule. The deciding factor was intent at the time of conception — the project, not the biology.

The decision launched gestational surrogacy as a stable American institution. Twenty-six US states now follow some version of the intent test for gestational arrangements.

(Supreme Court of California 1993)

6.6 The Three Tests, Side by Side

Test Picks the parent who… Where it works Where it fails
Genetic Contributed the gametes Disputes over abandoned IVF embryos Anonymous donation; egg donation; cases where the genetic parent never intended to raise
Gestational Carried and delivered the child Traditional surrogacy (Baby M); refusal-to-relinquish cases Gestational surrogacy where the carrier has no genetic tie and never intended to raise
Intent Set the project in motion and meant to raise Gestational surrogacy with a clean contract Divorce mid-pregnancy; death of an intended parent; bad-faith intent

No test handles every case. Most US jurisdictions use intent as the default and best interests as the backstop.

6.7 Thought Question — Three Mothers

A married couple in Los Angeles begins an IVF cycle using a paid egg donor — they intend the resulting child to be raised by the husband and wife together. The embryo is implanted in a paid gestational carrier in San Diego. During the second trimester, the husband and wife separate. The wife disclaims interest in the child. The egg donor — informed of the pregnancy through a friend in the clinic — files a petition for parental rights.

Write or discuss.

  1. Under the intent test, who is the legal mother?
  2. Under the genetic test? Under the gestational test?
  3. Which test is your test, and what does it commit you to in the cases the other tests handle better?

6.8 After You’ve Discussed

Note

The intent test does most of the day-to-day legal work in the US. It is fast, it is contract-friendly, and it produces predictable answers in the typical gestational-surrogacy case.

But the intent test presupposes a clean contract. When the contract dissolves — through death, divorce, dispute, or simple change of mind — the test runs out of inputs. Baby M’s best-interests-of-the-child backstop is what catches those cases. It is slower, less predictable, and more discretionary. It is also the principle the law falls back on when the cleaner test breaks down.

The American compromise is to use both: intent as the rule, best interests as the safety net.

7 LePage and the Alabama IVF Aftershock

7.1 The Case Behind the Headlines

In late 2020, at the Center for Reproductive Medicine in Mobile, Alabama, a hospital patient wandered into the clinic’s cryogenic storage area. The patient removed several frozen embryos from a tank of liquid nitrogen with bare hands, sustained burns, and dropped the embryos, which were destroyed.

Three sets of parents whose embryos were lost sued the clinic. The lower courts dismissed the wrongful-death claims, holding that frozen embryos were not “minor children” under the Alabama Wrongful Death of a Minor Act.

The Alabama Supreme Court reversed.

7.2 LePage v. Mobile Infirmary Clinic (Feb 2024)

Important

Held (Ala. Supreme Court, Feb. 16, 2024): The Alabama Wrongful Death of a Minor Act applies to “all unborn children, regardless of their location” — including embryos located outside of a biological uterus at the time of their destruction.

The plaintiffs may sue for the wrongful death of their frozen embryos under the same statute that would apply to the wrongful death of a born child.

The opinion does not state that all destruction of frozen embryos constitutes wrongful death; it states that the statute is available for the claim. Whether any particular destruction qualifies remains to be litigated.

(Supreme Court of Alabama 2024)

7.3 Justice Parker’s Concurrence

“In summary, the theologically based view of the sanctity of life adopted by the People of Alabama encompasses the following: (1) God made every person in His image; (2) each person therefore has a value that far exceeds the ability of human beings to calculate; and (3) human life cannot be wrongfully destroyed without incurring the wrath of a holy God, who views the destruction of His image as an affront to Himself.”

— Parker, C.J., concurring, LePage v. Mobile Infirmary Clinic (2024)

The concurrence is not the holding. The majority opinion rests on statutory interpretation; Parker’s concurrence supplies the religious framing. Students should be able to tell the difference.

7.4 The Clinical Reaction

Feb 16LePage ruling issued
Feb 21UAB (Univ. of Alabama at Birmingham) pauses IVF
Feb 22Alabama Fertility pauses IVF
Feb 23Infirmary Health pauses IVF
Mar 6State law signed granting providers immunity

In the eighteen days between the ruling and the legislative fix, an unknown number of patients had cycles cancelled mid-stimulation. The clinics’ position was not that the ruling banned IVF — it did not — but that the legal exposure of any embryo loss, normal in IVF practice, had become uninsurable.

7.5 The Legislative Fix

Important

Alabama Act 2024-21 (signed March 6, 2024) grants civil and criminal immunity to IVF providers and patients for the death of or damage to an embryo during the course of IVF treatment.

The act does not disturb the underlying holding that embryos are children for purposes of the Wrongful Death Act. The fix is a carve-out, not a reversal.

The structural question — whether embryos can be the subjects of wrongful-death claims in any context outside the IVF clinic — remains open. A donor who destroys leftover embryos; a clinic that disposes of abandoned ones; a researcher who uses them for stem-cell work — each of these is, in principle, exposed.

7.6 What’s Still in Litigation

The LePage plaintiffs’ damages claims continue. The wrongful-death-of-a-minor statute permits punitive damages with no statutory cap; the question of how a jury values a frozen embryo’s “life” is the next frontier.

Several other state legislatures have introduced bills since 2024 that would similarly extend wrongful-death protection to embryos.

Practical consequences in Alabama, post-Act 2024-21:

  • Patients can resume IVF; clinics can continue to dispose of “non-viable” embryos.
  • “Disposition” of remaining embryos at the end of treatment remains legally fraught — providers are quietly recommending against destruction.
  • An estimated 350,000 frozen embryos in Alabama storage occupy an unclear legal status.

7.7 After LePage

Note

LePage is not, strictly, about commodification or about parental rights. It lives in the same neighborhood, and the same legal logic.

If embryos are “children,” then their disposition, transfer, storage, and the contracts that govern their fate all become different legal questions. A clinic that “destroys” embryos is now, in Alabama, potentially in the wrongful-death liability landscape that, in the body of medicine, applies to medical errors causing the death of a born patient.

The post-Dobbs logic has reached IVF. The next case, wherever it arises, will be about whether the same logic reaches the millions of frozen embryos sitting in storage in jurisdictions whose statutes were never written with them in mind.

7.8 Florida 2026 — Surrogacy as the Next Front

The facts. A married couple in France contracted with a Florida woman for gestational surrogacy in 2024. In August 2025, a Broward County judge — Marlon Weiss — granted parental rights but volunteered in his opinion that surrogacy may be unconstitutional on fetal-personhood grounds.

Within twenty-four hours of the child’s birth, Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier moved to intervene in the confidential proceeding, arguing that commercial surrogacy violates the Thirteenth Amendment’s prohibition of slavery. The case is pending before Florida’s Fourth District Court of Appeal.

Why the slide is here. Uthmeier’s argument — that commercial surrogacy treats children as property and women’s reproductive labor as the kind of thing that can be sold — is structurally Anderson’s argument from 1990, made by a conservative state attorney general in 2026.

The political coalition that opposes commercial surrogacy in the United States now runs from feminist philosophers on the left to fetal-personhood advocates on the right. They disagree about almost everything else. They converge on the claim that some things are not properly bought.

(Ellenbogen 2026)

8 Three Quick Frontiers

8.1 Posthumous Reproduction

The practice. Sperm or eggs retrieved after death, then used to conceive a child. Sperm retrieval up to 36 hours post-mortem is technically feasible; egg retrieval is harder and rarer.

US case law is permissive but inconsistent. Israel and Australia have detailed statutory frameworks.

The question.

Does consent to reproduction survive death? Does explicit consent to gamete retrieval imply consent to reproduction? The Jocelyn Edwards case (New South Wales, Australia, 2011) — a widow who sued for use of her dead husband’s sperm — turned on exactly this distinction.

8.2 Savior Siblings

The Nash family, 2000. Lisa and Jack Nash’s first child, Molly, was born with Fanconi anemia — a fatal genetic blood disorder. The only cure was a stem-cell transplant from a matched sibling donor.

The Nashes used IVF and PGT-M to select an embryo that (a) did not carry the disease and (b) was a tissue match for Molly. Adam Nash was born in August 2000. His umbilical-cord blood was used to treat Molly, who is alive as of 2024.

The ethical question in two sentences: was Adam created as a means to Molly’s survival? Does it matter that the Nashes intended to love him for his own sake regardless?

8.3 Mitochondrial Replacement

Note

“Three-parent IVF.” A technique that transfers the nucleus of a fertilized egg into a second egg whose mitochondria are healthy. Used to prevent serious mitochondrial diseases that would otherwise be inherited from the egg donor’s mitochondria.

Approved in the UK in 2015; in clinical use since 2016. Approximately 50 children have been born with mitochondrial DNA from a third party.

The philosophical question is whether the mitochondrial donor is a parent at all. She contributes ~0.1% of the child’s DNA, no nuclear material, and (typically) no relationship. The intent test says no; a strict genetic test says yes.

9.2 The Through-Line

Part A asked what autonomy requires during a pregnancy.

Part B asked what families are owed around one — the markets in gametes and gestation, the contracts that move parental rights from one body to another, the cases that decide who counts as a parent when the standard answers diverge.

Same value, different terrain. The autonomy that protects a pregnant woman’s refusal of treatment is the same autonomy that lets a couple sign a surrogacy contract — and the same autonomy whose limits the Baby M court tried to draw.

The fight in both halves is over which conditions count as the conditions of a free choice, and which count as the conditions of an arrangement no law should enforce.

9.3 Review Questions

Write a brief answer to each, or use them as discussion prompts.

Recall

In your own words, state the intent test from Johnson v. Calvert. Give one kind of case where the intent test does not give a clear answer.

Apply

An Alabama woman wants her IVF clinic to destroy three frozen embryos she no longer needs. The clinic refuses, citing LePage. What is the clinic worried about, and what is the patient’s strongest reply?

Debate

Anderson says commercial surrogacy is wrong in itself, no matter how well it is regulated. Robertson says it is fine unless we can show specific harms. Pick the side you find harder to defend and make the strongest case you can for it.

9.4 Key Terms

  • Commodification — treating a good as the kind of thing that can properly be produced, exchanged, and disposed of under market norms.
  • Procreative liberty — Robertson’s term for the right to decide whether, when, and how to reproduce.
  • Reproductive justice — the frame, associated with Dorothy Roberts, asking whose reproductive choices are constrained by what social conditions.
  • Gestational vs. traditional surrogacy — gestational: surrogate has no genetic tie; traditional: surrogate’s egg is used.
  • Intent test — the rule from Johnson v. Calvert that parental rights belong to whoever set the project in motion intending to raise the child.
  • Best interests of the child — the family-law default standard that decides contested custody when other rules run out.
  • Donor anonymity — a contractual protection of a gamete donor’s identity; increasingly unenforceable in the consumer-DNA era.
  • Wrongful-death-of-embryo statute — a state law extending wrongful-death liability to the destruction of frozen embryos (Alabama, 2024).
