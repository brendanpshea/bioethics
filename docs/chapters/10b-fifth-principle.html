Lecture 10 — AI in Medicine

Part B: The Fifth Principle — Consent, Accountability, and Black Boxes

Author
Affiliation

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

1 The Machine in the Room

1.1 Where We Left Off

In Part A, one question diagnosed every failure: what was the model actually trained to do, and is that what we’re using it for? The cost algorithm, the sepsis alarm, the skin app — each was caught and partly fixed.

But every fix had the same hidden requirement: someone who could explain the system. Someone had to see that the label was cost, that the alert fired after suspicion, that the model was reading rulers. Part B asks what happens to consent, blame, and trust when — increasingly — nobody fully can.

1.2 A Case to Sit With

This time, you are the patient. Halfway through your annual physical, you realize your physician hasn’t typed a single note — she’s just talking with you, which is honestly nice. You ask about it.

“Oh — the AI listens to our visit and writes the note now,” she says. “It gives me two hours of my evening back. Don’t worry, I review everything it writes.”

  1. Should you have been told before the visit started? Asked?
  2. What would you actually want to know — and is there anything you said in that room you’d have phrased differently, knowing something was transcribing?
  3. She reviews everything it writes. Does that settle it?

1.3 What the Scribe Actually Heard

Ambient AI scribes are the most widely deployed generative AI in American medicine — most large health systems now use them. Here is the part the waiting-room poster doesn’t say.

  • Your visit’s audio travels to a vendor’s servers, is transcribed and summarized by a language model, and returns as a draft note your clinician edits — or, at the end of a long day, skims.
  • Investigations have documented scribe systems inventing plausible content: transcription tools inserting whole fabricated sentences, drafts recording “patient denies chest pain” for a question never asked. Part A’s hallucination problem, now writing your chart.
  • The chart is forever. The draft’s errors, signed, become medical fact.

1.4 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Whatever you answered, you probably reached for ideas like they should have told me, someone should check it, and if it’s wrong, that’s on somebody. Disclosure, oversight, blame — every one of those quietly assumes that someone can explain what the system did.

The four principles you’ve used all semester make the same assumption. Today we meet the philosophers who noticed that — and concluded the four principles need a fifth.

1.5 Today

By the end of Part B, you will be able to:

  • State Floridi’s five principles for AI ethics and explain what explicability adds to the four you already know.
  • Run AI disclosure through the four elements of informed consent, and distinguish disclosure of use, role, and performance.
  • Map who is plausibly liable when AI contributes to harm — clinician, hospital, vendor, regulator — and what each framing incentivizes.
  • Reconstruct the chatbot-therapy debate, including the documented harms, the 2026 settlement, and the Illinois ban — and run Mill’s test on that ban.
  • Apply the defer-or-override question to a concrete alert, using automation bias, algorithm aversion, and the deskilling evidence.

2 The Fifth Principle

2.1 Everyone Wrote a Principles List

In the late 2010s, AI ethics had a paperwork boom: governments, tech companies, and institutes published dozens of competing principle lists — the EU had one, Google had one, the OECD had one.

Luciano Floridi and colleagues did the obvious scholarly thing: they laid the major lists side by side and looked for convergence (Floridi et al. 2018). The lists kept agreeing — and what they agreed on was something a bioethics student would recognize on sight. The four principles of biomedical ethics had quietly become the world’s template for AI ethics.

2.2 The Four, Restated for Machines

Principle The AI reading Part A exhibit
Beneficence AI should genuinely benefit people and the planet the case for a working sepsis alarm
Non-maleficence don’t harm: safety, privacy, misuse the bromism case
Autonomy humans keep the power to decide whether to decide your aunt and the skin app
Justice fair distribution of benefits and harms the cost algorithm

Your toolkit just absorbed a new technology — almost. Floridi’s group concluded the four cover everything AI ethics needs… except one thing no medical technology had forced before.

2.3 Two Halves of Explicability

“…incorporating both the epistemological sense of ‘intelligibility’ (as an answer to the question ‘how does it work?’) and in the ethical sense of ‘accountability’ (as an answer to the question ‘who is responsible for the way it works?’).”

Floridi et al., defining the new fifth principle, explicability — “AI4People: An Ethical Framework for a Good AI Society” (2018) (Floridi et al. 2018)

Two questions, two halves of today’s lecture: how does it work? (consent) and who answers for it? (liability).

2.4 Why a Fifth Principle at All?

The argument is that the four principles presuppose explanation — they stop working without it.

  • You can’t consent to what can’t be conveyed to you (autonomy).
  • You can’t assign blame for what can’t be reconstructed (non-maleficence needs an address).
  • You can’t audit fairness in what can’t be inspected (justice — recall how the cost algorithm was caught: someone looked inside).

Explicability adds no new value of its own. It is the enabling principle — the one that keeps the other four usable when the decision-maker is a black box.

2.5 Why the Box Is Black

“Even the makers can’t explain it”?

A modern model is millions — sometimes billions — of numerical weights tuned during training. It does not contain a chain of reasons anyone can read out; its makers can describe how it was trained and how often it’s right, but usually not why it said this, here, today. A research field called explainable AI builds after-the-fact explanations — but these are approximations of the model’s behavior, not its actual reasons, and they can be wrong about it.

Keep the bar honest, though: your clinician can’t explain her pattern recognition either. The difference we’ll keep probing is that she can be questioned, trained, and held to account — she is an answerable black box.

2.6 The Scoreboard, Introduced

Each case today gets scored against the five principles. First up: the unannounced scribe from our opening.

Principle The scribe case
Beneficence real — two hours of a clinician’s evening returned ✅
Non-maleficence fabricated chart content, signed into fact ⚖️
Autonomy you never agreed to a third listener ⚖️
Justice who gets the careful review — the 9 a.m. or the 5 p.m. patient? ⚖️
Explicability nobody told you; no one can fully audit the draft’s source ❌

As the cases accumulate, the bottom row — explicability — keeps failing first.

3 Intelligibility: Consent with AI in the Loop

3.1 The Four Elements Meet the Machine

You learned the anatomy of valid consent early in this course: disclosure, understanding, voluntariness, capacity. AI doesn’t threaten all four equally.

  • Capacity and voluntariness are mostly untouched — the patient is the same patient.
  • Disclosure is strained because AI arrives ambiently — bundled into the EHR, the workflow, the dictation system — and nobody assigned anyone the job of mentioning it.
  • Understanding is strained the deepest: what, exactly, could the patient be brought to understand about a black box?

3.2 Must the Patient Be Told?

The case against routine disclosure

We don’t disclose the resident who pre-read the scan, the lab analyzer’s algorithm, or which UpToDate article the doctor skimmed. Care is full of undisclosed instruments. Disclosing everything buries the patient in noise — and disclosure of everything is disclosure of nothing.

The case for it

Surveys consistently find patients want to know — and trust collapses retroactively when they find out by accident. An instrument doesn’t draft conclusions; this one does. And unlike the resident, the AI’s involvement is invisible unless someone chooses to say it.

3.3 Told What, Exactly?

Suppose we disclose. The next problem is the regress.

  • “An algorithm was involved in your care” is true of everything and informative about nothing.
  • The full technical truth — architectures, training sets, validation statistics — is impossible to convey and wouldn’t help.
  • The working legal standard for consent offers the middle: disclose what a reasonable patient would find material to their decision.

Materiality moves the question from whether AI was used to what role it played — a flag the doctor weighed, or the de facto decision-maker? That distinction will carry the rest of the section.

3.4 The Scribe, Run Through the Elements

Element How the scribe case bends it
Disclosure a sign in the waiting room? a line in the intake packet nobody reads? said aloud, every visit?
Understanding of what — that audio leaves the building? that drafts can fabricate? the error rate (which nobody has measured at your clinic)?
Voluntariness you can opt out — by being the difficult patient who makes the doctor type for an hour
Capacity untouched — the one element AI leaves alone

A low-stakes technology, and every element bends. Now raise the stakes.

3.5 Understanding Without Explanation

The hardest version of the question: can consent be valid when no one — not the patient, not the physician, not the vendor — can explain why the model recommended this?

  • One answer: no. Autonomy means deciding for reasons; a recommendation without conveyable reasons can be accepted, but not really consented to. Explanation is non-negotiable.
  • The other: yes. Medicine has always run on validated-but-unexplained treatments — lithium stabilized mood for decades before anyone knew why, and nobody called those prescriptions unconsented. What matters is the track record, not the mechanism: we don’t know how it works, but we know that it works — on patients like you.

This dispute does not resolve. But the second answer quietly requires that the track record exist — external validation again, now wearing consent’s clothes.

3.6 Thought Question

A 58-year-old patient learns her mammogram will be read “by the radiologist, with AI assistance.” She asks for “a human only — no computer involved.” The AI second-reader demonstrably catches cancers human readers miss, and it’s now your department’s standard workflow.

  1. Does she have the right to refuse the AI? Is that like refusing a treatment — or like demanding the lab hand-count her blood cells?
  2. Did she even have the right to be told? (Your hospital’s current answer, most likely: it was in the intake paperwork.)
  3. Does your answer change if the AI reads first, and the human only reviews what it flags?

3.7 Make It Contemporary

Health systems now use language models to draft replies to patient portal messages, with the clinician editing and signing. In a blinded 2023 comparison, evaluators preferred the chatbot’s answers to physicians’ answers in about 79% of cases — and rated them significantly more empathetic (Ayers et al. 2023).

Some systems append a disclosure line (“drafted with AI assistance, reviewed by your care team”). Others don’t.

If the message is medically right, signed by your actual doctor, and kinder than she would have been at 6 p.m. — what exactly is the patient owed? Is the undisclosed draft a broken promise, or just a better template?

3.8 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Most disclosure debates blur three different duties. Disclosure of use: “AI was involved.” Disclosure of role: “AI flagged this; your doctor made the call” — versus “your doctor reviews what AI decides.” Disclosure of performance: “it misses about X% of cases like yours.”

The portal-message case feels easy because only use is at stake. The mammogram case is hard because the patient is really asking about role. And almost no one, anywhere, is currently offered performance — even though it’s the one a reasonable patient would most want.

4 Accountability: When the AI Is Wrong

4.1 When It’s Wrong, Who Answers?

Explicability’s second half: who is responsible for the way it works? With AI in the loop, the answer scatters — philosophers call it the problem of many hands.

  • The vendor built the model (and disclaims it). The hospital bought, configured, and tuned the alert threshold. The clinician followed it — or didn’t. The regulator cleared it — or never saw it.
  • Each hand points at the next. Harm without an address is the accountability failure in its purest form.
  • The question for this section isn’t only who pays — it’s what behavior each answer trains.

4.2 The Liability Map

Who Legal theory The problem with stopping here
Clinician malpractice — deviation from standard of care punishes the person with the least knowledge of the model’s flaws
Hospital corporate negligence — chose, configured, deployed it incentivizes caution, but hospitals hide behind “the doctor decided”
Vendor product liability — defective design almost entirely untested in court; contracts and trade secrets shield it
Regulator cleared it for market can’t be sued; clearance ≠ a guarantee, though juries hear it as one

The right-hand column matters most: every row’s framing trains someone to behave differently — and right now, most of the training lands on the clinician.

4.3 The Standard-of-Care Trap

The clinician’s bind, stated precisely:

  • Follow the AI and it’s wrong → “you outsourced your judgment to software.”
  • Override the AI and you’re wrong → “the warning was on your screen, doctor, and you dismissed it.”
  • As AI use becomes customary, the standard of care itself absorbs the machine — and the legally safest position becomes using AI exactly the way everyone else does.

That last point should bother you: herd behavior wearing the costume of prudence. The standard of care was supposed to encode judgment, not insulation.

4.4 Why Vendors Stay Dry

The vendor — the party with the most knowledge of the model’s failure modes — has so far carried the least legal exposure. The shield has three layers.

  • The “decision support” disclaimer: the software merely advises; a licensed professional decides. The clinician’s license becomes the vendor’s liability sponge.
  • Contract terms: hospitals sign indemnification clauses and accept “as-is” performance language that patients never see.
  • Trade secrecy: the very opacity that creates the risk also frustrates the lawsuit — you can’t depose a weight matrix.

Knowledge and exposure point in opposite directions. That is an accountability architecture, and somebody chose it.

4.5 The Regulatory Map

Where’s the regulator in all this? It depends entirely on which kind of AI — recall Part A’s three machines.

  • Diagnostic AI is regulated as a medical device: 1,250+ authorized, with premarket review and (slowly improving) postmarket surveillance.
  • Predictive and generative tools mostly ride the clinical-decision-support carve-out: software that “supports” a clinician who can review its basis isn’t a device at all.
  • In January 2026, the FDA loosened that carve-out’s guidance in a deliberately generative-AI-friendly direction — the gap between what’s deployed and what’s reviewed is widening by design, on the theory that innovation needs room.

One detail worth knowing: Woebot — the most carefully validated therapy chatbot — shut down its consumer app in mid-2025, citing the cost of seeking FDA authorization its less cautious competitors never attempted. Regulation that binds only the conscientious has a name problem.

4.6 Scoreboard: The Ignored Alert

Build the case from Part A’s own facts: the rebuilt sepsis model alerts constantly; a nurse, rationally trained by forty false alarms, dismisses #41; the patient is septic; harm is done.

Principle The ignored-alert case
Beneficence the alarm exists to save exactly this patient ⚖️
Non-maleficence harm arrived through the design — threshold, volume, fatigue ❌
Autonomy the nurse “chose” — inside a choice architecture she never chose ⚖️
Justice the chart shows her dismissal, not the vendor’s threshold ❌
Explicability accountability scatters across four parties; the record blames one ❌

4.7 Thought Question

The AI says your patient has a 90% chance of sepsis. The resident examines her and sees no clinical signs — and has watched this same alert cry wolf all month.

  1. What should happen in the next ten minutes? (Not “in policy” — at this bedside.)
  2. If the resident overrides the alert and the patient crashes, who failed — resident, hospital, vendor, or threshold? Assign percentages if you dare.
  3. Now swap it: the resident follows the alert, and the patient is harmed by aggressive unnecessary treatment. Did your percentages move? Why?

4.8 Make It Contemporary

Here is the current legal landscape, and it is genuinely strange: the regulator stepped back (the 2026 guidance), the courts haven’t stepped in (almost no decided malpractice cases turn on clinical AI yet), and professional societies issue guidance with no teeth.

So who is actually deciding AI liability in American medicine right now? Hospital risk managers and vendor lawyers, in procurement contracts nobody else reads.

Is contract-by-contract private ordering a reasonable way to allocate responsibility for patient harm — or a vacuum wearing a suit? What would have to happen (one big verdict? one statute?) to change it?

4.9 After You’ve Discussed

Note

The anthropologist Madeleine Elish studied automation disasters — autopilots, early self-driving cars — and noticed a pattern she named the moral crumple zone: as control shifts to the machine, blame stays with the nearest human, who absorbs the moral impact the way a car’s crumple zone absorbs the physical one (Elish 2019).

The nurse at alert #41 is a moral crumple zone. So, arguably, is the physician “reviewing” sixty scribe drafts at 6 p.m. The question to ask of every human-in-the-loop design from now on: is the human there to catch errors — or to catch liability?

5 The Chatbot Therapist

5.1 The 3 a.m. Problem

Before the harms, the honest case for — because any critique that skips it is critiquing an imaginary world.

  • Roughly a third of Americans live in federally designated mental-health professional shortage areas; therapy runs $100–200 a session; waits stretch months.
  • The chatbot is free, anonymous, infinitely patient, and awake at 3 a.m. — which is when the crisis is.
  • People report real comfort, and for some, it’s the first time they’ve told anyone.

The alternative to the chatbot, for millions, is not a therapist. It is nobody. Any honest accounting of the harms has to keep that fact on the table.

5.2 Two Very Different Machines

Purpose-built therapy apps

Scripted around evidence-based methods (usually CBT), narrow by design, guardrailed, some clinically trialed. Woebot was the standard-bearer — pre-written responses, no improvisation, 1.5 million users.

General companion chatbots

LLM-based, engagement-optimized, role-playing, unguarded by design — built to keep you talking, not to discharge you. This is where the documented harms cluster.

The public debate constantly blurs these. And the market just resolved the blur the wrong way: Woebot shut down its consumer app in June 2025, citing FDA-authorization costs — while the unguarded companions, which never sought authorization, kept growing.

5.3 The Documented Harms

Recall sycophancy from Part A: the learned tendency to tell users what they want to hear. In a confidant, it’s a flaw. In a therapist, it is disqualifying — therapy’s whole job is the productive no.

  • In safety testing reported in 2025, a companion-style bot advised a fictional recovering methamphetamine user that he should take “a small hit of meth to get through this week.” That sentence helped move a state legislature, and we’ll meet the statute shortly.
  • Testers have documented bots affirming delusions and deepening isolation — engagement optimization and therapeutic challenge pull in opposite directions, and engagement is what gets measured.
  • The machine that cannot disappoint you cannot treat you.

5.4 The Litigation

The harms reached courtrooms fast, and the legal theory matters more than the headlines.

  • Families of teenagers who died by suicide after months-long chatbot relationships filed wrongful-death suits against Character.AI and OpenAI beginning in 2024–25.
  • In January 2026, Character.AI and Google settled the leading cases, including the first-filed Garcia suit; cases against OpenAI continue (CNN Business 2026).
  • The core allegation is not “the bot said a wrong thing.” It is design defect: that engagement-maximizing systems engineered the attachment itself — the isolation, the dependency — and that the harm flowed from the design working as intended.

5.5 The Illinois Ban

The Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act (Illinois, August 2025)

The first state law to prohibit AI from providing therapy. AI systems may not deliver therapy or “therapeutic decision-making,” or advertise themselves as therapists; administrative and supplementary uses under a licensed professional’s oversight remain legal. Civil penalties run to $10,000 per violation (Illinois General Assembly 2025). The legislature acted with near-unanimity, citing the documented bad-advice cases — including the “small hit of meth.”

Contrast the lighter touch elsewhere: Utah and Nevada regulate — mandatory AI disclosure, bans on selling user data — without prohibiting.

5.6 Run the Mill Test

A state has forbidden competent adults something many of them want. You own a tool for exactly this — the three questions from our paternalism lecture.

Mill’s question Applied to the AI-therapy ban
Is the chooser competent? The adult user — mostly yes. (Minors are the easy case; Illinois didn’t stop at minors.)
Is the choice voluntary and informed? The live cell: does a sycophantic system that engineers attachment corrupt the very choice to keep using it?
Does it harm only the chooser? Mostly yes — which, for Mill, makes the ban hard paternalism, presumptively forbidden.

The ban’s whole defense lives in the middle row: if the product manufactures the consent it then points to, the choice was never fully voluntary — and the paternalism is soft, not hard. Decide what you think; the legislature already did.

5.7 Thought Question

Your longtime patient — isolated, chronically depressed — tells you her companion chatbot is “the only one who really listens.” She’s stopped returning her sister’s calls. She is an adult, she is stable, and by every metric in her chart she is better than last year.

  1. What do you actually say, in the room, in the next sixty seconds?
  2. Is “it’s working” the end of the analysis? What is it working at?
  3. What would have to change for you to escalate — and what would “escalating” even mean here?

5.8 Make It Contemporary

The platforms moved before most legislatures did. In late 2025, Character.AI banned open-ended chat for users under 18 outright; OpenAI built a separate, restricted under-18 experience with parental controls.

Two readings: (a) responsible companies fixing a real flaw faster than government could; (b) an admission, priced by their own lawyers, that the product was dangerous — in which case why does the adult version need no fixing?

Which reading do you take — and can a product be safe for a lonely 19-year-old if its makers concluded it wasn’t safe for a 17-year-old?

5.9 After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction this whole section has been circling: tool harms versus relationship harms. A wrong drug answer is a tool harm — accuracy fixes it, and disclaimers warn about it. Engineered attachment is a relationship harm — and making the bot more accurate, more fluent, and more empathetic makes it worse, not better.

Now look back at the regulation. Disclaimers, disclosure rules, fact-checking standards — nearly all of it aims at tool harms. The design-defect lawsuits and the Illinois ban are the first legal instruments aimed at the second kind. That’s why this section’s cases felt different from the sepsis alarm: they are.

6 What the Machine Knows About You

6.1 Why Your Chatbot Conversation Isn’t a Medical Record

HIPAA does not protect health information. It protects health information held by certain entities — providers, insurers, and their contractors.

  • A consumer chatbot is none of those. What you type into it has, legally, roughly the protection of a social-media post: the company’s own privacy policy, changeable at will and enforceable mostly by embarrassment.
  • The conversations people have with companion bots — suicidality, addiction, abuse — are exactly the disclosures HIPAA was built to guard.
  • Litigation makes it concrete: chat logs are discoverable. The most intimate “therapy” sessions of the decade are sitting on subpoenaable servers.

6.2 Inside the Wall: Scribes and BAAs

When AI is inside HIPAA’s wall — the scribe vendor signs a business associate agreement — the protection is real but narrower than patients assume.

  • The BAA binds the vendor to safeguard identifiable data — and routinely permits use for “product improvement,” which is to say: your visit may help train the next model.
  • De-identified data can leave the wall entirely, and you learned the catch when we studied genomics: de-identification has known limits, and rich clinical narratives are exactly the kind of data that re-identifies.
  • The honest summary for a patient: protected, yes; private in the way you imagine, no.

6.3 Models That Remember

One more mechanism, specific to large models: memorization.

  • Models can reproduce rare or distinctive training examples nearly verbatim — researchers have extracted names, addresses, and unique phrases from deployed models. A medical record distinctive enough is a record potentially retrievable.
  • Which raises the question nobody has answered: whose charts taught the model? Did they agree? Could they withdraw? When we studied research ethics, using people’s tissue without consent had a name and a scandal. Training data is the tissue of this decade, and the Common Rule wasn’t written for it.

6.4 Scoreboard: The Privacy Asymmetry

Principle Chatbot confession Scribe audio
Non-maleficence ❌ no legal floor at all ⚖️ BAA floor, with leaks by design
Autonomy ⚖️ “agreed” via terms of service ⚖️ disclosed via intake packet
Justice ❌ the most desperate users are least protected ⚖️
Explicability ❌ nobody can say where the words went ⚖️ auditable in principle, unaudited in practice

The asymmetry to carry out of the room: the less medical the setting feels, the less protected the disclosure — exactly backwards from how patients actually behave.

7 The Clinician Between Machine and Patient

7.1 Automation Bias and Its Mirror

Automation bias

The documented tendency to let the machine’s answer displace your own — to stop searching once the system has spoken. The resident who stands down a working hunch because the risk score is low is its clinical face.

Algorithm aversion

The mirror failure: abandoning a good tool after one visible error — while forgiving human colleagues the identical mistake. The unit that quietly stops using a validated alarm after a bad month is its clinical face.

Both are calibration failures, and both have hurt patients. The professional skill of the next decade isn’t trusting AI or distrusting it — it’s knowing which error you’re currently making.

7.2 The Deskilling Worry, Now With Data

The worry used to be speculative. In 2025 it acquired a number.

  • Polish endoscopy centers in a colonoscopy AI trial checked what happened to clinicians’ unassisted performance after months of AI-assisted work: adenoma detection without the AI fell from 28.4% to 22.4% — a 20% relative drop, in 19 experienced specialists (Budzyń et al. 2025).
  • The skill didn’t pause while the machine helped. It decayed — the first real-world evidence of automation deskilling tied to patient outcomes in medicine.
  • For a nursing audience the question is personal: which of your assessment skills are you still practicing, once a score practices them for you? And who is checking?

7.3 When to Defer, When to Override

No formula survives contact with a real bedside, but a defensible frame exists. Ask two questions:

  • Was the model validated on patients like this one? If yes, and your disagreement is a hunch you can’t articulate — that hunch may be automation-era folklore. The evidence usually beats the vibe.
  • Do you hold information the model cannot see? The model reads the chart; you can see the patient — the gray color, the new confusion, the thing not yet in any field. That is exactly when overriding is your job, not your risk.

Either way, the duty explicability adds: be able to say afterward which question you answered, and how — to the family, to the board, and if it comes to it, to the court. Defensible isn’t the same as right. It’s the part you control.

Down the columns, the four classic principles strain case by case. In the last column, explicability fails in every row. That is Floridi’s argument, made concrete: explicability is not a footnote to the four principles — it is what keeps them standing.

8 Where This Leaves Us

8.1 Wrap-Up

The course’s four principles survived their hardest test — a technology that decides without explaining — but they needed a fifth to do it: explicability, intelligibility plus accountability. Intelligibility reshaped consent: the question stopped being was AI used? and became what role did it play, and what would a reasonable patient need to know? Accountability reshaped blame: the problem of many hands, the vendor’s shield, and the moral crumple zone showed that “who answers for the machine” is currently decided by default, not by design. The chatbot cases put both halves under maximum stakes — and the clinician’s job quietly changed along the way, from deciding to deciding when to defer, and being able to say why.

The technology in this lecture will be obsolete before you retire. The five questions you now know how to ask of it won’t be.

8.2 Review Questions

Recall

State Floridi’s five principles and explain the two halves of explicability — intelligibility and accountability — including the question each one answers. Then explain why Floridi’s group called explicability an enabling principle rather than a fifth value.

Apply

An emergency department deploys an AI triage tool that assigns waiting patients a priority score. A patient with atypical symptoms is scored low, waits four hours, and suffers a preventable cardiac arrest. Using the liability map, identify the strongest claim against each of the four parties — and say which framing would most improve future patient safety.

Debate

Should clinicians be legally required to disclose any AI involvement in a patient’s care? Use the use/role/performance distinction to say precisely what your rule would require — and defend it against the strongest objection from the other side.

8.3 Key Terms

  • Explicability — Floridi’s fifth principle: AI must be intelligible and accountable; the enabling condition for the other four.
  • Intelligibility / accountability — explicability’s two halves: “how does it work?” / “who answers for how it works?”
  • Black box — a system whose outputs cannot be traced to human-readable reasons, even by its makers.
  • Materiality standard — disclose what a reasonable patient would find relevant to their decision.
  • Problem of many hands — responsibility scattered across so many contributors that no one is answerable.
  • Standard of care — the legal yardstick for malpractice; absorbs AI as its use becomes customary.
  • Moral crumple zone — Elish’s term for the human kept in the loop partly to absorb blame when the system fails.
  • Automation bias / algorithm aversion — over-trusting the machine / abandoning a good machine after one visible error.
  • Deskilling — the decay of unpracticed clinical skills under automation.
  • Covered entity / business associate agreement — who HIPAA actually binds / the contract that extends it to vendors.
  • Memorization — a model’s capacity to reproduce distinctive training examples nearly verbatim.
