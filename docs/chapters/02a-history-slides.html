Part A: From Hippocrates to the Belmont Report: A History

Brendan Shea, PhD

2026-05-17

A Case to Open With

A Study You Should Refuse

You are a physician asked to help run a syphilis study.

  • The subjects are poor Black men in Alabama.
  • They are told they have “bad blood,” not syphilis.
  • Penicillin now works, but the study keeps going.
  • Your supervisors say the knowledge will help future patients.

Do you stay in the study? If not, what principle tells you to walk away?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Hold onto your answer. Several of the reasons you gave for walking away were not obvious to physicians at the time. They had to be learned case by case, and eventually written down. Part A traces how that happened.

Start with moral discomfort before giving students the vocabulary. The point is to make the historical material feel like an answer to a real problem they already feel.

Today

By the end of Part A, you will be able to:

  • Trace the move from “doctor knows best” to patient consent
  • Tell what actually happened in the Carrie Buck, Nuremberg, Willowbrook, and Tuskegee cases
  • Explain which protection each scandal forced into existence
  • Compare the Nuremberg Code, Helsinki, and the Belmont Report
  • Explain why IRBs and ethics committees exist today

The Old Deal: Doctor Knows Best

The Deal in One Sentence

  • The doctor decides. The patient trusts. No one else asks.
  • Western medicine’s oldest model, traced to the Hippocratic tradition
  • Built on real duties: help the sick, “do no harm,” keep confidences
  • One name for it: paternalism — caring for you by deciding for you

Hippocrates of Kos. Public domain. (U.S. National Library of Medicine 2002)

Its Bright Side and Its Dark Side

The bright side

  • Clinicians really do know more
  • Emergencies need fast judgment
  • Patients often want guidance, not a menu
  • Medicine cannot work without professional judgment

The dark side

  • Expertise slides into domination
  • Good intentions can hide coercion
  • The patient becomes an object of care
  • The profession can protect itself first

A Bedside Moment

Protective lying

A physician tells a family, “Don’t tell him it is cancer yet. He will give up.”

  • Kindness or control?
  • Beneficence or disrespect?
  • What is missing from this encounter?

Paternalism was usually defended as expertise plus benevolence, not cruelty. That is exactly why it lasted so long — and why the abuses ahead were so hard to see from the inside.

When Medicine Serves the State

The Idea Called Eugenics

  • Eugenics: the belief that society can be improved by controlling who is allowed to have children
  • “Good” traits encouraged; “bad” traits — disability, poverty, the “wrong” ancestry — bred out
  • It was not fringe: it was backed by respected scientists
  • In the United States, it was backed by law

Carrie Buck, Age 17

  • Virginia, 1924. Carrie is poor and a foster child.
  • She becomes pregnant after being raped by a relative of her foster family.
  • To hide it, she is labeled “feebleminded” and committed to a state institution.
  • Her infant daughter, Vivian, is judged “defective” after a few minutes’ observation.

“Three Generations” — Buck v. Bell (1927)

  • Virginia wants Carrie sterilized so she can have no more children
  • The U.S. Supreme Court agrees, 8 to 1 (Supreme Court of the United States 1927)
  • Justice Holmes writes: “Three generations of imbeciles are enough.”
  • Carrie is sterilized at 17. Vivian later makes her school’s honor roll.
  • The law did not stop the harm — the law ordered it

The Scoreboard So Far

Scandal What was missing The rule we got
Eugenics & Carrie Buck (1927) Your body is yours; the state cannot breed people Nothing yet — the courts called this legal

The scoreboard is our running spine. Each disaster leaves a gap; later, a code fills it. Right now the gap is wide open.

Thought Question

You are the physician at the Virginia institution in 1927. The court order to sterilize Carrie has arrived.

  • Your supervisor calls it legal, routine, and “kind to society.”
  • You suspect Carrie’s “feeblemindedness” is really poverty and a pregnancy from rape.
  1. Do you perform the operation? If you refuse, what are you appealing to — the law says you are the one in the wrong.
  2. Would it change anything if Carrie herself said yes? Could she give real consent in this situation?
  3. The state’s reasoning is “fewer ‘unfit’ children means a better society.” What is wrong with that argument even when it is sincere?

Make It Contemporary

This is not safely in the past:

  • 2006–2010: about 150 women in California prisons were sterilized, many under pressure and without proper approval.
  • 2017: a Tennessee judge offered reduced jail time to inmates who agreed to a vasectomy or a long-acting contraceptive.

Same question: where is the line between an offer, pressure, and coercion — and who is too constrained to truly say yes?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three things to draw out:

  • “Legal” did not mean “right.” Buck v. Bell has never been formally overturned — law can lag ethics by decades.
  • Consent does not erase coercion. A “yes” from a confined, dependent person may not be free. Ask who is asking and what saying no would cost.
  • “For the good of society” is the move to watch. Trading one person’s body for a population-level gain treats her as a means — exactly the reasoning the later codes were written to block.

Atrocity and the First Rule

Medicine Inside the Camps

  • In Nazi concentration camps, prisoners were used as research material
  • Refusal was not an option a prisoner had
  • One example: prisoners were immersed in ice water for hours to time how long downed pilots might survive
  • Many were injured, disabled, or killed — “science” was the excuse

The Doctors’ Trial (1946–1947)

  • 23 defendants from the Nazi medical system
  • Charges: war crimes and crimes against humanity
  • The judges had outrage but no written standard to point to
  • So they wrote one

Nuremberg Code: The First Principle

“The voluntary consent of the human subject is absolutely essential. This means that the person involved should have legal capacity to give consent; should be so situated as to be able to exercise free power of choice, without the intervention of any element of force, fraud, deceit, duress, over-reaching, or other ulterior form of constraint or coercion; and should have sufficient knowledge and comprehension of the elements of the subject matter involved, as to enable him to make an understanding and enlightened decision.”

The Nuremberg Code, Principle 1 (1947) (Nuremberg Military Tribunals 1947)

The Code’s Other Plain Rules

  • The study must have a real purpose, not just curiosity
  • Risks must be cut to the minimum, and be worth it
  • The subject can quit at any time, for any reason
  • The researcher must stop if it turns dangerous

Eugenics & Carrie Buck (1927) Your body is yours (gap still open)
Nazi experiments → Nuremberg Code (1947) The subject’s free “yes” Voluntary consent is absolutely essential

“It Can’t Happen Here”

Why One Code Was Not Enough

  • Nuremberg came from judges, about Nazis — many U.S. doctors thought “not us”
  • The World Medical Association wrote the Declaration of Helsinki (1964) to make research ethics medicine’s own job (World Medical Association 1964)
  • But rules on paper do not help if no one outside the lab ever checks
  • Two American cases proved it

Willowbrook

  • Willowbrook was a vastly overcrowded New York institution for children with intellectual disabilities
  • Hepatitis spread constantly through its wards
  • Researchers deliberately gave the virus to children to study it and test a treatment
  • The defense offered: “they would have caught it here anyway”

Willowbrook State School administration building postcard, via Wikimedia Commons and the New York Public Library, public domain.

Was the Consent Real?

  • The institution was full
  • For a time, the one open door was the research ward
  • So parents were told, in effect: enroll your child, or wait years for a bed
  • A signature was collected. Was it a free choice?

Beecher’s Warning (1966)

  • Henry Beecher was a respected Harvard anesthesiologist — an insider, not a critic
  • In a leading medical journal he laid out 22 unethical studies already published in respected journals (Beecher 1966)
  • Examples: penicillin withheld from soldiers to study rheumatic fever; live cancer cells injected into chronically ill patients without their knowledge; the Willowbrook hepatitis studies themselves
  • These were funded, published, and praised — done by ordinary, successful researchers

What Beecher Actually Argued

  • His sharpest point: a signed form is not the real safeguard — consent can be an empty ritual
  • He noted the journals were complicit by publishing the work at all
  • He put more trust in a conscientious investigator than in paperwork
  • History drew a different lesson: character is not enough — review has to be structural (the coming IRB)

The tension is the teaching point: Beecher bet on individual virtue; the system that followed bet on structure. A good aside: do we actually need both, and does either work without the other?

Thought Question

Your child has a severe intellectual disability and you cannot care for him at home.

  • The only institution with an open bed will admit him — but only into the hepatitis research ward.
  • The wait for a non-research bed is years.
  1. You sign the form. Was your consent voluntary? What would have to be true for it to count?
  2. Researchers said the children “would have caught hepatitis here anyway.” Is that a good reason to deliberately give it to them?
  3. Suppose instead the research ward simply had better staffing and nicer conditions. Is that offer fine, or still a problem?

Make It Contemporary

The same shape recurs today:

  • A patient enrolls in a drug trial because it is the only way to afford a medication they need.
  • A Phase I study offers $5,000 to healthy volunteers; the people who sign up are mostly those who most need the money.

No one lied; everyone signed. Is the trial still taking advantage of these people?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three different failures hide in this one case — keep them apart:

  • Coercion — a threat (“no bed unless you enroll”). The choice is not free.
  • Undue inducement — an offer so strong it clouds judgment ($5,000; the better ward).
  • Exploitation — taking unfair advantage of someone’s bad situation, even with honest consent and no deception.

And the “they’d get it anyway” defense fails: a background risk does not license deliberately imposing harm (Jonsen 1998).

Forty Years: Tuskegee

Tuskegee: The Setup

  • Began in 1932 under the U.S. Public Health Service (Brandt 1978)
  • 600 Black men were enrolled in Alabama
  • 399 had syphilis
  • Researchers wanted to observe the untreated disease
  • The men were poor and medically underserved

CDC Archives. Public domain.

Tuskegee: The Deception

  • Men were not told they had syphilis
  • They were told they had “bad blood”
  • Free meals, exams, and burial insurance encouraged participation
  • The study presented itself as care
  • In reality, observation mattered more than the patients’ welfare

Tuskegee: Penicillin Withheld

  • Penicillin became standard therapy in the 1940s
  • Researchers still did not treat the men
  • They even interfered with outside access to care
  • The study continued toward autopsy, not cure
  • The subjects were being used as means, not ends

Tuskegee: Why It Lasted

  • Subjects were poor and politically weak
  • Racism made their suffering easier to ignore
  • The study was bureaucratically routine
  • Professional deference discouraged whistleblowing
  • There was no external review strong enough to stop it

Tuskegee: Public Reaction

  • Exposed by the press in 1972
  • Public outrage was immediate
  • Trust in medical institutions was deeply damaged
  • The damage was especially severe in Black communities
  • The U.S. could no longer rely on professional self-policing alone

Thought Question

What was the deepest wrong in Tuskegee?

  • The deception?
  • The withholding of treatment?
  • The racist choice of subjects?
  • The 40 years of institutional indifference?

You can argue for more than one answer, but which wrong seems most central?

Make It Contemporary

  • In 1997 the U.S. government formally apologized for Tuskegee — 25 years after it ended.
  • The justice worry has partly flipped: women and minority groups have often been left out of trials, so treatments are less well tested on them.

Is excluding a group from research a justice failure of the same family as Tuskegee, or a different kind of wrong?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Two payoffs worth the time:

  • Map each wrong to a principle. Deception → respect for persons; withheld penicillin → beneficence; choosing poor Black men → justice. The 40 years of silence maps to none of them cleanly — codes do not legislate moral courage.
  • Justice runs both ways. Tuskegee was wrongful inclusion under exploitation. Modern ethics also worries about wrongful exclusion — fair selection means neither dumping risk on the vulnerable nor denying them benefit.

Belmont and the Rise of Bioethics

The Word “Bioethics”

  • Van Rensselaer Potter popularized the term bioethics in 1970 (Potter 1970)
  • He imagined a bridge between science and human values
  • The field grew quickly in the 1970s
  • New centers formed at Hastings and Georgetown
  • Medicine needed public moral language, not just internal rules

National Research Act (1974)

  • Congress responded directly to the research ethics crisis
  • The Act created a national commission
  • The commission studied how to protect human subjects
  • The goal was not only punishment
  • The goal was a durable ethical framework

Belmont Report (1979)

  • The Belmont Report was published in 1979 (National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research 1979)
  • Short, influential, and still central
  • Not a checklist
  • Not a criminal code
  • A framework for thinking about research ethics

Respect for Persons

  • Respect for Persons starts from the idea that individuals are agents
  • Consent matters because persons are not tools
  • Those with diminished autonomy need added protection
  • This principle anchors informed consent
  • It carries the strongest anti-paternalist force

Beneficence

  • Beneficence means more than avoiding catastrophe
  • Also try to secure subjects’ welfare
  • Minimize likely harms
  • Maximize likely benefits
  • Make risk-benefit judgment explicit rather than casual

Justice

  • Justice asks who bears the burdens
  • Ask who receives the benefits
  • Ask whether easy-to-exploit groups are being targeted
  • Tuskegee is a classic failure of justice
  • Fair subject selection becomes an ethical requirement

Three Foundational Documents Compared

Document Origin Main emphasis Best one-line summary
Nuremberg Code War-crimes tribunal Voluntary consent Persons are not raw material
Helsinki Medical profession Research governance by physicians Research needs disciplined review
Belmont U.S. national commission Respect, beneficence, justice Turn principles into oversight

Bioethics Timeline

Oversight Today

What IRBs Do

  • IRBs review proposed human-subject research
  • Ask whether risks are minimized
  • Ask whether risks are justified
  • Ask whether subjects are selected fairly
  • Ask whether consent protections are adequate

How an IRB Thinks

Clinical Ethics Committees

  • IRBs govern research
  • Clinical ethics committees help with bedside conflict
  • They advise on capacity, surrogates, end-of-life decisions, and conflict
  • They are multidisciplinary by design
  • Their existence reflects distrust of one-person moral authority

Why This Still Matters at the Bedside

  • Research ethics changed clinical ethics too
  • Patients came to be seen as agents, not passive recipients
  • Consent, refusal, truth-telling, and confidentiality moved to the center
  • The old question was “What should the doctor do?”
  • The new question is “How should clinicians decide with patients?”

The Rulebook You Just Built

Together, these are why research today requires documented consent and independent review.

Wrapping Up Part A

Medical ethics moved from professional paternalism toward explicit respect for persons because abuse made the older model impossible to defend. Nuremberg named consent. Willowbrook and Beecher showed abuse can look respectable. Tuskegee exposed racism, deception, and 40 years of institutional failure. Belmont turned these lessons into a framework that still governs research and echoes through bedside care.
