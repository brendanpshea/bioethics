Lecture 6 — Genomics and Enhancement

Part B: Engineering Persons — CRISPR, Enhancement, and Identity

Author
Affiliation

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

1 A Clinic Visit That Sounds Like the Future

1.1 Would You Select?

You and your partner are doing IVF. Six embryos make it to the testing stage. The clinic offers a new add-on for $50,000. They will read each embryo’s whole genome and rank them on predicted IQ, height, and risk for common diseases. You can pick the embryo at the top of the list. The doctor stresses that it is legal, voluntary, and currently used by other couples in the clinic.

Write or discuss:

  1. Is this medical treatment, disease prevention, or something else? Does the label matter?
  2. The expected IQ gain from picking the top embryo is about 2 points. Does that change your answer?
  3. If you would not pay $50,000 for this, but it became free tomorrow, would you take it then?

The scenario is not hypothetical. US fertility clinics began offering whole-embryo polygenic scoring around 2020. The first business model is in slides 35–38. The point of the opener is to surface “what counts as treatment?” before naming it.

1.2 After You’ve Discussed

Note

The thought question turns on a distinction we will spend the lecture testing.

  • Treatment restores normal function — an antibiotic for an infection, insulin for diabetes.
  • Prevention keeps you out of harm — a vaccine, a statin.
  • Enhancement improves you beyond the normal range — sharper memory, taller body, longer life.

The line between these sounds clear. It is not. Most of today is about why the line keeps collapsing — and what is left to argue about once it does.

1.3 Today

By the end of Part B, you will be able to:

  • State three accounts of the treatment-enhancement distinction and explain why each one keeps collapsing.
  • Tell somatic from germline editing, and explain why this does most of the moral work in the gene-editing debate.
  • Describe what Casgevy (FDA 2023) does, and why somatic gene therapy is much less contested than germline editing.
  • Compare three positions on enhancement: Savulescu’s procreative beneficence, Sandel’s giftedness argument, and Habermas on making vs. growing.
  • Explain the non-identity problem and apply it to a real case.
  • Describe what polygenic embryo selection is doing in US fertility clinics today, and what it can and cannot deliver.

2 The Treatment-Enhancement Line

2.1 Why This Line Matters in Practice

The treatment-enhancement distinction is not just an academic puzzle. It does work in three concrete places.

  • Insurance. Health insurers pay for treatment. They do not pay for enhancement. Whether a procedure is one or the other decides who can afford it.
  • Professional duty. Doctors have an obligation to treat. They do not have an obligation to enhance. A patient cannot demand enhancement the way she can demand treatment.
  • Regulation. The FDA approves drugs as treatments for specific conditions. Off-label and enhancement uses fall outside its main review.

Move a procedure from one side of the line to the other, and three different systems respond.

2.2 A Working Definition

The standard definition in the philosophical literature is precise.

Enhancement = biomedical interventions used to improve human form or functioning beyond what is necessary to restore or sustain health.

A few implications travel with that definition.

  • It is the use, not the technology, that makes something an enhancement. The same drug can be treatment for one patient and enhancement for the next.
  • Most enhancement-capable drugs and procedures enter medicine first as treatments. They show up as enhancements later, off-label.
  • The definition rests on what counts as “health” — which is exactly the unstable concept the next three slides will probe. (Moseley and Juengst 2025)

2.3 Three Accounts at a Glance

Account What treatment is What enhancement is Who advocates
Professional-domain What the profession says it is What the profession refuses to do Practitioners; medical societies
Normal-function Restores species-typical function Pushes beyond the typical range Boorse 1977; Daniels
Disease-based Addresses a diagnosable malady Acts on a healthy system Gert, Culver, Clouser 2006

Each account does real work in clinical practice. Each one also keeps collapsing under pressure.

2.4 The Professional-Domain Account

The idea. Treatment is whatever doctors agree they should do. Enhancement is whatever they refuse. Medical societies set the line by professional consensus.

Why it collapses. Medicine has a track record of medicalization — absorbing new conditions into its scope as cultural demand grows. Shyness became social anxiety disorder. Restlessness in boys became ADHD. Aging in men became “low T.” Each was once outside medicine. Each is now inside.

If the profession’s own boundaries shift with demand, the profession’s word for what counts as treatment cannot be the bedrock of the distinction.

2.5 The Normal-Function Account

The idea (Boorse, Daniels)

Health is the ability to do what people of your age and sex can typically do. Treatment restores you to that range. Enhancement pushes you above it.

This account anchors the distinction in physiology, not professional opinion.

Where it collapses

  • Prevention. A vaccine pushes your immune response above normal on purpose. Statins lower cholesterol below the average for your age. Both look like enhancement under this account. Both are clearly treatment in practice.
  • Disability. A powered wheelchair that travels faster than a healthy walker does counts as enhancement here. Asking why is asking what is wrong with the account.

(Boorse 1977)

2.6 The Disease-Based Account

The idea (Gert, Culver, Clouser). Treatment addresses a malady — a condition that involves real harm and has no good external cause. Enhancement acts on a healthy system that has no malady to address.

Why it collapses. The list of recognized maladies keeps growing — and often grows to match what the profession (and the market) want to provide. This is nosological elasticity: coining new diseases to license new interventions.

The clearest current example is the next block of slides. ADHD diagnosis rates have moved in lockstep with stimulant prescriptions, which have moved in lockstep with classroom and workplace demand for focus. Each step was inside the disease-based account. The aggregate looks more like enhancement.

(Gert et al. 2006)

2.7 Why the Line Keeps Collapsing

Each account does something the other two cannot.

  • The professional-domain account is honest about who actually decides. It gives no principled answer.
  • The normal-function account is honest about biology. It collapses on prevention.
  • The disease-based account is honest about clinical practice. It collapses under medicalization.

The distinction does not vanish. It just stops being settle-able from the outside. Every concrete case in this lecture — Casgevy, Adderall, He Jiankui, polygenic IQ — runs the same argument again, on different ground.

That is the lecture’s spine. Watch for it. (Moseley and Juengst 2025)

3 From Somatic to Germline

3.1 Somatic vs. Germline

Somatic changes

  • In one tissue, in one person.
  • Not in sperm or egg cells.
  • Not inherited.
  • The change dies with the patient.
  • All currently approved gene therapies are somatic.

Germline changes

  • In every cell of the resulting person, including reproductive cells.
  • Heritable. Passed to children.
  • Each descendant inherits it.
  • Banned for clinical use in nearly every country.
  • The He Jiankui case is the only known human germline edit to date.

The same molecular tool can do either. The moral weight is in which cells it touches.

3.2 CRISPR-Cas9 — How It Works

CRISPR-Cas9 is, in plain terms, a programmable find-and-replace for DNA. You design a short guide molecule that matches the sequence you want to change. The guide drags Cas9 (a cutting enzyme) to that spot. Cas9 cuts. The cell tries to repair the cut, and the edit ends up in place.

Casgevy and the He Jiankui case both use this technology. The difference between them is somatic vs. germline.

3.3 Casgevy (FDA December 2023)

The first CRISPR-based therapy approved for clinical use in the US treats sickle-cell disease, an inherited blood disorder that causes severe pain crises and organ damage.

The procedure works like this. Doctors harvest the patient’s own stem cells. In the lab, they use CRISPR to switch on a gene that produces a different form of hemoglobin. The edited cells are then reinfused. The patient’s bone marrow makes the new hemoglobin from then on.

This is a somatic edit. It changes the patient. It does not change her future children.

3.4 Casgevy by the Numbers

  • Approved by the FDA in December 2023.
  • About 100,000 Americans live with sickle-cell disease. Most are Black.
  • The list price is roughly $2.2 million per patient.
  • Insurance coverage in early 2026 is uneven. Medicaid coverage varies by state.
  • The procedure requires weeks of chemotherapy to clear the marrow before the edited cells go back in. Most patients can only be treated at specialized centers.

The science is a triumph. The access question is a justice question. We will come back to it in the health-care lecture.

3.5 Luxturna and the Broader Picture

Luxturna (FDA 2017) treats an inherited form of blindness caused by a mutation in the RPE65 gene. A working copy of the gene is injected directly into the retina. About $425,000 per eye.

Several other somatic gene therapies are now approved or in late-stage trials — for hemophilia, for spinal muscular atrophy, for some inherited immune disorders.

All of them are clearly treatment under all three accounts of the line. None of the moral arguments later in this lecture apply to them.

The hard cases start when the same tools are used in embryos — or in healthy people.

3.6 He Jiankui — Briefly

What he did. In November 2018, Chinese scientist He Jiankui announced he had used CRISPR to edit the CCR5 gene in human embryos. He had implanted the embryos. Twin girls — Lulu and Nana — had been born. A third edited child was born shortly after.

His stated goal was to confer resistance to HIV. The edit was performed without serious clinical justification, with deeply flawed consent, and outside any sanctioned oversight.

He was sentenced to three years in prison in 2019. He was released in 2022 and has since attempted to return to research. The international scientific consensus against germline editing has held — for now.

Paired case: He Jiankui’s CRISPR Twins — the full case study, including the consent failures and the scientific community’s response.

4 Cognitive Enhancement

4.1 Off-Label Stimulants in Healthy Adults

The most common form of enhancement actually happening today does not involve embryos or CRISPR. It involves a prescription pad.

  • Roughly 1 in 6 US college students reports nonmedical use of stimulants like Adderall.
  • Rates are higher in pre-professional programs — nursing, medical, and law students above the average.
  • Modafinil, prescribed for narcolepsy, is now widely used off-label by shift workers, surgical residents, and military personnel.
  • Most users get their pills from a friend with a prescription. A growing number get them from telehealth services.

This is enhancement in plain sight. It is also enhancement that looks a lot like treatment from the outside.

4.2 What the Drugs Actually Do

The marketing implies a cognitive transformation. The evidence is more modest.

  • In diagnosed ADHD, stimulants improve attention and reduce disruptive symptoms in a clear, well-replicated way.
  • In healthy adults, the effects are small. Recent meta-analyses find a modest boost to simple attention tasks and a near-zero effect on complex problem-solving.
  • Side effects — insomnia, anxiety, raised heart rate, dependence — are real and dose-dependent.
  • The largest effect for healthy users is on perceived productivity. Subjects feel sharper, even when their actual performance does not move much.

This matters for the ethics. The drugs are not nothing. They are also not what the campus rumor says they are.

4.3 ADHD Diagnosis Over Time

Year US children diagnosed with ADHD Adult stimulant prescriptions
1997 ~3% uncommon
2007 ~7% rising
2016 ~10% common in adults
2022 ~11% sharply higher; telehealth-driven

The diagnosis rate has roughly tripled in twenty-five years. The criteria have broadened in each DSM revision since 1994.

Some of that increase is real recognition of a real condition. Some of it is the disease-based account collapsing in slow motion: as demand for the drug grew, the criteria for the diagnosis that justifies the drug expanded to match. Nosological elasticity in real time.

4.4 Thought Question — A Roommate’s NCLEX Request

Your roommate, a healthy nursing student with no ADHD diagnosis, asks to borrow one of your Adderall pills the night before NCLEX. She is anxious. She has heard from upperclassmen that “everyone uses it.” You are prescribed it for a diagnosis you have had since high school.

Write or discuss.

  1. Apply the three accounts of the treatment-enhancement line to her request. Which one makes the strongest case for “no”?
  2. Does the answer change if she says she just wants to try one pill at a normal dose?
  3. If you would say no to your roommate, would you say no to yourself using the same drug to gain an edge on the same test?

4.5 After You’ve Discussed

Note

The three accounts give three different reasons for “no” — and they cut at different angles.

  • Professional-domain: prescribing is a professional act. Sharing a prescription with someone undiagnosed is outside that act. The clinical relationship is what makes the use legitimate; outside that relationship, the same pill is something else.
  • Normal-function: the drug pushes attention above the normal range in a healthy user. That is enhancement, not treatment.
  • Disease-based: she has no malady. There is nothing to treat. The pill is acting on a healthy system to give her a competitive edge.

A fourth angle is equity. If passing NCLEX increasingly requires stimulants, the test starts measuring access to a diagnosis, not nursing knowledge.

5 The Non-Identity Problem

5.1 Parfit’s 14-Year-Old Girl

Important

The thought experiment

Imagine a 14-year-old girl who is thinking about having a child. If she has the child now, the child will have a difficult start — limited resources, an inexperienced parent, predictable hardships. If she waits until she is 22, she will be more prepared, more stable, and the child she has then will have a much better life.

It seems obvious that waiting is the right thing to do.

But here is the puzzle. The child she would have at 14 is not the same person as the child she would have at 22. Different egg, different sperm, different person. If she waits, the first child never exists. He doesn’t get a better life; he simply does not get a life at all.

— Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (1984)

5.2 The Puzzle Stated Sharply

So who was wronged by the early pregnancy?

  • Not the actual child. His alternative was nonexistence. He is glad to be alive. He has no complaint.
  • Not the never-existing child. That child has no complaints, because that child never existed.

The puzzle is real. We feel a strong intuition that the early pregnancy was wrong. The standard person-affecting reasoning cannot say who was wronged. (Parfit 1984)

This matters everywhere we choose the conditions of someone’s existence — embryo selection, germline editing, even decisions about when to conceive.

5.3 He Jiankui’s Twins Through This Lens

Lulu and Nana exist only because their parents took part in He Jiankui’s experiment. A different embryo would have meant a different child — or no child at all.

So on the simplest reading: the twins were not wronged by being edited. The alternative was that they not exist.

That cannot be where the analysis ends. Most people share a strong sense that what He did was wrong. The non-identity problem forces us to locate the wrong somewhere other than “harm to the specific child who exists.”

Two candidate locations show up later in the lecture: a wrong to the kind of relationship parents and children should have (Sandel, Habermas), and a wrong to the future adult who was set up without consent (Feinberg, Habermas).

6 The Enhancement Debate

6.1 The Central Question

If safe, accurate gene editing or embryo selection were available — and many forms already are — should parents use them to give their children advantages they would not otherwise have?

Three serious answers. Each is held by careful philosophers. None is fringe.

  • Savulescu: yes, and parents have a positive duty to do so.
  • Sandel: no, because doing so corrodes something valuable in the parent-child relationship.
  • Habermas: no, because the child cannot consent to being someone else’s project.

6.2 Savulescu — Procreative Beneficence

The argument. If you are going to bring a child into existence, you have a moral reason to bring the child with the best expected life. The reasoning is straightforward consequentialism: more well-being is better than less well-being, all else equal.

The argument applies the same way to disease risk and to traits. If a parent should pick the embryo less likely to develop cancer, the parent should also pick the embryo more likely to be tall, intelligent, or cheerful — to the extent those traits track well-being.

Savulescu calls this procreative beneficence. It is the most permissive position in the contemporary literature.

(Savulescu 2001)

6.3 Sandel — Verbatim

“To appreciate children as gifts is to accept them as they come, not as objects of our design, or products of our will, or instruments of our ambition. Parental love is not contingent on the talents and attributes the child happens to have.”

— Michael Sandel, The Case Against Perfection: Ethics in the Age of Genetic Engineering (2007), p. 45.

(Sandel 2007)

6.4 Sandel’s Giftedness Argument

The argument is not that enhancement makes children worse off. It often does not. The argument is about what enhancement does to the parent and to the practice of parenting.

  • A parent who accepts the child as a gift can love the child unconditionally. Effort goes into the child’s flourishing on the child’s own terms.
  • A parent who designs the child treats the child as a project. Love starts to track how well the project is going.
  • Beyond the family, a society of designed children loses what Sandel calls solidarity — the sense that we are all subject to the same lottery and owe each other something for it.

The argument is not against medicine. It is against the idea that parenting is a manufacturing problem.

6.5 Habermas — Making vs. Growing

For Habermas, the wrong of enhancement is not in the child’s outcomes. It is in the kind of being a designed child is.

  • A child who grew — under nature’s contingency — can come to understand her life as her own. The traits she has are not someone else’s choice.
  • A child who was made — selected or edited toward a specific design — is, in a real sense, the project of her parents’ lives. She cannot retroactively consent to that. She cannot un-design herself.

This is not a religious argument. It is an argument about the structure of autonomy. The future adult is owed a life that is hers, not an extension of someone else’s plan.

The He Jiankui twins are the sharpest test case. They exist only because of the edit. They were also, in Habermas’s terms, made in a way no child should be. (Habermas 2003)

6.6 Three Positions at a Glance

Savulescu Sandel Habermas
Enhancement is A duty, where it improves the child’s life A corrosion of the parent-child relationship A wrong to the future autonomous adult
What’s lost without it Well-being the child could have had Nothing — the giftedness is the point Nothing — the autonomy is the point
What’s lost with it Nothing intrinsic; only access concerns Unconditional love; social solidarity The child’s authorship of her own life
Polygenic IQ scoring is Permissible; perhaps obligatory if affordable The textbook example of the drive to mastery The clearest case of making a person

All three can agree that Casgevy is fine. They split sharply on embryo selection.

7 Feinberg’s Open Future, Applied to the Body

7.1 Open Future, Now for the Body

Part A used Feinberg’s argument about a child’s right to an open future for the case of genetic information — testing a healthy child for an adult-onset disease.

Part B applies the same principle to genetic engineering. The principle is the same: do not foreclose, in advance, choices the future adult should be free to make on her own.

  • The informational version says: don’t tell the eight-year-old about her BRCA risk before she can decide whether she wants to know.
  • The engineering version says: don’t build the eight-year-old toward a specific future before she can decide what kind of future she wants.

The argument is consistent with letting the same person, at 25, walk into a clinic and choose. (Feinberg 1980)

7.2 The Future Person Who Never Consented

Habermas and Feinberg meet at the He Jiankui twins.

  • Habermas: they were made, in a way that makes their lives the project of someone else’s plan.
  • Feinberg: a future they might have chosen for themselves was foreclosed in the embryo dish.
  • Non-identity problem: they cannot complain that they were harmed, because the alternative was nonexistence.

The wrong, then, is not to the twins-vs-no-twins comparison. It is to the kind of relationship He Jiankui’s experiment set up between him, the parents, and two future adults who could never have agreed to be designed this way.

That is the cleanest formulation in the literature. It is also why germline editing remains banned in clinical use almost everywhere.

8 Polygenic Embryo Selection

8.1 What Polygenic Embryo Selection Is

No editing. No CRISPR. Just selection.

The clinic creates embryos through IVF, tests each one, calculates a polygenic risk score for whatever traits the parents are paying to assess, and lets the parents choose which embryo to implant.

The technology has been used to screen out single-gene diseases (Tay-Sachs, cystic fibrosis) for decades. What is new is selecting on risk of common disease — and on traits like height or predicted IQ.

8.2 From Disease Screening to Trait Selection

The standard preimplantation test screens for things almost no one wants — a fatal childhood disease, a chromosomal abnormality. The new market screens for things that look more like preferences.

  • Risk of adult heart disease.
  • Risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Predicted height.
  • Predicted educational attainment.
  • Predicted IQ.

Each step looks like a small extension of the last. The aggregate has crossed a line the field used to police more carefully. There is no US federal law against it.

8.3 Orchid Health and Heliospect

Two US companies are the live edge of the field.

  • Orchid Health (San Francisco) offers what it calls “whole-genome embryo screening” — sequencing every embryo and reporting risk scores across hundreds of conditions, including some adult-onset ones. Prices range from a few thousand dollars per embryo upward.
  • Heliospect Genomics offers polygenic embryo selection that explicitly includes scoring for IQ. Reporting by The Guardian in 2024 documented the service in detail.

Both operate legally in the US. The market is small but growing. Neither product is available in most European countries.

8.4 How Accurate Is a Polygenic IQ Score?

The marketing implies precision. The science is humbler.

  • Current polygenic scores for IQ explain about 10–15% of the variance in intelligence-test performance. The rest is environment, gene-environment interaction, and noise.
  • For a couple selecting among 6–10 sibling embryos, the expected IQ gain from picking the top-ranked one is roughly 2–4 points. Within the noise of the test itself.
  • Predictions work best in the population the score was trained on — overwhelmingly people of European ancestry, as Part A noted.
  • Almost everything that makes a person’s life go well — temperament, motivation, the household they grow up in — is not in the score.

The product on offer is not a designer baby. It is a small statistical nudge sold with the language of choice.

8.5 Thought Question — IQ at $50,000

You and your partner can afford the $50,000 selection package. The clinic shows you six embryos ranked by predicted IQ. The top embryo is 2.3 points higher than the bottom one. The clinic counselor says “most of our families choose the top.”

Write or discuss.

  1. Do you select on IQ? Why or why not?
  2. Suppose the service became free tomorrow. Does your answer change?
  3. Suppose the gap were 10 points instead of 2.3. Does your answer change?
  4. If you would not select for IQ, would you select for low risk of type 2 diabetes? What is different about the two choices?

8.6 After You’ve Discussed

Note

A few distinctions worth carrying away.

  • What is actually selected. The “best” embryo is best only in expectation, and the expectation is small. The choice is a probability bet, not a guarantee.
  • What is left out. Most of what shapes a child’s life is environment and chance — neither of which is in the score. Selection promises agency over a slice of the outcome and quiet about the rest.
  • Where the three positions land. Savulescu says select. Sandel says you have just defined the parent-child relationship in terms of a 2-point statistical edge. Habermas says you have committed a future adult to a project she cannot un-author.
  • Equity. The service is currently sold at price points that exclude most American families. If it works at all, it works to widen advantage that money already buys.

9 Wrap

9.1 Scoreboard — The Treatment-Enhancement Collapse

Where it shows up Looks like treatment because Looks like enhancement because
Casgevy for sickle cell Treats a clear malady; restores normal hemoglobin Permanent edit; price puts it out of reach for most patients
Adderall off-label Same molecule as the prescription drug Acts on a healthy brain; gives a competitive edge
He Jiankui’s CCR5 edit Framed as HIV prevention Healthy embryos; no clear medical need; heritable
Polygenic disease screening Selects against future illness Treats embryos as products to be ranked
Polygenic IQ selection Sold as “best chance for your child” No malady; pure trait selection
Future germline editing Could prevent inherited disease Could redesign human capacities

Six cases. One unstable line. That is what the lecture has been arguing.

9.2 Review Questions

Write a brief answer to each, or use them as discussion prompts.

Recall

State the three accounts of the treatment-enhancement distinction in your own words. For each, name one concrete example where it gives an unhelpful answer.

Apply

A young woman with sickle-cell disease is considering Casgevy, but her state’s Medicaid program may not cover it. Name two things she needs to understand about the treatment, and one ethical issue that goes beyond her individual case.

Debate

A US state legislature is considering a bill that would ban polygenic embryo selection for non-disease traits in fertility clinics. Take a position. Identify the strongest argument against your position and respond to it.
