Part A: Reading Genomes — Prediction, Privacy, and Dual Use

Brendan Shea, PhD

An Introduction to Bioethics.

A Newborn on the Postpartum Floor

Would You Sign?

You are a parent on the postpartum floor, twelve hours after a healthy delivery. A research nurse offers to read your newborn’s whole genome at no cost. The report, returned in two weeks, will list gene variants linked to several hundred conditions. Some are treatable in childhood. Some show up only in adulthood. Some are of uncertain meaning. Your partner is leaning yes. You want to ask more questions first.

Write or discuss:

  1. Which of those three kinds of result would you want to receive, and which would you rather not know?
  2. Does your answer change if the test is offered at the six-year visit instead?
  3. Whose decision is this — yours, your partner’s, or the child’s own, twenty years from now?

Resist the urge to settle the question. The point is to surface the time-shape of genetic information: a decision the parents make today binds the child for life.

After You’ve Discussed

Note

The thought experiment turns on a distinction we will return to all lecture.

  • Actionable now — a finding the family can act on in childhood (a treatable metabolic condition).
  • Actionable later — a finding that matters in adulthood, when the child can decide for herself whether to know (BRCA1, late-onset Alzheimer’s risk).
  • Informative but not actionable — a finding that names a risk no one can act on yet (some Huntington’s variants in a healthy infant).

Today

  • Define geneticization and use it to name a pattern that runs through prenatal testing, ancestry kits, and forensic genealogy.
  • Explain why a “reference genome” built mostly from one or two people is not a neutral starting point, and what an ancestry bias in genetic prediction looks like in practice.
  • State the NASEM 2023 headline rule on race and genetic variation, and apply it to a real research scenario.
  • Describe what GINA (2008) covers and what it does not, and name two state-level fixes for the gap.

The Human Genome Project: Promise and Aftermath

A Thirteen-Year Arc

The HGP was a public, international project led by the NIH and the Department of Energy. The UK’s Wellcome Trust Sanger Centre was the largest non-US partner. The project began in 1990 and was meant to finish by 2005. In 1998, a private company — Celera Genomics, run by Craig Venter — said it would finish first using a faster method. The race ended in a tie at a White House press conference in June 2000.

The Public-Celera Race

The public effort (HGP)

  • Funded by NIH, DOE, and the Wellcome Trust.
  • Clone-by-clone method: cut the genome into mapped chunks, sequence each, reassemble.
  • Slower, but more careful with repeats.
  • Data released to public databases each night.

Celera Genomics

  • Privately funded; planned to sell access to the result.
  • Whole-genome shotgun method: break the genome into random fragments, sequence in bulk, reassemble by computer.
  • Faster and cheaper, but leaned on the public data to fill gaps.
  • Backed off its patent plan under public pressure.

Both finished. The shotgun method won the science fight; the public group won the policy fight.

Collins 1999, Verbatim

“By the year 2010, it is expected that predictive genetic tests will be available for as many as a dozen common conditions, allowing individuals who wish to know this information to learn their individual susceptibilities and to take steps to reduce those risks for which interventions are or will be available. Such interventions could take the form of medical surveillance, lifestyle modifications, diet, or drug therapy.”

— Francis S. Collins, “Medical and Societal Consequences of the Human Genome Project,” NEJM 341 (1999): 28.

(Collins 1999)

Promised vs. Delivered

Collins predicted by 2010 What 2026 actually has
Routine cheek-swab risk prediction for common diseases A small set of strong single-gene tests; broad risk prediction still uneven
Drugs dosed by genotype A few dozen drug-gene pairs in use; far fewer than promised
Twelve common conditions with usable predictive tests Several, but most predict only modest risk
Genetic privacy laws “fully in place” GINA 2008 covers health insurance and employment only
Public catching up with the science Ancestry-kit marketing still routinely overpromises

The gap is not a failure of the project. It is the lecture’s first lesson about how genetic claims travel.

“Missing Heritability”

What GWAS expected and what it found

A genome-wide association study (GWAS) lines up the DNA of people who have a trait — height, diabetes, schizophrenia — against people who don’t, and looks for variants that show up more often in one group. By 2010, GWAS had been run on hundreds of thousands of people.

Twin and family studies had predicted that most variation in these traits was heritable, and that GWAS would find a few moderately strong variants. What it found instead were thousands of variants of tiny effect — which still leave most of the predicted heritability unexplained. That gap is the missing heritability problem. It is part of why “cheek-swab risk prediction” did not arrive on the 1999 schedule.

Geneticization

Lippman Names the Tendency

“Geneticization refers to an ongoing process by which differences between individuals are reduced to their DNA codes, with most disorders, behaviours and physiological variations defined, at least in part, as genetic in origin.… It refers as well to the process by which interventions employing genetic technologies are adopted to manage problems of health.”

— Abby Lippman, “Prenatal Genetic Testing and Screening,” American Journal of Law & Medicine 17 (1991): 19.

(Lippman 1991; Gannett 2023)

Three Related Pulls

Geneticization — the tendency to cast human differences, risks, and identities as mostly genetic. A cultural and rhetorical pattern, not a scientific claim.

Genetic reductionism — the working assumption that the right level to explain a trait is the gene. Sometimes useful; often overshoots.

Genetic determinism — the stronger claim that genes fix outcomes — that DNA is destiny. Almost no working geneticist believes this in its strong form. It lives mostly in popular framings and marketing copy.

The three travel together. Catch one and you usually catch the others.

Gene × Environment — the Working Picture

The textbook case is phenylketonuria (PKU). It causes severe intellectual disability — but only if the diet contains a substance called phenylalanine. Find the gene at birth, change the diet, and the disability never appears. Same DNA, different outcome.

Most diseases the HGP was meant to crack — diabetes, heart disease, depression — sit closer to the PKU end than the popular framing suggests. Genes matter. So does what you live next to, eat, and can afford.

The Vocabulary You Need

Four Words

Term What it means
Gene A stretch of DNA that codes for a working product (usually a protein). Humans have about 20,000.
Allele One of the possible versions of a gene at a given spot. You get one from each parent.
Genome The full set of DNA in an organism’s cells — about 3.2 billion base pairs in humans.
Variant Any place where one person’s DNA differs from a reference sequence. Most variants do nothing visible. A few do a lot.

The older word mutation is still used, but variant has largely replaced it. “Mutation” carries an unhelpful hint that the difference is always bad.

Germline vs. Somatic

Germline

  • Present in the egg or sperm — and so in every cell of the resulting person.
  • Heritable: passed to children.
  • A germline edit changes one person and every descendant.

Somatic

  • A change in one tissue, in one person, in non-reproductive cells.
  • Not heritable: dies with the patient.
  • Most cancers are somatic. So are most approved gene therapies (Casgevy for sickle-cell disease, for example).

This distinction does most of the moral work in Part B. Editing one patient’s bone marrow is not the same as editing every cell in every descendant.

Whose Genome Is the Reference?

The Reference Is a Composite

When a clinical lab says a patient has “a variant,” it means the patient’s DNA differs in one spot from the human reference genome. The reference is a published sequence anyone can look up. Everyone else’s DNA gets compared against it.

The reference is a composite. It is stitched together from cell lines that came from several anonymous donors. It is not the genome of any one person.

That choice matters, because most of the reference is from very few of those donors. (Gannett 2023)

The Buffalo Man and Craig Venter

Important

Where the reference actually comes from

The public HGP reference is roughly 74% from one anonymous male donor. He was known internally as “RP11” because his cells became the most-used cell line at Roswell Park Cancer Institute in Buffalo, New York. He was paid $50, signed an anonymous consent form, and never knew he was the main donor.

The Celera reference is roughly 71% from Craig Venter himself. Venter quietly put his own DNA into the donor pool and confirmed it in public in 2002.

For about twenty years, the standard against which billions of human genomes have been compared was therefore mostly two men.

(Gannett 2023)

Why It Matters: Ancestry Bias

GWAS finds variants by comparing thousands of cases to thousands of controls. If those controls are mostly of one ancestry, the variants the study finds are the ones that matter in that group. The predictions built from them work best in that group too.

Where GWAS participants come from (cumulative, mid-2010s):

  • ~80% European ancestry
  • ~10% Asian ancestry
  • ~3% African ancestry
  • ~1% Hispanic / Latin American
  • Remainder: everyone else

What that means in the clinic:

A polygenic risk score for coronary disease trained on this data is about 4–5× more accurate in people of European ancestry than in people of African ancestry.

The score is not biased because the math is bad. It is biased because the training data was.

(Popejoy and Fullerton 2016)

Toward a Pangenome

The fix is not to “patch the reference.” It is to replace one reference with many. A pangenome reference is a set of high-quality genomes from people around the world, queried as a group rather than as one string.

The first draft human pangenome was published in 2023. Its 47 starting genomes are still only a fraction of human variation. The shift — from one reference to many — closes the bias gap at the root rather than patching it downstream.

Prenatal Testing

Three Kinds of Genetic Testing

Type Who is tested What it tells you
Diagnostic A patient with symptoms Whether their disease has a known genetic cause
Predictive A healthy person at risk Their chance of developing a disease later
Reproductive A future parent, fetus, or embryo Whether a future person is likely to be affected

The same lab test can be used for any of the three. What changes is who is asking, on whose behalf, and what they will do with the answer.

NIPT — How It Works

Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT) reads bits of fetal DNA that float in the mother’s blood. It is available from about ten weeks of pregnancy. NIPT is screening, not diagnosis. A positive result raises the chance the fetus has Down syndrome but does not confirm it.

A confirming test — usually amniocentesis at 15–18 weeks — is needed before any permanent decision.

NIPT, Down Syndrome, and the >90% Question

NIPT has been offered to nearly all pregnant patients in the US since the mid-2010s, regardless of age. After a confirmed Down syndrome diagnosis, the US termination rate is above 60%. In Denmark and Iceland, where first-trimester screening is universal, the rate is close to 95%.

A question that returns later in the course

The disability scholar Adrienne Asch argued that aborting a fetus diagnosed with Down syndrome is not the same as the general right to abortion. The grounds matter, she said. “We don’t want a child like that” sends a message to people already living with the condition.

This lecture does not settle the argument. The disability-rights treatment belongs to a later lecture. The point here is that NIPT is the technology that made the question scale — from rare diagnosis to a routine offer.

Predictive Testing and the Open Future

BRCA in Adults

The genes BRCA1 and BRCA2 are tumor-suppressor genes. Certain variants in either raise lifetime risk of breast and ovarian cancer by a lot. Among Ashkenazi Jewish women, three specific variants account for most of the inherited risk. Predictive testing in healthy adult women is, by now, routine.

What a positive result changes for an adult woman is concrete: more frequent screening, the option of preventive surgery in her thirties or forties, sometimes drug therapy. The decision is hers, with a genetic counselor.

The adult case is largely a settled use of informed consent. The child case is not.

Testing Healthy Children for Adult-Onset Conditions

BRCA in a healthy eight-year-old

  • The risk is real, but does not become actionable until her twenties or thirties.
  • The result will follow her through every insurance form and school record for two decades before she can act on it.
  • The American Society of Human Genetics has long advised against testing children for adult-onset conditions with no childhood treatment.

Huntington’s in a healthy eight-year-old

  • A near-certain prediction of a fatal brain disease in midlife.
  • No treatment. No prevention.
  • The professional view is even firmer: do not test.
  • Yet some adult Huntington’s patients say they wish they had been told earlier. Others say the opposite.

The same rule, applied twice, gives the same answer for different reasons.

Feinberg’s “Open Future,” Applied to Information

Feinberg’s right to an open future

The legal philosopher Joel Feinberg argued that children hold a class of “rights in trust.” These are autonomy rights they cannot yet use, but which adults must keep open on their behalf. Parental choices that close off a future the child might have chosen are, on this view, a distinct kind of wrong.

The first uses were about things like religion and schooling. Applied to genetic information, the rule is simple: don’t fix the meaning of an adult-onset risk before the future adult can decide whether to know it.

The same adult, twenty years later, can walk into a clinic and ask.

Newborn Genomic Sequencing — A Live Debate

BabySeq and GUARDIAN

Two long-running US studies are quietly normalizing the choice we opened with.

  • BabySeq (Boston, since 2015) randomly assigned newborns to whole-genome reading plus normal care, or normal care alone. It tracked what families and pediatricians did with the results.
  • GUARDIAN (New York, since 2022) is enrolling a much larger group. It returns about 150–250 conditions, all chosen for childhood treatability.

Neither study has reported major harm. Both report that families want the results — even for adult-onset conditions the researchers were nervous about returning.

ACMG vs. AAP — Two Positions

American College of Medical Genetics American Academy of Pediatrics
Default frame Sequencing is cheap; expanding the panel of conditions returned is the responsible move The longstanding rule against testing healthy children should not erode with technology
What to report A regularly updated list of “medically actionable” findings, including some adult-onset Conditions with a childhood treatment, by preference
Why Information delayed is treatments missed Information returned is futures narrowed
Direction of pressure Expand the panel Hold the line

Both groups are firmly mainstream. They disagree, in public, about what to do with the next four million newborns.

Direct-to-Consumer Testing

23andMe by the Numbers

  • Founded 2006; cleared by the FDA as a home health test in 2017.
  • About 14 million customers by 2023, most for ancestry rather than health.
  • A saliva tube; about $100; results in six weeks.
  • Customers can opt their DNA into research; about 80% historically have.
  • Filed for bankruptcy in 2025; the data itself is still a corporate asset under sale.

The product is two products in one box: a recreational ancestry report, and a clinical-grade test for a small set of disease variants.

The 2023 Breach

In October 2023, attackers got into 23andMe accounts by reusing passwords leaked from other sites. Because many customers had opted into a feature called “DNA Relatives,” each broken account also exposed information about that user’s relatives — relatives who had never been hacked themselves.

About 6.9 million users were affected. Lists targeting Ashkenazi Jewish and Chinese-ancestry customers were posted on hacker forums. The company settled in late 2024 for about $30 million.

Race and Genetics: The NASEM Framework

Why This Comes Up Here

Race is not the topic of this lecture. The technology puts it on the table all the same, in two places.

  • Ancestry kits: a customer is told she is “23% Italian” with a precision the science does not support. The reference panels behind those percentages are social-political artifacts, not natural categories.
  • Genetic research: studies often report results by self-reported race, treating race as a stand-in for genetic variation. The 2023 National Academies report says that is a mistake.

The field has argued about race and genetics since the HGP itself. The latest professional consensus is short and specific.

NASEM 2023 — The Headline Rule

Important

Recommendation 1. “Researchers should not use race as a proxy for human genetic variation. In particular, researchers should not assign genetic ancestry group labels to individuals or sets of individuals based on their race, whether self-identified or not.”

— National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, Using Population Descriptors in Genetics and Genomics Research (2023), Summary, Rec. 1.

The reasoning, in one line: race is a social-political category. It shifts by country and by era. Human genetic variation is continuous, not discrete. Mapping the first onto the second produces errors that go in predictable directions. The same report retires Caucasian from genetic research — a term coined in 1795 by Blumenbach to rank a skull he found beautiful, carrying no genetic information then or now. (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2023)

Population Descriptors at a Glance

Descriptor What it captures When to use it
Race A social-political category about perceived ancestry; shifts by country and era Not as a stand-in for genetic variation; sometimes as a stand-in for social exposure (with a stated reason)
Ethnicity A social-political category about shared cultural heritage For cultural features, not genetic ones
Geography Where a person’s recent ancestors lived Useful to describe sampling; not a stand-in for ancestry
Genetic ancestry The paths through a family tree by which DNA was passed down When the study question is about ancestry as such
Genetic similarity A number measuring how genetically alike two people are The preferred tool for most population comparisons

Adapted from NASEM 2023, Table S-1.

Forensic Genetic Genealogy

GEDmatch and the Golden State Killer

Important

Joseph James DeAngelo, 2018

Between 1973 and 1986, one offender committed at least 13 murders and dozens of rapes across California. The case went cold for thirty years. In 2018, a Sacramento team uploaded a DNA profile from a 1980 crime scene to GEDmatch, an open genealogy site used mostly by hobbyist family-tree researchers.

The profile matched several of DeAngelo’s distant cousins. None of them had any idea their saliva tubes could finger a relative for murder. Investigators built family trees from the matches, narrowed to DeAngelo, picked up DNA from his car door handle, and arrested him.

He pleaded guilty in 2020.

How Long-Range Familial Search Works

The method works because of one fact about DNA. A third cousin shares about 0.8% of their DNA with you, in long, readable chunks. With a database of a few million users, some third or fourth cousin of any given American is almost certainly in it.

Your choice to stay out of the database does not, by itself, keep you out of searches it enables.

The Law Catches Up

Year Action
2018 DeAngelo arrest brings the method to public attention
2019 US Department of Justice issues an interim policy for federal use — only with prosecutor approval, only for violent crimes
2021 Maryland becomes the first US state to regulate forensic genealogy by statute — court approval required, limited crimes
2021 Montana passes a tighter law requiring a search warrant
2023– Other state legislatures consider similar bills; most US states still have no law

The federal courts have not yet ruled on whether a Fourth Amendment “search” happens when investigators query a database your cousin uploaded.

Thought Question — Whose Consent?

Your second cousin — someone you have met twice in your life — uploads her saliva to a genealogy site. Six years later, investigators use her upload to identify you as a suspect in a thirty-year-old assault case. They are right; you did it. You are arrested, charged, and convicted.

Write or discuss.

  1. Did you have a privacy interest that was violated, given that your own DNA was never tested?
  2. Does the answer change if you are innocent and were briefly a suspect before being cleared?
  3. Does it change if the crime is a serial murder vs. a thirty-year-old simple assault?

GINA and the Gap

GINA 2008 — The Federal Floor

The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, signed in May 2008, was the first US federal law aimed at genetic discrimination. It does two things, and only two.

  • Health insurance: insurers may not use a person’s genetic information to set eligibility, premiums, or coverage. They may not request or require it.
  • Employment: employers with 15 or more workers may not use genetic information in hiring, firing, or promotion.

That is the federal floor. Everyone in the US is covered by it. The areas it does not reach are large.

The Four-Cell Gap

Insurance line Covered by GINA?
Health insurance Yes — cannot use genetic information
Life insurance No — may request it and price on it
Disability insurance No
Long-term-care insurance No
Employer category Covered by GINA?
Civilian employer, ≥15 employees Yes
Civilian employer, <15 employees No
US military No — service members may be reassigned or separated on genetic findings

Anyone who tells a patient “you are protected against genetic discrimination” is, strictly, telling them about half the truth.

Florida 2020 and the State-Level Patchwork

In 2020, Florida became the first US state to extend protection to life, disability, and long-term-care insurance — partly closing the GINA gap inside its own borders. A few other states have passed narrower versions. Many more have tried and failed.

Federal law has not moved since 2008. For now, the action is in state legislatures and in the actuarial departments of life insurers. Both know that a whole-genome reading for under a thousand dollars is on the near horizon for every American applicant.

Key Terms

  • Geneticization — Lippman’s term for the cultural tendency to explain human differences, risks, and identities primarily in genetic terms, and to favor genetic interventions as the preferred solution to health problems.
  • Genetic determinism — the strong claim that genes fix outcomes — that DNA is destiny — regardless of environment; almost no working geneticist defends it in this form, but it persists in popular and marketing framings.
  • Germline vs. somatic — germline changes occur in reproductive cells and are inherited by every descendant; somatic changes occur in non-reproductive tissue of one person and die with that person.
  • Genome-wide association study (GWAS) — a research method that scans the DNA of thousands of people with and without a trait to identify variants statistically linked to it; results work best in the ancestry group the study was run on.
  • Polygenic risk score — a number calculated by adding up the small effects of thousands of variants across the genome to estimate an individual’s risk for a complex trait; accuracy depends heavily on whether the training population matches the person being scored.
  • NIPT — non-invasive prenatal testing; reads fragments of fetal DNA floating in the mother’s blood from about ten weeks of pregnancy to screen for chromosomal conditions like trisomy 21; a screening result, not a diagnosis, and requires confirmatory testing before any permanent decision.
  • Open future (Feinberg) — Feinberg’s principle that children hold autonomy rights “in trust”; adults must keep those futures open rather than foreclosing choices — including the choice of whether to know one’s genetic risks — before the child can make them herself.
  • Population descriptor (NASEM) — a label used to group people in genetics research; the 2023 NASEM report recommends using geographic ancestry or genetic similarity rather than race as a stand-in for genetic variation, since race is a social-political category, not a biological one.
  • Investigative genetic genealogy — a forensic technique that uploads a crime-scene DNA profile to a consumer genealogy database, identifies distant-cousin matches among users, and builds a family tree to narrow to a suspect; used to solve the Golden State Killer case in 2018.
  • GINA — the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (2008); bars health insurers and employers from using genetic information against people, but does not cover life, disability, or long-term-care insurance, or the US military.
