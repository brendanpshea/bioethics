Lecture 3 — Research Ethics

Part B: From Tuskegee to Today: Protecting Research Subjects

Author

Brendan Shea, PhD

Published

May 18, 2026

1 Where We Left Off

1.1 The New Question

  • Part A explained how trials work
  • The rules that protect participants were written after real abuses
  • This part puts those rules to work on hard, current cases
  • The hard question: when does reviewed, legal, well-funded research still wrong people?

1.2 Today

By the end of Part B, you will be able to:

  • Apply the Belmont principles as concrete IRB review questions
  • Identify vulnerable populations and the protections they require
  • Judge when placebo controls are and are not ethically defensible
  • Analyze the short-course AZT trials as a justice debate
  • Explain why research on tissue and data (HeLa) needs its own consent rules
  • Describe recent developments: the rewritten Common Rule, trial-diversity law, and research misconduct

2 The Cases That Built the Rules

2.1 Three Abuses, Three Protections

Modern research protections were built one scandal at a time.

Case The wrong What it forced into place
Nazi experiments No real consent; extreme harm Voluntary consent; Nuremberg (Nuremberg Military Tribunals 1947)
Willowbrook Dependent children, coercive setting Vulnerable-population protection; independent review (Jonsen 1998)
Tuskegee Deception, withheld treatment, racist selection Belmont: respect, beneficence, justice (Brandt 1978; National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research 1979)

Each row is a failure on the left and the safeguard it forced on the right.

2.2 Thought Question

What was the deepest wrong shared by these cases?

  • People were used as means to an end
  • People could not refuse freely
  • Powerful institutions targeted the vulnerable
  • Researchers treated knowledge as more important than persons
  1. Which answer seems most central to you, and why?
  2. Are these really different wrongs, or just different ways of naming the same one?
  3. If you had to design one protection first, which wrong would you aim it at?

2.3 Make It Contemporary

Today the abuse is less likely to look like camps or segregated wards.

  • It may look like a confusing consent form.
  • It may look like recruiting the people least able to say no.
  • It may look like calling exploitation “access.”

If a study is legal, reviewed, and well funded, what warning signs would still make you uneasy?

2.4 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three distinctions are worth keeping sharp:

  • Consent failures, harm failures, and justice failures are not identical. Tuskegee had all three.
  • These cases are not only about cruelty. They are also about institutions finding reassuring language for doing the wrong thing.
  • Modern oversight exists because good intentions are not enough. The whole point is to distrust self-justification before the damage is done.

3 Belmont, Put to Work

3.1 Belmont in One Slide

  • The Belmont Report (1979) named three principles (National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research 1979)
  • Respect for persons → real consent; extra care where autonomy is reduced
  • Beneficence → minimize harm; justify risk against benefit
  • Justice → who bears the burdens, who gets the benefits
  • Each becomes a concrete question an IRB must ask of every study

3.2 One Map from Principle to Protection

3.3 You Are the IRB

This protocol lands on your desk:

A 12-month placebo-controlled trial of a new Alzheimer’s drug. Participants: residents of one memory-care facility. Consent: signed by adult children as surrogates. Site chosen because “follow-up is easy — they don’t move.”

  1. Score it on each Belmont principle. Where is it weakest?
  2. What single change would you require before approving it?
  3. Is “easy follow-up” ever a legitimate reason to pick a population?

3.4 After You’ve Discussed

Note
  • Respect for persons: surrogate consent is allowed, but the residents’ own assent — and dissent — still count. A child’s signature is not the whole story.
  • Beneficence: a 12-month placebo arm in a progressive disease needs serious justification (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office for Human Research Protections 2026).
  • Justice: “they don’t move” is convenience. Convenience is exactly what fair selection forbids.

4 Vulnerability and Fair Selection

4.1 Who Counts as Vulnerable?

  • Some groups face special pressure, dependence, or limited freedom in research settings
  • In U.S. regulation, children, prisoners, and pregnant people receive additional protections under specific subparts of 45 CFR 46 (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office for Human Research Protections 2026)
  • Cognitive impairment, economic hardship, and dependent relationships can also create vulnerability (World Medical Association 2024)
  • Vulnerability is not a personality type
  • It is usually about power, context, and what saying no would cost
  • Justice cuts both ways: wrongful exclusion from research is also a failure

4.2 Vulnerable Populations and Typical Protections

Group Main worry Typical protection
Children Limited legal and developmental capacity Parent/guardian permission plus child assent when possible
Prisoners Constrained freedom and risk of coercion Narrower allowable research categories and extra scrutiny
Pregnant people / fetuses Risk to more than one party and historical exclusion Additional risk justification and review
People with cognitive impairment Difficulty understanding or sustaining choice Capacity assessment, surrogate consent, respect for assent or dissent
Economically disadvantaged participants Undue inducement and convenience recruitment Careful payment review and fair selection

4.3 Who Gets Studied Is Now Law

  • For decades, trial participants were disproportionately white, male, and young — even for diseases that hit other groups hardest
  • Drugs then reach the market with weaker evidence for the people who will actually take them
  • The US FDORA law (2022) now requires sponsors of many trials to file a Diversity Action Plan: enrollment goals by age, sex, race, and ethnicity, and a plan to meet them
  • The bet: fair selection means not only “don’t exploit the vulnerable” but “don’t leave them out”
  • Open question: do enrollment targets change recruitment, or just paperwork?

4.4 Thought Question

You are on an IRB reviewing a vaccine study.

  • The disease burden is high in prisons.
  • Researchers want to recruit incarcerated people because follow-up will be easy.
  • They plan a modest payment and better clinic access for participants.
  1. Is this fair inclusion, opportunistic targeting, or both?
  2. What extra protections would you demand before approving it?
  3. Would excluding prisoners entirely also create a justice problem?

4.5 Make It Contemporary

A company launches a drug trial but excludes pregnant people, many disabled adults, and people with low literacy because follow-up will be “messier.”

  • The drug will still be marketed broadly if approved.

Is this caution, convenience, or injustice?

4.6 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three points to keep in play:

  • Vulnerability is about power, not just fragility. Ask who depends on whom, who can walk away, and who cannot.
  • Extra protections should not become automatic exclusion. Protecting people is not the same as making them invisible.
  • Convenience is the enemy. Easy to recruit is never enough reason by itself.

5 Research Without a Trial

5.1 Henrietta Lacks

  • In 1951, Henrietta Lacks, a Black woman, was treated for cervical cancer
  • Without her knowledge, cells from her tumor were taken and grown
  • They became HeLa — the first immortal human cell line, used in countless studies and products
  • She was never asked; her family was not told for decades and shared in none of the benefit
  • No drug, no placebo, no “arm” — and still a major research-ethics failure

5.2 Make It Contemporary

You give a blood sample for a routine test. A leftover vial is stored.

  • Years later it is used — deidentified — in genetic research and a profitable diagnostic.
  • You were never specifically asked. A broad form at intake said “samples may be used for research.”
  1. Was that consent meaningful?
  2. Do you have a claim to a say — or a share — in what was discovered?
  3. Is “deidentified” enough, when the sample is DNA?

5.3 After You’ve Discussed

Note
  • Consent for this study is not consent for all future research. Tissue and data banks rely on broad consent — useful, but ethically thinner than specific consent.
  • Benefit-sharing is part of justice. If people make a discovery possible, who profits from it is an ethical question, not an afterthought.
  • “Deidentified” weakens but does not erase the stake — genomic data can re-identify, and can implicate relatives who never agreed.

5.4 What the Rules Now Require

  • US human-subjects research follows the Common Rule, rewritten in 2018 (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office for Human Research Protections 2026)
  • Consent forms must now open with a short “key information” summary in plain language
  • Broad consent: a person can agree once to future, unspecified research on their stored samples and data
  • Most multi-site studies now use a single IRB instead of one per site
  • Broad consent is more honest than silence — but thinner than asking each time

6 Placebo, Poverty, and Global Research

6.1 Helsinki’s Placebo Rule

  • The modern Declaration of Helsinki says new interventions should usually be tested against the best proven intervention (World Medical Association 2024)
  • Placebo is acceptable if no proven intervention exists
  • It may also be acceptable for compelling methodological reasons
  • But not if participants face additional risk of serious or irreversible harm by going without the proven treatment (World Medical Association 2024)
  • That last condition is the one people fight about

6.2 Why This Rule Is Hard

  • Sometimes the best global treatment is unavailable in the place where the study is being done
  • A shorter, cheaper, or simpler regimen might still save many lives
  • Researchers then ask whether the ethically relevant comparison is the best global care or the best locally available care
  • That is not a purely technical question
  • It is a question about whether poverty changes what researchers may permissibly offer

6.3 The AZT Trials in Five Lines

  • In 1994, a long-course zidovudine regimen reduced perinatal HIV transmission
  • That regimen was expensive and logistically difficult in many low-resource settings
  • Later trials tested shorter, cheaper regimens, often against placebo
  • Critics argued this withheld a known effective intervention (Lurie and Wolfe 1997)
  • Defenders argued the real question was what feasible strategy could work where the long regimen was not realistically available (Varmus and Satcher 1997)

6.4 Two Famous Positions

Lurie and Wolfe (Lurie and Wolfe 1997)

  • A proven regimen already existed
  • Placebo meant withholding known benefit
  • Poverty should not lower the ethical standard
  • Participants in poorer countries should not get weaker protections

Varmus and Satcher (Varmus and Satcher 1997)

  • The long regimen was not realistically implementable in many settings
  • A shorter regimen might still save lives at scale
  • The ethically relevant comparison had to include feasibility
  • Rejecting the trials might preserve principle while blocking practical benefit

6.5 What Is Owed After the Study?

  • A trial can be clean during the study and still abandon people after it
  • Post-trial access: do participants keep the intervention that worked for them?
  • Ancillary care: what about conditions the study uncovers but did not set out to treat?
  • For the AZT debate this is decisive — proving a regimen works, then withdrawing it, uses the host community
  • “What happens to them afterward?” is part of the design, not cleanup

6.6 A Placebo Decision Tree

6.7 Thought Question

You are on an ethics committee in 1997 reviewing a short-course AZT trial in Uganda.

  • In the sponsor country, the long-course regimen is already known to work.
  • In the host country, that regimen is too expensive and too complex for ordinary use.
  • The proposed study compares a shorter regimen to placebo.
  1. What is the right comparison group?
  2. Does local unavailability change what researchers ethically owe participants?
  3. If the shorter regimen could save many more babies in practice, does that justify the placebo arm?

6.8 Make It Contemporary

The same shape keeps returning:

  • A sponsor wants to test a simpler treatment in a low-resource setting.
  • The best global therapy exists, but the local system cannot reliably provide it.
  • The study might produce a realistic improvement, but only by starting from a lower baseline.

Is that ethically responsive to local reality, or is it using local injustice as part of the study design?

6.9 After You’ve Discussed

Note

Three hard truths belong together here:

  • “Local standard” describes the world; it does not settle what is ethically owed.
  • Feasibility matters, but poverty should not become a discount on human protections. A study can be practical and still exploitative.
  • Post-trial access is part of the ethics, not cleanup afterward. If participants help create useful knowledge, researchers must ask who will actually benefit from it (Emanuel et al. 2004; World Medical Association 2024).

7 When the Knowledge Is the Problem

7.1 Fabrication, Falsification, Plagiarism

  • Protecting participants is not the only duty — the knowledge itself must be trustworthy
  • The US Office of Research Integrity names three core wrongs:
    • Fabrication — making up data
    • Falsification — manipulating data, images, or results
    • Plagiarism — passing off others’ work or ideas as your own
  • A faked study can change treatment for millions before anyone notices

7.2 The Reproducibility Problem

  • Many published findings fail when other scientists try to repeat them
  • Drivers: pressure to publish, selective reporting, weak methods, and how rarely studies are replicated
  • Recent high-profile cases include alleged image manipulation in an influential 2006 Alzheimer’s paper and a university president’s 2023 resignation over lab-paper integrity
  • Responses being tried: data sharing, preregistration, replication studies, faster retractions
  • For a clinician: “published” and “peer-reviewed” are a floor, not a guarantee

7.3 Thought Question

A landmark drug trial is later found to contain manipulated data. Thousands of patients were treated based on it.

  1. Who was wronged — the study’s participants, future patients, or both?
  2. Is faking data a worse research-ethics violation than a consent failure, or just a different one?
  3. What would have caught this sooner, and why usually doesn’t it?

7.4 After You’ve Discussed

Note
  • Integrity is a subject-protection issue too. Bad knowledge harms the patients later treated by it, not only the people in the study.
  • The safeguards are thin by design. Science runs on trust and infrequent replication; misconduct exploits exactly that.
  • Self-correction is real but slow. Retractions and failed replications are how the system fixes itself — often years later.

8 Wrapping Up Part B

8.1 The Point of Protection

  • Research needs volunteers
  • Volunteers are not raw material
  • That is why consent, independent review, fair selection, and post-trial duties belong together
  • The rulebook exists to keep learning answerable to persons
  • When it works, research becomes both scientifically stronger and morally clearer

8.2 Wrapping Up Part B

Research-ethics rules are only as good as their use: Belmont as live IRB questions, real protections for vulnerable groups, hard limits on placebo when effective treatment exists, the AZT debate over justice across borders, the tissue-and-data problems Henrietta Lacks still poses, and today’s evolving rules — the rewritten Common Rule, diversity requirements, and the fight against research misconduct. The central lesson is demanding: ethical research is not research with a signature, but research designed — during and after — so that the people who make knowledge possible are treated as persons first.
