Part B: Euthanasia, Assisted Death, and Their Critics

An Introduction to Bioethics.

Part B — Helping to Die

Where We Left Off

In Part A every hard case was about whether to let dying happen — to stop or withhold treatment for a patient who could no longer decide. We ended on a puzzle: the law treats withholding and withdrawing treatment as the same, yet many caregivers feel that stopping a machine causes the death in a way that never starting it does not.

Part B walks straight into that feeling. We now ask about a sharper act: not letting a patient die, but actively helping them die — on their own clear request. This is where medicine, law, and philosophy disagree most deeply.

A Case to Sit With

In early 2014, Brittany Maynard, 29, was diagnosed with an aggressive brain cancer (glioblastoma) and given about six months to live. Her doctors described the death she could expect: seizures, escalating pain, loss of speech and self.

She and her husband moved from California to Oregon, one of the few states where a terminally ill, mentally competent adult can ask a doctor for a prescription to end their own life. She set a date, spoke publicly about her choice, and on November 1, 2014, took the medication and died at home. (Maynard 2014)

Write or discuss:

  1. Is what Brittany did different, morally, from a patient who refuses a ventilator and dies?
  2. Her doctor wrote the prescription; she took it herself. Does who performs the final act matter?
  3. Should this option be legal everywhere — or are there people it would put at risk?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

Notice how Brittany’s case differs from Nancy Cruzan’s in Part A.

  • Cruzan could not speak for herself; others decided whether to let her die.
  • Maynard was fully competent and decided for herself; she asked for help to actively end her life.

Part A was about who decides for the silent. Part B is about what a person may ask for — and what others may do — when the patient is the one asking. Almost everything today turns on one disputed line: the line between killing and letting die.

Today

By the end of Part B, you will be able to:

  • Sort the key terms — active/passive euthanasia, voluntary/non-voluntary/involuntary, and physician-assisted death — and say why the distinctions matter.
  • Reconstruct Rachels’ argument that killing and letting die carry no moral weight in themselves, and Foot’s reply.
  • Explain the positive case for assisted death from Brock (autonomy) and Singer (quality of life).
  • Explain Velleman’s secular argument against a right to die.
  • State the disability-rights critique and how Harriet McBryde Johnson pressed it against Singer.
  • Apply the doctrine of double effect to palliative sedation, and describe where assisted death is legal and what the data show.

First, Get the Words Right

Why Vocabulary Comes First

This is the one topic where people argue past each other because they are using the same words for different acts. Before any ethics, we need a clean map.

Three distinctions do all the work:

  • Active vs. passive — doing something to cause death vs. withholding something.
  • Voluntary vs. non-voluntary vs. involuntary — who wanted it.
  • Assisted death vs. euthanasia — who performs the final act.

Get these straight and most of the confusion disappears.

The Concept Map

Active vs. Passive Euthanasia

Euthanasia means bringing about a death for the sake of the person who dies — a “good death.” It splits in two:

  • Active euthanasia — someone administers something that causes death, such as a lethal injection.
  • Passive euthanasia — death is allowed to happen by withholding or withdrawing treatment, such as stopping a ventilator.

Almost every legal system treats these very differently: passive euthanasia is widely accepted, while active euthanasia is illegal in most of the world. The whole philosophical fight is about whether that difference is justified.

“Withdrawal Is Not Killing” — The Standard View

Most clinicians and courts insist that stopping treatment is not the same as killing.

  • When a ventilator is withdrawn and the patient dies, the standard view says the disease kills her; the doctor merely steps out of the way.
  • When a doctor injects a lethal drug, the doctor kills her.

On this view, a patient has a strong right to refuse treatment (so passive euthanasia is permissible), but no one has a right to be killed (so active euthanasia is not). Hold onto this claim — it is exactly what Rachels will attack.

Assisted Death vs. Euthanasia

Physician-assisted death (PAD / MAID)

The doctor prescribes a lethal medication, but the patient takes it themselves. This is what Brittany Maynard did. The final act is the patient’s.

(Active, voluntary) euthanasia

The doctor administers the medication directly — for example, a lethal injection at the patient’s request. The final act is the doctor’s.

This line is legally decisive. US states that permit assisted death allow only the prescribing model; Canada and several European countries allow the doctor to administer as well. “MAID” (Medical Assistance in Dying) is the Canadian term and covers both.

Does the Distinction Hold? Rachels vs. Foot

The View Rachels Attacks

In 1975 the American Medical Association’s policy captured the standard view exactly: a doctor may stop “extraordinary means” of prolonging life (passive), but the “intentional termination of the life of one human being by another — mercy killing” (active) is forbidden. (Rachels 1975)

The assumption: passive euthanasia is sometimes permissible, active euthanasia never is — because killing is in itself worse than letting die.

James Rachels set out to show that this assumption is confused.

Rachels’ Challenge (1975)

Rachels argues that if we have already decided a patient should die — say, a newborn with a condition so severe that withholding treatment is judged acceptable — then passive euthanasia is often the crueler option.

  • Withholding treatment can mean a slow death over days, by dehydration or infection.
  • A quick lethal injection would cause less suffering.

So if anything, the act we permit (passive) can be worse for the patient than the act we forbid (active). Something has gone wrong with the distinction.

Smith and Jones

Rachels’ thought experiment

  • Smith will inherit a fortune if his young cousin dies. He sneaks into the bathroom and drowns the boy in the bath, making it look like an accident.
  • Jones stands to inherit the same fortune. He sneaks in planning to drown the boy — but the child slips, hits his head, and slides face-down into the water. Jones watches, ready to push him under if needed, and lets him drown.

Smith killed; Jones merely let die. Is Jones any less monstrous? Almost no one thinks so. (Rachels 1975)

The Bare Difference Argument

The Smith and Jones case is built so that everything is the same except killing vs. letting die — same motive, same outcome, same intention. Rachels calls what is left the bare difference.

  • If killing were worse than letting die in itself, Jones would be at least a little less guilty than Smith.
  • He is not. So the bare difference between killing and letting die carries no moral weight on its own.

If that is right, then “but that would be killing” cannot, by itself, settle the euthanasia debate.

What Rachels Does and Doesn’t Show

Important

Be precise about the conclusion.

  • Rachels does not show that active euthanasia is right, or that killing is usually fine.
  • He shows only that the killing/letting-die distinction itself does not carry the moral weight people lean on it for.

Other things — consent, motive, consequences, risk of abuse — may still make killing usually worse. Rachels just denies that the bare act-type does the work. Whether anything else can rescue the distinction is Foot’s question.

Foot’s Reply: Duties, Not Acts

Philippa Foot agrees the bare physical difference is thin — but argues the distinction survives once we ground it in duties and rights. (Foot 1977)

  • A negative duty not to harm people (not to kill) is generally more stringent than a positive duty to aid people (to save).
  • I may not murder a stranger to use his organs, even to save five others — my duty not to kill him outweighs my duty to save them.

So “killing vs. letting die” is a stand-in for “violating a strong negative duty vs. a weaker positive one.” The distinction was tracking something real after all.

Why Foot’s Move Matters

On Foot’s view, the reason Jones is as bad as Smith is not that letting die equals killing in general. It is that Jones had no right to let the boy drown — he had a duty to save him and a murderous intent besides.

Change the case to a doctor who may permissibly stop a treatment the patient refused, and the negative/positive distinction does real work: the doctor violates no duty by stopping, but would violate one by injecting.

The upshot

Rachels and Foot agree the bare act-type is morally inert. They split on whether the duties behind the act-type — strong duties not to harm, weaker duties to aid — revive a defensible line between euthanasia’s forms.

Scoreboard: Rachels vs. Foot

What does the real work? Consequences, motive, consent Negative vs. positive duties
Implication for euthanasia Removes a key objection Leaves a principled brake

The debate does not end here — it relocates. If the line between active and passive is not bedrock, the next question is whether the values underneath it actually support assisted death.

If the Line Blurs, Should We Permit It?

Brock’s Positive Case

Dan Brock turns the discussion around. Instead of asking whether killing is special, he asks: what values justify letting patients refuse treatment in the first place? (Brock 1992)

He finds two:

  • Self-determination — competent people have the right to shape their own lives, including their dying.
  • Well-being — we let people judge for themselves when continued life is a benefit or a burden.

These are the same two values, Brock argues, that support voluntary active euthanasia.

Brock: The Parity Argument

If self-determination and well-being are weighty enough to let a patient refuse a ventilator and die, Brock asks, why are they not weighty enough to let a patient request a lethal drug and die?

  • In both, a competent person judges that continued life is worse than death.
  • In both, a doctor helps carry out that judgment.

Brock’s challenge to the standard view: you cannot consistently celebrate the right to refuse treatment and flatly forbid voluntary euthanasia, because the same moral foundation underlies both. He takes the worries seriously, but locates them in consequences and safeguards, not in the act itself.

Singer: Rejecting Sanctity of Life

Peter Singer pushes further, attacking the premise he thinks blocks clear thinking: the sanctity-of-life doctrine — the idea that human life is always equally precious simply because it is human. (Singer 1994)

  • Singer replaces it with a quality-of-life ethic: what matters morally is the character of a life for the one living it.
  • For a preference utilitarian like Singer, killing a self-aware being is wrong mainly because it thwarts that being’s preferences — so when a person prefers death, voluntary euthanasia can respect rather than violate them.

Singer’s Edge — and the Backlash It Invites

Important

Singer’s view has a sharp edge that will matter for the rest of the lecture.

If what makes killing wrong is thwarting a being’s preferences about its own future, then beings without such preferences — Singer’s examples include some severely impaired newborns — do not have the same protection. Singer has defended non-voluntary euthanasia in such cases. (Singer 2011)

This is exactly the reasoning that the disability-rights movement regards as dangerous — and it is why a quality-of-life ethic, meant to expand compassion, draws its fiercest critics from disabled people themselves.

Thought Question

A patient with ALS is on a ventilator and fully competent. She asks her physician to turn it off, knowing she will die within minutes. The physician does, giving sedation for comfort. The patient dies. Everyone agrees this was lawful and ethical.

The next patient, with the same disease but not yet ventilator-dependent, asks the physician for a lethal medication to take at home.

  1. Is the physician’s role really different in the two cases?
  2. If the first is permitted, on what grounds would you forbid the second?
  3. Does it change your answer that, in the first case, the patient could not have lived long without the machine?

Make It Contemporary

This is not hypothetical. After Brittany Maynard’s death, California passed the End of Life Option Act (2015), joining Oregon (1997), Washington, Colorado, and others. By the mid-2020s, assisted death was legal in eleven US jurisdictions, covering roughly a fifth of Americans.

Every US law allows only the prescribing model, requires a terminal diagnosis (usually six months), and demands the patient be competent and act voluntarily.

Discuss: Are those three safeguards — terminal, competent, voluntary — the right lines? Which would you tighten or loosen?

After You’ve Discussed

Note

A distinction the safeguards quietly assume:

  • In assisted death, the patient performs the final act. This builds in a last check — the person must still, at the end, choose to act.
  • In euthanasia, someone else performs it. This is available to patients too disabled to act for themselves, but it removes that final self-check.

US law has chosen the first model precisely because the patient’s last act is itself a safeguard. Canada and the Netherlands accept the second — and inherit a harder problem of verifying consent at the very end.

The Brakes: Velleman and the Critics

Velleman: Against the Right to Die

J. David Velleman offers the most interesting secular objection — one that grants almost everything to the other side. (Velleman 1992)

  • He concedes that death can genuinely benefit some patients, and that they may be entitled to it.
  • He still argues against establishing a right or institution of assisted death.

His worry is not about the act. It is about what offering the option to everyone does to everyone.

Velleman: The Cost of Having a Choice

Why an option can harm you

Right now, a gravely ill person who keeps living needs no justification — there was simply no choice. But once death becomes an accepted option, the situation changes for everyone, including those who never wanted it.

Now a patient must, in effect, justify continuing to live — to family, to caregivers, even to herself. The frail and dependent may feel they ought to “stop being a burden.” Merely making the choice available can strip away the protection of having no choice at all. (Velleman 1992)

Velleman: A Careful Conclusion

Important

Velleman’s argument is easy to misread, so be exact.

  • He is not claiming euthanasia is intrinsically wrong, or that no one should ever have it.
  • He argues that a general right or public policy is dangerous, because the option’s mere existence reshapes the pressures on the vulnerable.

This is why his view cuts across the usual lines: a secular philosopher, granting that death can be a benefit, still resisting a right to it. It also sets up the critics who say the vulnerable are not hypothetical.

The Disability-Rights Critique

For disability-rights groups such as Not Dead Yet, Velleman’s abstract worry is daily reality. Their objection is not religious; it is about who is actually at risk.

  • “Better dead than disabled.” Assisted-death laws rest on the judgment that some lives are not worth living — and the lives singled out are usually disabled ones.
  • People often request death not because of pain but because of dependence, lost dignity, and being a burden — conditions that better support could change.
  • The same society that offers suicide prevention to the able-bodied may offer suicide assistance to the disabled. That asymmetry is the heart of the critique.

McBryde Johnson Meets Singer

Harriet McBryde Johnson was a lawyer and disability-rights activist who had a progressive neuromuscular condition and used a wheelchair. In 2002 she debated Peter Singer at Princeton and wrote about it in “Unspeakable Conversations.” (Johnson 2003)

Her core reply to Singer: his confidence that disability makes a life worse is an outsider’s assumption. Disabled people consistently rate their own quality of life far higher than the non-disabled imagine. The premise of the quality-of-life argument is, she said, simply mistaken.

The unsettling part

Johnson found Singer personable and his logic internally consistent — which is what frightened her. A coherent, well-meaning argument can still license treating some lives as expendable. The danger is not cartoon villainy; it is reasonable-sounding premises.

Coercion, Duty, and Access

The critics converge on three concrete risks that safeguards must answer.

  • Coercion — subtle pressure from family, cost, or exhausted caregivers, not held at gunpoint but nudged.
  • The “duty to die” — once dying is an option, the dependent may feel obligated to take it (Velleman’s worry, lived).
  • Access disparities — those with the least social support may find death the most available form of “care,” while the well-resourced get treatment.

These are empirical questions. Which is why both sides now argue about data — and we turn to it after one more conceptual tool.

Double Effect and Palliative Sedation

The Doctrine of Double Effect

The doctrine of double effect tries to explain how an act with a foreseen bad effect can still be permissible — if the bad effect is not intended. (McIntyre 2023)

A classic four-part test: the act is permissible only if

  1. the act itself is good or neutral (giving pain medication);
  2. the bad effect is not the means to the good (relief doesn’t work by killing);
  3. the bad effect is foreseen but not intended; and
  4. there is a proportionate reason (the suffering is grave enough).

Double Effect in Practice

The standard medical example: a dying patient in severe pain is given escalating morphine. The dose needed for comfort may also depress breathing and hasten death.

  • Under double effect, this is permissible: the doctor intends relief and merely foresees the risk to breathing.
  • The same physical act — giving the drug — would be wrong if the doctor intended the death and used pain relief as a cover.

Double effect makes intention do the moral work. Critics (including Rachels) ask whether a distinction that turns on a doctor’s private intention can really bear that much weight.

Palliative Sedation

Palliative sedation is the practice of sedating a dying patient — sometimes to unconsciousness — to relieve symptoms that nothing else can touch (intractable pain, agitation, breathlessness).

  • It is legal and widely accepted, and is usually justified by double effect: the aim is relief of suffering, not death.
  • In its deepest form — continuous sedation to unconsciousness, often while withholding artificial nutrition and hydration — the patient never wakes and dies within days.

Is Palliative Sedation “Slow Euthanasia”?

The defense

The intention is to relieve refractory suffering, not to kill. Studies suggest properly titrated sedation does not clearly shorten life. By double effect, it stays on the right side of the line.

The worry

If you sedate someone to permanent unconsciousness and stop feeding them, the outcome is certain death. Critics call this “slow euthanasia” — euthanasia that keeps the form of double effect while abandoning its substance.

This is the same fault line as Rachels vs. Foot, in a new place: does the intention behind an act change what the act is?

Oregon After 25+ Years

Oregon’s law is the most studied in the world, because the state publishes annual data. The findings are consistent year after year. (Oregon Health Authority, Public Health Division 2024)

  • The top reasons patients give are loss of autonomy, being less able to enjoy activities, and loss of dignity — each cited by most patients.
  • Inadequate pain control (or fear of it) is cited by a minority — roughly a quarter.
  • About a third of patients who get the prescription never take it; having the option is itself a comfort.

The data complicate both sides: little evidence of the predicted “carnage,” but confirmation that the drivers are existential, not just physical pain.

Canada: The Fastest Expansion

Canada moved further and faster than anywhere else.

  • 2015: In Carter v. Canada, the Supreme Court struck down the ban on assisted death. (Supreme Court of Canada 2015)
  • 2016: Parliament legalized MAID for adults whose natural death was “reasonably foreseeable.”
  • 2021: A new law removed the terminal requirement, allowing MAID for serious, incurable conditions that are not life-threatening.

By 2023, MAID accounted for about 1 in 20 deaths in Canada — among the highest rates in the world, and the speed of expansion is itself now part of the debate.

The Psychiatric MAID Controversy

The hardest frontier is assisted death for psychiatric suffering — where the illness is mental, not terminal.

  • The Netherlands, Belgium, and Spain permit assisted death for non-terminal suffering, including conditions that are partly or wholly psychiatric.
  • The dispute is sharp because two of the law’s safeguards strain here: Is the suffering truly irremediable? Can a condition that distorts judgment leave the patient fully competent to consent?

A Case at the Frontier: Noelia Castillo

In 2022, Noelia Castillo, a young Spanish woman with OCD and borderline personality disorder, attempted suicide and was left paraplegic and in constant pain. In 2024 she asked to die under Spain’s euthanasia law.

Her father fought her in court for 601 days, arguing her mental illness made her wish to die a symptom, not a choice. Every court — up to the European Court of Human Rights — found she had capacity. She died in March 2026, age 25.

Important

Why it’s the hard case

Her death was approved for physical pain, but fought over psychiatric capacity. She was not dying — she was disabled. So the case fuses both critiques: Is this autonomy honored, or a vulnerable woman society failed first and offered death second?

Castillo: Two Readings of the Same Facts

Autonomy honored

Refusing a capable woman’s request only because her suffering is psychiatric applies a double standard we never use for physical illness. Every court agreed she could choose.

Vulnerability abandoned

Safeguards checked whether she qualified, not why she got there: lost home, institutional care, untreated trauma, assaults never investigated. A pianist offered to fund her care. Was death simply more available than a livable life?

The paired case study works this through in full. It is the live form of every argument in this lecture.

Canada Hits the Brakes

A pause worth noticing

Canada planned to extend MAID to people whose sole underlying condition is a mental illness. The start date was set for March 2023 — then delayed to 2024 — then delayed again to March 2027.

The reasons cited were exactly the critics’ worries: clinicians were not confident they could reliably tell an irremediable mental illness from a treatable one, or a settled wish from a symptom. Even a system committed to expansion found a line it was not ready to cross.

After You’ve Discussed: Two Kinds of Slope

Note

“Slippery slope” is often dismissed as a fallacy, but separate two versions:

  • A logical slope says permitting A commits you to B. This is usually weak — you can permit voluntary, terminal cases and still forbid the rest, as Oregon does.
  • An empirical slope says that in practice, categories drift: eligibility widens, safeguards loosen, numbers climb. Canada’s trajectory is evidence this can happen — not as logic, but as politics and habit.

The honest question is not “is there a slope?” but “which slope, and is this society good at stopping on it?”

Wrap-Up

We began with one disputed line — killing vs. letting die — and watched Rachels argue it carries no weight in itself while Foot rebuilt it from duties. Brock and Singer pressed the case for assisted death from autonomy and quality of life; Velleman and the disability-rights critics, led here by Harriet McBryde Johnson, pressed back — not from religion, but from concern for the vulnerable and for what a mere option does to them.

The doctrine of double effect let us see palliative sedation as the same fight in new clothing: does intention change what an act is? And the law on the ground — Oregon’s steady data, Canada’s rapid expansion and its sudden pause on psychiatric cases — shows a society still arguing, in real time, about where the brakes belong. The line between mercy and danger is exactly what no one has finished drawing.

Key Terms

  • Active euthanasia — administering something that causes death.
  • Passive euthanasia — allowing death by withholding or withdrawing treatment.
  • Voluntary / non-voluntary / involuntary — with the patient’s request / without their input / against their wish.
  • Physician-assisted death (PAD/MAID) — the doctor prescribes; the patient performs the final act.
  • Bare difference argument (Rachels) — killing vs. letting die carries no moral weight in itself.
  • Negative vs. positive duties (Foot) — the duty not to harm is more stringent than the duty to aid.
  • Doctrine of double effect — a foreseen bad effect can be permissible if unintended and proportionate.
  • Palliative sedation — sedating a dying patient to relieve refractory suffering.
