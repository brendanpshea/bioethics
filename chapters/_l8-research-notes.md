## L8a — Theories of Justice & the Right to Health Care

### Seattle "God Committee" (cold-open anchor)
- 1962, **Admissions and Policy Committee** of the Seattle Artificial Kidney Center at **Swedish Hospital** — the country's only dialysis center when it opened January 1962.
- Grew out of Dr. **Belding Scribner**'s (Univ. of Washington) shunt, which turned dialysis from a ~6-week stopgap into a chronic, long-term therapy.
- Scarcity: **3 treatment slots, ~60 patients** who needed them in the area.
- Committee = **7 anonymous, unpaid laypeople**: a lawyer, a minister, a housewife, a state official, a banker, a labor leader, a surgeon ("doctor-citizen").
- Selected by perceived **"social worth"** — employment, church attendance, marital status, "future potential."
- Exposed publicly by **Shana Alexander, "They Decide Who Lives, Who Dies," *LIFE* magazine, Nov. 9, 1962** (she observed for 6 months). **[alexander1962]**
- Pedagogical point: this is allocation *without* a theory of justice → motivates the four theories. Widely credited as a founding episode of modern bioethics; later helped drive the 1972 federal **ESRD Medicare entitlement** (Congress made dialysis universally funded rather than keep rationing it).

### The four theories (conceptual — classics, low time-sensitivity)
- **Utilitarian** — maximize aggregate well-being / life-years. Real-world expression: **QALYs**, cost-effectiveness, UNOS survival-benefit scoring, UK **NICE**.
- **Rawlsian** — principles chosen behind the **veil of ignorance**; protect the worst-off (difference principle). Rawls, *A Theory of Justice* (1971). **[rawls1971]**
- **Libertarian (Nozick)** — entitlement theory; no one is *owed* health care; redistribution as coerced. Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974). The organ "is the donor's to give" → directed donation, markets. **[nozick1974]**
- **Capabilities (Sen, Nussbaum)** — justice = securing the real freedoms/functionings people need for a human life; health is a central capability. Sen, *Development as Freedom* (1999) **[sen1999]**; Nussbaum, *Creating Capabilities* (2011) **[nussbaum2011]**.

### Right to health care
- **Norman Daniels** — extends Rawls's *fair equality of opportunity* to health: disease/disability narrow the "normal opportunity range," so a just society owes care that restores normal functioning. *Just Health Care* (1985); *Just Health* (2008). **[daniels2008]**
- Positive vs. negative rights framing; US treats food/housing/education as partial rights but health care as **mostly a market good** (the outlier among rich democracies).

### Organ allocation — the worked application (figures move; verified May 2026)
- **>105,000** on the US national transplant waiting list (mid-2025); **>92,000 awaiting kidneys** (~87% of all candidates). Managed by **OPTN/UNOS**. **[optn_data]**
- ~**27,500+** kidney transplants performed in 2025 (slightly fewer than 2024).
- Allocation principles in tension: **"sickest first" vs. "best outcome" vs. "first-come."**
- **Opt-in vs. opt-out (presumed consent):**
  - **Spain** = world leader, **52.6 donors per million population (2024)**; soft opt-out + the **ONT** national coordination infrastructure (the real driver — family consent ~8/10 vs. UK ~6/10). **[spanish_model]**
  - **US ≈ 48.1 pmp (2024)** — high by global standards but below Spain; US uses opt-in registries.
  - Lesson: opt-out *law alone* doesn't explain Spain — infrastructure + clinical leadership do.
- **Paid markets — Iran** is the *only* country with a legal, government-regulated kidney market (since **1988**); it **eliminated its renal waiting list by ~1999**. Official payment ~USD 1,400 + negotiated recipient payments. But **~43% of donors cite purely financial motives** and **~75% say selling should be banned** → the exploitation/coercion critique. Ghods & Savaj, CJASN (2006). **[ghods2006]**
- Ability-to-pay distortion: multi-listing across regions (the Steve Jobs liver example) — wealth buys better odds within an ostensibly need-based system.

## L8b — Why US Health Care Costs So Much, Delivers So Little

### The driving paradox (cold open) — verified May 2026
- **National health spending 2023: $4.9 trillion, $14,570 per capita, 17.6% of GDP** (CMS NHE; grew 7.5% in 2023). **[cms_nhe2023]**
  - ⚠️ Update from earlier estimate: per-capita is **~$14,570 (2023)**, not ~$13k.
- Roughly **double** the OECD average per capita; the visible outlier on spending-vs-outcomes.
- **Life expectancy: 78.4 yrs (2023)** vs. OECD average **81.1** — a **2.7-yr gap** (was just 0.2 yr in 2003). **[oecd_hag2025]**
  - Rebound trajectory: **76.4 (2021) → 77.5 (2022) → 78.4 (2023)** as COVID, heart disease, and overdose deaths fell. (CDC/NCHS)
- **Infant mortality: 5.61/1,000 (2023, CDC/NCHS)**, 5.60 (2022) — *confirmed against CDC primary; the earlier web "3.2" was a subgroup figure, do not use.* OECD avg ~4. Black infant mortality ~**10.9** vs. Asian ~3.5 (≈3× disparity). Rate rose in 2022 (first increase since 2001–02). **[cdc_infant2023: NVSR 74(7), 2025]**
- **Maternal mortality: 17/100,000 (2023, US)** vs. OECD avg **10.3** — highest among high-income countries, rising since 1990. **[oecd_hag2025]**

### The fragmented patchwork (coverage shares, 2023 — Census P60-284 / KFF) **[census_coverage2023]**
- **Employer-sponsored insurance ≈ 53.7%** of the population (the core).
- **Medicare ≈ 18.9%**; **Medicaid ≈ 18.9%**.
- **Direct-purchase / individual (incl. ACA exchanges) ≈ 10.2%.**
- **Uninsured ≈ 7.9–8.0% (~26 million)**; insured share **92.5%** (near record high).
- **VA / military** = separate, genuinely government-*run* (socialized) delivery.
- Note shares overlap/sum >100% because people hold multiple coverage types in a year.

### The employer-insurance "accident" & the tax exclusion
- WWII **wage freezes (War Labor Board, 1942–43)** → employers competed with health benefits instead of wages; **1943 IRS ruling + 1954 Internal Revenue Code** made employer health contributions **tax-exempt**.
- That exclusion is now the **single largest federal tax expenditure: ~$299B (2022 income+payroll); JCT/CBO est. ~$451B for FY2026.** **[tpc_esi_exclusion]**
- 2024 avg ESI premiums: **$8,951 single / $25,572 family** (KFF Employer Survey).

### A little history since 1945 (dates are stable)
- **1945–48:** Truman proposes national health insurance → defeated (AMA "socialized medicine" campaign).
- **1965:** **Medicare & Medicaid** signed (LBJ) — the last major coverage expansion before ACA.
- **1972:** Medicare extended to ESRD (dialysis) and the disabled.
- **1973:** **HMO Act** — managed care.
- **1986:** **EMTALA** — hospitals must screen/stabilize emergencies regardless of ability to pay (an unfunded near-universal *emergency* right).
- **1993–94:** **Clinton** health plan fails.
- **2003:** Medicare **Part D** (drug benefit).
- **2010:** **ACA** — exchanges, Medicaid expansion, pre-existing-condition protections, individual mandate (penalty later zeroed 2019).
- *What didn't change:* still employer-centered, still not universal.

### Why so expensive? — "It's the prices, not the utilization"
- **Anderson, Hussey & Petrosyan, "It's Still the Prices, Stupid," *Health Affairs* (2019)** — updates the 2003 Anderson/Reinhardt paper. Americans **don't use more care**; each unit costs more (drug prices, clinician salaries, hospital prices). **[anderson2019]**
- **Administrative costs:** US health-system admin ≈ **8% of spending vs. 1–3%** in peer countries; **hospital admin 25.3% of costs** (highest of 8 nations; Scotland 11.6%, Canada 12.4%) (Himmelstein, *Health Affairs* 2014). Billing/insurance admin ≈ **$2,497/person US vs. $551 Canada**, ~**34%** of expenditures (Himmelstein et al., *Annals of Internal Medicine* 2020). **[himmelstein2020]**
- Other drivers: fee-for-service incentives, hospital/insurer consolidation, little price transparency/negotiation, drug prices.

### Why poorer outcomes? — not only the health system
- **NRC/IOM, *U.S. Health in International Perspective: Shorter Lives, Poorer Health* (2013)**: the US disadvantage spans the life course and **persists even for advantaged, insured, non-poor white Americans** → it's not only the uninsured or minorities. **[nrc2013]**
- Causes named: fragmented system + **unhealthy behaviors/diet/obesity**, **firearms**, **vehicle deaths**, **drug overdose** (~107,941 in 2022 → 105,007 in 2023), **income inequality/poverty**, weaker primary care, large uninsured population.
- Honest nuance for class: much of the outcome gap is **social/behavioral**, not deliverable by clinics — important so students don't over-blame "the system."

### What the US is genuinely best at
- **Quaternary/specialty care:** Mayo, Cleveland Clinic, MD Anderson — world-leading.
- **Cancer survival:** US has top/near-top 5-yr survival internationally — **#1 for prostate**, top tier for breast & colorectal (CONCORD program, *Lancet/Lancet Oncology*). **[concord]**
- Biomedical **innovation/R&D**; **no waits** for elective/specialty care for the well-insured.
- Caveat: within-US racial disparities remain large even where averages lead.

### Where we struggle (foreground for nursing audience)
- **Nursing/staff shortage:** HRSA (Nov 2024) projects a **~10% RN shortage in 2027** (13% non-metro vs. 5% metro), shortfall **>500,000** by 2030; **NCSBN: ~138,000 nurses left since 2022, ~40% intend to leave by 2029**; **2024 RN turnover ~16%**; **>80,000 qualified nursing-school applicants turned away in 2024** (faculty/capacity limits). **[hrsa_nursing2024]**
- Pricing opacity, access gaps, rural hospital closures, burnout, recent life-expectancy decline.

### Compare the world (four models of universal coverage)
| Model | Example | Who pays | Who delivers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-payer, gov't-*run* | **UK (NHS)** | Taxes | Government |
| Single public insurer, private delivery | **Canada** | Taxes | Private/mixed |
| Multi-payer "sickness funds," mandated | **Germany** | Payroll (funds) | Private/mixed |
| Regulated private mandate | **Switzerland** | Individuals (subsidized) | Private |
- Switzerland ≈ the closest cousin to the **ACA** model. Point: universal coverage has **many designs**, not one.
- Sets up the live debate: **Medicare-for-All / single-payer vs. public option vs. market reforms** — each picks a different 8a value.
